# ACRE Persona 34 — The Paid Human Middleware
### Larkspur Weddings — a 4-person wedding planning studio that *is* the AI-in-the-middle, for hire

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #26 (Family 7, consumer-as-hub); ◆ the coordinating hub serves a private individual, and the planner is paid to be exactly what the product does. Serve-or-threaten is the open question this persona exists to answer.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Professional Constellation-Runner
**Instance:** Larkspur Weddings, Cotswolds/London. Founder/lead planner, a second planner, an assistant, a part-time bookkeeper. 22–28 full-plan weddings a year at £6k–£15k planning fees, plus on-the-day coordination packages. The product being sold is, precisely, coordination.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Per wedding: a 12–20 supplier constellation (venue, caterer, florist, band, photographer, registrar, transport, hair, cake…) assembled around a couple making once-in-a-lifetime decisions with maximal emotion and zero experience. The planner is the constellation's entire nervous system, by appointment. |
| Tooling | A wedding-planning platform (timeline/budget), Pinterest boards, email, and WhatsApp in volumes that would frighten an MSP — couples at midnight, florists at dawn, the venue's events office at office hours only. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The planner — and that's the business model. The question isn't who carries it but how many weddings one planner can carry before quality (or the planner) breaks. Current ceiling: 10–12 full plans each. |
| Buying behaviour | Sole-trader instincts: tools bought personally, kept if they survive a wedding season. Anything client-facing must be beautiful — the brand is taste. |
| What failure costs | There is no reprint: the date is absolute, the event unrepeatable, the client emotionally all-in. A failure isn't a missed deadline; it's a ruined wedding, a sobbing phone call, and a review that ends the business. The stakes-to-fee ratio is the highest in the library. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A professional hub running a dense, emotionally supercharged supplier constellation toward a single immovable date, on behalf of civilian clients whose decision-making is once-in-a-lifetime — with family politics as a shadow constellation behind the paying one.

**Cast:**
- **Frankie Wells, 37** — founder & lead planner (Cirencester)
- **Mia Castellano, 27** — assistant planner; spreadsheet soul, on-the-day general (Cheltenham)
- **Zoe Hammond, 32** — the bride; project-manages software for a living, which cuts both ways
- **Greg Farnham, 45** — venue events manager at a manor house; gatekeeper of everything on site
- **Plus:** the other partner (lower-engagement, occasionally ambushed into opinions), the caterer, florist, band, photographer — and **Carol, mother of the bride**: unbudgeted, unappointed, omnipresent

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The date** — the absolute clock; every other constraint is negotiable against it and it is negotiable against nothing
- **The venue's rules** — corkage, curfews, candle policies, load-in windows: Greg's domain, contractual and final
- **The registrar/church** — legal formalities with their own diaries
- **The weather** — the only node with no phone number; plan B is a permanent parallel universe
- **The families** — funding flows from them, opinions with it; veto power without contract (Carol is an external immovable node with a guest list)

**Topology:** Hub-and-spoke at maximum spoke count, with a twist: the *client* is simultaneously the constellation's purpose, a decision bottleneck, and an emotional variable. Suppliers know each other across weddings (the regional wedding circuit is a dense repeat-game community); the couple passes through it once.

**Lifecycle (per wedding, 12–18 months):**
- *Onboarding & dreaming:* budget reality vs. Pinterest; the gentle education of the couple begins.
- *Booking season:* the big suppliers locked 12+ months out; every booking a deposit, a contract and a dependency.
- *The long middle:* decisions in waves (stationery, menus, flowers), couple-energy oscillating, Carol's opinions compounding.
- *Midpoint reset:* the budget reckoning — aggregate spend meets remaining wishes; something gives, diplomatically.
- *The final six weeks (the crunch):* timeline lockdown, supplier confirmations, seating-plan diplomacy (the most politically dense artefact in the library), the schedule-of-the-day distributed to twenty parties.
- *The day:* a 14-hour live operation run from Mia's clipboard and Frankie's earpiece; every coordination failure now visible to 120 guests in real time.
- *Dissolution:* sudden and total — albums, thank-yous, an invoice, and a constellation that never re-forms (though every supplier edge persists into the next wedding).
- *Trust curve:* the couple's trust is the product — built in the first three meetings, spent by any visible wobble, and tested hardest exactly when they're most stressed. Supplier trust is circuit-wide and career-long: Frankie's real asset is that the best caterer answers her texts first.

**Cadence:** Couple-paced in the middle (bursts of decisions, then exam-season silence), supplier-paced at the edges, and everything converging to hour-by-hour in the final week. WhatsApp is the substrate of all of it.

**Boundary objects:** The master timeline (the wedding's constitution), the budget tracker (where dreams meet arithmetic), the supplier contracts and payment schedule, the day schedule (twenty parties, one document), the seating plan (pure crystallised family politics), the Pinterest board (the dream's evidence locker).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** Entirely, professionally, on Frankie — that's the fee. The design question: every hour of articulation the product absorbs either (a) lets Frankie carry more weddings at higher quality, or (b) makes the couple wonder what they're paying her for. The persona's commercial knife-edge.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Frankie holds the supplier circuit's true map (who's brilliant, who's flaky in August, which band splits fees). Greg holds the venue's non-negotiables and its unwritten flexibilities. Zoe holds the vision and its drift. Carol holds opinions and (40% of) the budget. Mia holds the day-of operational reality. The couple's actual preferences — as opposed to stated ones — emerge across eighteen months of choices and live nowhere but Frankie's notes.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Decision latency at the couple: suppliers' deadlines queued behind Zoe's work crunch — **eliminate** the invisibility (deadlines and consequences made gently visible); **contain** the couple's right to be slow — they're the client
2. Supplier-detail drift: the florist's delivery time vs. the venue's load-in window, discovered on the morning — **eliminate**; the classic cross-spoke constraint failure at unrepeatable stakes
3. The Carol channel: instructions arriving from a non-client with budget power — **contain**: family politics can't be processed away; anchor decisions to the couple, log everything else as input
4. Budget drift surfacing as one terrible conversation — **eliminate** the surprise (Sorrel & Gray's pattern at higher emotional voltage)
5. The final-week information cascade: twenty parties needing the same updates at different resolutions — **eliminate**
6. Frankie's "that's a beautiful idea and it will not work in that marquee" — **preserve**: the planner's candour is what the fee buys; a product that helps couples route around it destroys the value chain it serves
7. Planner burnout at the ceiling — **contain** structurally; the product raises the ceiling, which *is* the serve-the-planner answer

**Conflicting interests:**
- The couple wants the dream; the budget wants arithmetic; Carol wants her sister's daughter's wedding outdone
- Each supplier optimises their craft moment; the timeline needs them interlocking
- Frankie's fee says "handle everything"; her margin says "at scale"; the couple's emotion says "be always available"
- The venue protects its fabric and licence against everyone's creativity

*Contain-class: the emotional and familial conflicts are the terrain, not the problem. The product anchors decisions to the paying couple and keeps every input attributed.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap a supplier pre-booking: routine. Swap the florist three weeks out: Frankie's circuit produces a replacement in 48 hours — *this* is what her network is worth, and no platform replicates it yet. Swap *Mia* before a wedding day: the day-of operational knowledge is documented (schedules) but the instincts aren't; risk rises visibly. Swap *Frankie* mid-plan (illness): the business's nightmare — eighteen months of preference-knowledge and supplier-diplomacy partially evaporates; the couple's trust transfers only if the record is complete enough to make the handover seamless. Swap *the couple* — weddings do get cancelled: the constellation dissolves against contracts and deposits, and the planner manages the saddest unwind in the library.

**Afterlife:** The couple's constellation never re-forms (one hopes); the supplier circuit is the real persistent structure — every wedding re-prices every edge on it, and Frankie's referral flow (venues recommend planners; planners recommend everyone) is the business's bloodstream. The album outlives all of it. The preference-record of *how this couple decided* dies — except as Frankie's accumulating craft, which is currently unexternalisable. Reviews are the public afterlife: one five-star paragraph funds a quarter's pipeline.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Serve the planner, unambiguously — the strategic answer to the family's ◆ question. Absorb the articulation Frankie shouldn't carry: supplier confirmations chased, cross-spoke constraints checked (every delivery window against every load-in), payment schedules tracked, the final-week cascade distributed at per-party resolution. Make the couple's decision queue gentle and visible — deadlines with consequences explained, never nagging. Log every input (Carol included) attributed and anchored to the couple's actual decisions. Hold plan B alongside plan A as first-class. Raise Frankie's ceiling from ten weddings to fifteen *without the couples ever feeling the multiplication* — the product's success metric in one sentence.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (3+ = structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Frankie | Conductor · Coordinator · Strategist (circuit map) · Client Owner | **4 — by design; the fee pays for the overload, the product relieves it** |
| Mia | Coordinator (day-of) · Production Realiser | 2 |
| Zoe | Client Owner · Creative Lead (the vision) | 2 — plus the emotional load no archetype names |
| Greg | Gatekeeper (the venue) · Production Realiser | 2 |
| Carol | Hidden Stakeholder · Sponsor (partial) | 2 — uncontracted, unavoidable |

### Frankie Wells, 37 — Founder & Lead Planner *(The Hired Hub)*
**Cirencester.** Ex-events at a five-star group; went solo after planning her sister's wedding better than her job's £2m conferences. Carries ten weddings in her head and a supplier circuit in her phone. **Attitude to AI:** intrigued and threatened in equal measure — she's read that planners are exactly what AI replaces, and suspects the truth is the opposite. **Winning her looks like:** the drudge-coordination vanishes, her judgement and taste become *more* visible to clients, and she takes on four more weddings a year without her Sundays.

### Mia Castellano, 27 — Assistant Planner *(The Day-Of General)*
**Cheltenham.** Runs wedding days like a stage manager: earpiece, clipboard, trainers under the dress. **Attitude to AI:** native; builds her own Notion trackers already. **Winning her looks like:** the final-week cascade assembles itself, the day schedule updates everyone's phones in real time, and her promotion case writes itself in saved hours.

### Zoe Hammond, 32 — The Bride *(The Civilian PM)*
**London.** Project-manages software releases; arrived with a Gantt chart and discovered weddings don't work like sprints. Decisive at work, oscillating here — because this one has her mother in it. **Attitude to AI:** fluent and keen; would happily self-serve status at midnight rather than message Frankie. **Winning her looks like:** she always knows what's decided, what's pending and what it costs — and the tool never once makes her wonder why she's paying a planner.

### Greg Farnham, 45 — Venue Events Manager *(The House Rules)*
**The manor.** Two hundred weddings deep; has seen every candle-policy argument and lost none. Protects the building, the licence and his Saturdays with the same calm. **Attitude to AI:** institutional indifference; works from the venue's own sheet. **Winning him looks like:** every supplier arrives knowing his rules already, load-ins run to the window, and nobody rings him twice about the same question.

### Carol, 61 — Mother of the Bride *(The Shadow Sponsor)*
**Harrogate.** Paying for the marquee and the band; holding opinions about everything else. Loves fiercely, interferes accordingly. **Attitude to AI:** none knowingly. **Winning the edge looks like:** her input is always heard, logged and attributed — and the decisions stay visibly Zoe's, which, deep down, is what Carol wants too.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Frankie ↔ Zoe | Planning agreement | Deep, fast-built → **wobble-sensitive at stress peaks** | WhatsApp + meetings | Emotional asymmetry: routine to the professional, once-in-a-lifetime to the client |
| Frankie ↔ supplier circuit | Per-wedding bookings | Career-long, reciprocal → **the real asset** | Texts answered first | The circuit is a repeat-game community the couple passes through once |
| Frankie ↔ Greg | Venue contract (couple's) | Professional, repeat → stable | Email + site visits | His rules are physics; her relationship converts them to flexibility at the margins |
| Zoe ↔ Carol | Family | Loving → **wedding-strained** | Sunday calls | The shadow channel; budget power without decision rights |
| Couple ↔ suppliers | Contracts via planner | Thin → planner-mediated by design | Through Frankie | Disintermediation here destroys the value chain — the product must reinforce the mediation |
| Mia ↔ everyone (the day) | The schedule | Total, for 14 hours → then dissolves | Earpiece + phones | Swift-trust at Gorse Films intensity, with champagne |
| **Frankie ↔ AI** | — | Threatened-then-converted | In-product | ◆ The strategic edge: the product must visibly amplify her, never audition for her job |
| **Zoe ↔ AI** | — | Self-serve native | Couple surface | Midnight status without midnight messages |
| **Suppliers ↔ AI** | — | Conscripted, zero-onboarding | WhatsApp bridge | The Gorse rule: value on first contact or nothing |
| **Carol ↔ AI** | — | Unknowing | Attributed input log | Heard, logged, not empowered |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- ◆ The family's question, answered: **serve the planner.** The planner's circuit knowledge, taste and crisis judgement are unreplicable; the articulation burden is. Position as the planner's force multiplier — and let planners bring the product to couples, never the reverse.
- The couple surface must thread a needle: full transparency on decisions and money, zero exposure of the backstage chaos — one truth per legitimate audience, where one audience is paying for serenity.
- The attributed-input log (Carol's channel) generalises: every consumer-hub constellation has uncontracted stakeholders with real power; logging-without-empowering is the design pattern.
