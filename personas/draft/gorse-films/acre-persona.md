# ACRE Persona 16 — The Rapid-Assembly Crew
### Gorse Films — a 5-person video production company building and dissolving a full crew per shoot

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #8 (Family 2, agency-led); ◆ adds the rapid-assembly / rapid-dissolution constellation.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Per-Shoot Assembler
**Instance:** Gorse Films, Bristol. Founder/executive producer, a producer, a production coordinator, an in-house editor, a part-time finance manager. £1.1m; brand films, commercials and branded documentary. The permanent company is five people; the company that makes anything is fifteen to forty, for days at a time.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Extreme: the entire delivery workforce — director, DOP, sound, gaffer, grade, motion — is freelance, assembled per project from a trusted-list hierarchy, dissolved at wrap. |
| Tooling | Email + call sheets (PDF, sacred), WhatsApp groups per shoot (born and abandoned in two weeks), Frame.io for review, Dropbox for delivery, a budget spreadsheet per job. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The producer per project; the coordinator in the 72 hours around any shoot, at intensity no other persona matches. |
| Buying behaviour | Per-project P&L thinking: tools must be justifiable inside one job's budget. Adopts review platforms because clients see them; resists anything crew must learn — the crew changes every job. |
| What failure costs | Shoot days are irreversibly expensive: a missed crew booking, a wrong location time or an unprepared client approval costs thousands per hour, on camera, in front of the client. Post-shoot, version chaos in the edit erodes margin invisibly. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A tiny permanent core that inflates into a full temporary organisation within days, performs under irreversible time pressure, and dissolves before any onboarding process could complete — then does it again with 60% different people.

**Cast:**
- **Edie Holloway, 44** — founder & executive producer (Bristol)
- **Tasha Reid, 32** — production coordinator; builder of call sheets (Bristol)
- **Niall Burke, 37** — freelance director, second-call on the trusted list (London)
- **Wren Castle, 31** — in-house editor; the constellation's only persistent maker (Bristol)
- **Joanna Pitt, 42** — client marketing director (the approval)
- **Plus:** producer, DOP, sound recordist, gaffer, runners, colourist, composer — per shoot

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The shoot date** — locked by talent, location and client diaries; everything else flexes around it
- **Location permits, weather and daylight** — non-negotiable physics with paperwork
- **Client legal/brand sign-off** — appears at script and at final cut; can unmake either
- **Music licensing** — a third party's pricing and terms gating the emotional centre of the film

**Topology:** Pulse-constellation: a core duo (producer + coordinator) around which a complete temporary hierarchy crystallises — film crews carry centuries-old role grammar (director > DOP > gaffer), so **structure is imported, not built**. Swift trust from credits and word-of-mouth does in hours what other constellations need months for.

**Lifecycle (per project, 4–8 weeks; the shoot itself 1–4 days):**
- *Assembly (days, not weeks):* director attached, crew called in trust-list order, availability roulette. The call sheet becomes the constitution.
- *Pre-production:* treatment, script, location, casting — client approvals on each, each one a potential stall.
- *The shoot (the crunch, relocated):* unlike every other persona, the crunch is mid-lifecycle and *scheduled*: 12-hour days where coordination failure costs by the hour and nobody checks email.
- *Post:* the constellation thins to editor + director + client; the edit-review loop (v1 → "thoughts from the wider team" → v7) is where margin quietly dies.
- *Dissolution (immediate):* wrap means gone — crew on other jobs within days; the WhatsApp group fossilises mid-sentence.
- *Trust curve:* imported at peak via the role grammar and credit lists; *individual* trust is built or destroyed in single days — one bad shoot removes a name from the list forever, usually without the person knowing.

**Cadence:** Email-paced in prep, WhatsApp-frantic around the shoot, Frame.io-comment-paced in post. Three completely different communication regimes per project, and the client experiences all three.

**Boundary objects:** The treatment (sold the dream), the call sheet (the one document everyone reads), the shot list, the budget (versioned against creep), the cut on Frame.io (where the client's "wider team" materialises), the licence and release paperwork (boring until it's a lawsuit).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Tasha around the shoot — call sheets, diet requirements, parking permits, weather plans: a 72-hour articulation supernova. On the producer across the arc. On Wren in post, where unfiltered client feedback meets creative reality.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** The trusted list — who's good, fast, difficult, worth double — lives in Edie's head and is the company's actual asset. Tasha holds shoot logistics. Niall holds the film-in-his-head that no document fully captures. Wren holds the footage's real contents ("there's a better take, I logged it"). Joanna holds the wider team's unstated politics. Nothing persists between projects except memories and invoices.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Assembly-window scramble: crew booking by phone roulette against availability nobody can see — **eliminate**
2. Call-sheet errors and last-minute changes that don't reach everyone — **eliminate** (on a shoot day this is the catastrophic class)
3. The "wider team" ambush in post: new stakeholders appear at v3 with v1 opinions — **contain**: the client's right to internal politics is real; the product's job is making its cost visible and its arrival early
4. Edit-round creep: "one more tweak" with no round economics — **eliminate** the invisibility (Studio Veld's failure mode 2, at higher day rates)
5. Wrap amnesia: everything learned about crew, location and client dissolving at dissolution — **eliminate**
6. The DOP's "we lose the light in 40 minutes" and the director's veto on a bad take — **preserve**: on-set creative authority is the entire quality system; no tool may ever blur it

**Conflicting interests:**
- The client wants the film their CEO imagines; the director wants the film on the treatment; these diverge at v2
- Crew want full-rate day bookings and quick payment; production wants flexibility and 30-day terms
- Edie wants the trusted list loyal; the trusted list wants whoever pays first
- Wren wants locked cuts; everyone upstream treats locked as a synonym for draft

*Contain-class: anchor the client conflict to the treatment and the round ledger; anchor crew economics to bookings and payment terms made visible.*

**Substitution stress-test:** This constellation substitutes *by design* — the role grammar means a replacement DOP is productive by 8am. The exceptions define the product: swap *Tasha* in the 72-hour window and the shoot is genuinely at risk (the logistics live in her head and a hundred unsent messages); swap *the director* after treatment and the film restarts creatively; swap *Joanna* mid-post and approval politics reset at v5 prices. Substitution here is cheap exactly where the role grammar is strong and catastrophic exactly where it isn't.

**Afterlife:** The trusted list *is* the afterlife: every project re-prices every name on it. Footage outlives everything (re-edits, cutdowns, "can we get the 2023 rushes?" — answerable only if wrap archiving happened). The client relationship persists project-to-project with total amnesia in between: the new shoot re-asks every preference the last one learned. The crew's WhatsApp numbers are the industry's real LinkedIn.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Hold the trusted list as living memory — availability, rates, history, notes — and run the assembly call-around. Make the call sheet self-updating and confirm receipt per person. Keep the round ledger and stakeholder map in post, so the wider team's arrival is priced, not absorbed. Archive at wrap automatically: footage log, crew notes, client preferences, licence terms. Be invisible on set — the product's shoot-day presence is a correct call sheet and silence.

### Edie Holloway, 44 — Founder & Executive Producer *(The Address Book)*
**Bristol.** Twenty years from runner to EP; knows every good crew member in the south-west and exactly which two should never share a unit base. Sells, budgets, soothes. **Attitude to AI:** commercially curious, creatively protective; would delegate the call-around tomorrow. **Winning her looks like:** assembly takes hours not days, and the company's knowledge stops being co-extensive with her memory.

### Tasha Reid, 32 — Production Coordinator *(The 72-Hour Engine)*
**Bristol.** Ex-festival ops; thrives at intensity that would hospitalise the org chart. Builds call sheets at midnight, fields the 6am "where's parking?" calls. **Attitude to AI:** desperate yes — anything that confirms receipt and chases silence. **Winning her looks like:** the call sheet updates itself to everyone's phone and she can see who hasn't read it.

### Niall Burke, 37 — Freelance Director *(The Imported Authority)*
**London.** Treatment-writer, set-commander, post-perfectionist; works with four production companies and remembers which ones protected his cut. **Attitude to AI:** fine in prep and post admin; total allergy to anything visible on set or near creative judgement. **Winning him looks like:** the politics arrive filtered, the feedback arrives consolidated, and nobody ever shows him a dashboard.

### Wren Castle, 31 — Editor *(The Persistent Maker)*
**Bristol.** The only maker who survives every dissolution; the company's de facto archive. Logs footage obsessively; nobody reads the logs. **Attitude to AI:** already uses transcription and auto-logging; wants the feedback chaos solved more than any cutting tool. **Winning them looks like:** every Frame.io comment, email note and "the CEO said" relay lands consolidated, attributed and against timecode.

### Joanna Pitt, 42 — Client Marketing Director *(The Approval With a Committee Behind Her)*
**Client-side.** Commissioned the film to be famous internally for it. Her "small thoughts" emails carry six invisible stakeholders. **Attitude to AI:** moderate; her company has a policy she hasn't read. **Winning her looks like:** she looks decisive upward because the process below her never wobbles in public.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Edie ↔ trusted list | Per-day bookings | Deep but **re-priced every job** → one bad day delists silently | Phone + WhatsApp | The list is the company; its state lives in one head |
| Edie ↔ Joanna | Project contract | Warm → **politics-opaque**: she speaks for a committee Edie never meets | Email + Frame.io | The wider team is the hidden counterparty |
| Tasha ↔ crew (per shoot) | Call sheet | Instant, role-imported → **hours old and load-bearing** | WhatsApp group | Swift trust at maximum; zero shared history as backup |
| Niall ↔ Wren | Per project | Craft-mutual → stable across jobs | Edit suite + Frame.io | The creative spine of post; client feedback strains it from outside |
| Wren ↔ Joanna's wider team | None | None → **the ambush edge** | Frame.io comments at v3 | Unknown stakeholders with reset-grade power |
| Company ↔ locations/permits/weather | Licences | N/A → physics | Paperwork + forecasts | The immovables that make shoot-day coordination irreversible |
| **Edie ↔ AI** | — | Willing delegator | In-product | The address-book externalisation is the win and the sensitivity: that data is her power |
| **Tasha ↔ AI** | — | Maximal, immediate | WhatsApp + call sheet | The 72-hour window is the product's proving ground |
| **Crew ↔ AI** | — | Conscripted per shoot, no patience | Phone-native, zero-onboarding | ◆ The defining constraint: the cast changes faster than onboarding can run — the product must work on first contact, every time |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- ◆ The rapid-assembly constraint is architectural: any crew-facing surface must deliver value in the first 30 seconds of first contact (a call sheet that confirms, a map that's right), because there is no second day to learn it.
- The trusted list is the most valuable and most personal dataset in the library so far: externalising it without expropriating it (it's Edie's career) is a trust-design problem, not a feature.
- Post-production's stakeholder-ambush edge needs the Studio Veld feedback ledger plus timecode: comments consolidated, attributed, and priced per round.
