> “On set, my word is final — that's not ego, it's how a crew of forty makes four hundred decisions a day. Off set, I'm a freelancer like everyone else, waiting to hear if the client's ‘wider team' has thoughts.”

## His world
Niall's job changes shape three times per project: salesman (the treatment), commander (the shoot), and diplomat (the edit, where the client's committee materialises at v3 with v1 opinions). He gives total focus in bursts and expects the production company to filter the politics so his head stays in the film. The ones that do get his best work and his first availability.

## What he's trying to do
- Make work that feeds the reel, not just the invoice
- Protect the cut: the film on the treatment is the film he signed up to deliver
- Work with producers who handle the chaos so he can handle the craft

## What gets in his way
- Feedback arriving raw, contradictory and unattributed from stakeholders he's never met
- "Small tweaks" that are structural recuts wearing diminutives
- Anything — anything — visible on set that isn't about making the day

## How he feels about AI
Fine in prep and post admin (transcripts, selects logging); total allergy to it near creative judgement or visible on set. He will never open a dashboard, and the right product never asks him to.

## In his own words
> “Consolidate the notes, name who said what, and tell me which ones the client actually means. Then leave me alone with the editor.”

## Win him by
Filtered, attributed, consolidated feedback in post — and complete invisibility everywhere else. Directors talk; the producer who runs the calmest post gets the best directors.
