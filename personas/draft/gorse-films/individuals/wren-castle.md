## Their world
Wren survives every dissolution: crews scatter, directors move on, and the footage — and its feedback chaos — stays in the suite. The craft (rhythm, story, grade-prep) is the job they love; the meta-job is feedback forensics: who said what, which note supersedes which, whether "the CEO's thoughts" arrived via Joanna accurately. Their logs could answer half the company's questions; nobody asks until the 2023 rushes are suddenly urgent.

## What they're trying to do
- Cut the best film the footage allows, round-creep notwithstanding
- Get feedback consolidated, attributed and pinned to timecode before it reaches the timeline
- Make the archive count: wrap-time logging that future projects actually use

## What gets in their way
- The v3 stakeholder ambush: new voices, old opinions, reset-grade power
- "Locked" as a synonym for draft
- Being the only person who knows what's actually in the footage

## How they feel about AI
Already deep in it — transcription, auto-logging, selects suggestions. The feedback chaos matters more to them than any cutting tool: solve the notes problem and the craft gets its hours back.

## In their own words
> “Every note, one place, one owner, one timecode. I'll take it from there. I'm good at the rest.”

## Win them by
Feedback consolidation against timecode, round economics made visible, and an archive that makes their logging legible — turning the company's most patient person into its best-equipped one.
