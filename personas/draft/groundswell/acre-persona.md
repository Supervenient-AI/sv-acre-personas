# ACRE Persona 8 — The Enablement Network
### Groundswell Collective — a 3-person nonprofit equipping grassroots campaign groups

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Capacity-Builder
**Instance:** Groundswell, a three-person nonprofit (plus freelancers, plus a new digital campaigner) that supports activists and community groups to run national campaigns — strategy, media, political engagement. Fully remote.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Outward by mission: the org exists to push capability across its own boundary and leave it there. The "clients" are volunteer groups with less capacity than the tiny hub serving them. |
| Tooling | Google Workspace (Drive "a total mess of stuff"), Slack internally — but the groups live on WhatsApp, so WhatsApp it also is. Notion half-adopted, Canva, Zoom, Calendly: a lot going on, not much of it synced. |
| Who carries coordination cost | Ruth — the director's week is one to two days of reporting treadmill plus the connective tissue of everything else. |
| Buying behaviour | Two screens before any purchase: an **ethics screen** (supplier politics and values genuinely move decisions) and a **budget screen** (subscription sprawl is a visible line item). Two of the team did a six-week AI course and joined its WhatsApp community for ongoing help. |
| What failure costs | Mission damage compounds quietly: knowledge that doesn't transfer, campaigns that don't outlive the hub's involvement, funders who can't see the impact. Hardest red line: some work involves people recounting trauma; those conversations are never transcribed, full stop, until security is beyond doubt. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A tiny expert hub serving many even-less-resourced external groups — where the "clients" are volunteer campaigners, the relationships are the methodology, and the goal is to leave capability *behind* rather than retain dependency.

**Cast:**
- **Ruth Calder-Jones, 45** — director (Sheffield)
- **Sam Whitaker, 33** — campaign lead (Manchester)
- **Maya Osei, 29** — digital campaigner, newly joined (Birmingham)
- Occasional freelancers
- **Donna Pryor, 52** — volunteer campaign lead at a health-justice group (the spoke that stays)
- **Naomi Fenwick, 41** — programme officer at a funding foundation (sympathetic, deadline-bound, numbers-hungry)
- The board (quarterly papers)

**External immovable nodes** (outside the working team, veto power over it):
- **Funders' grant cycles** — application windows and reporting deadlines that shape the org's year regardless of campaign reality
- **The political calendar** — votes, consultations, awareness days: campaign moments set by parliament and press, movable by no one
- **The Charity Commission and the board** — governance gates; quarterly papers that must exist whether or not there was time to write them

**Topology:** Hub-and-many-spokes, but with deliberately *outward* knowledge flow: the hub teaches templates and journeys to the spokes and wants the spokes to keep running after the hub steps away. A second, upward edge runs to funders, who supply the money and consume the evidence.

**Lifecycle:**
- *Per campaign:* assembly is slow and relational — trust with a volunteer group is built in calls and WhatsApp voice notes before any "work" begins. Then the capacity-building arc: template taught, journey launched, group increasingly autonomous. Dissolution is the *goal*: the hub steps back, the group keeps running. Whether anything actually persists after step-back is the org's defining open question.
- *Per grant:* apply → deliver → report, on the funder's calendar. The reporting deadline always lands mid-campaign.
- *Midpoint pattern:* campaigns sag after the launch energy; the group's volunteers wobble; Ruth's intervention at the sag *is* the methodology, and it's pure relational judgment no template captures.
- *Trust curve:* with groups, the inverse of the commercial pattern — built slowly from near-zero (activists are rightly suspicious of professionalised help) and extremely durable once earned, but destroyed permanently by anything that makes supporters feel processed.

**Cadence:** WhatsApp-paced with groups (evenings and weekends, when volunteers exist), office-hours with funders, quarterly with the board, and an internal Slack that mixes all three time signatures into one stream.

**Boundary objects:** The campaign plan, the supporter-email journey (taught from a template every time), the funder report, the board pack, the impact evidence — qualitative, relational, and chronically hard to quantify.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Ruth, in the gaps between every layer — group to funder, team to board, campaign to campaign. The reporting treadmill is articulation work formalised; the rest of it is invisible even to her.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Ruth holds the methodology, the funder relationships and the political map — the org *is* substantially her head. Sam holds the live campaign state. Maya arrives holding digital fluency and a sharp sense of how trust breaks when tools are imposed — and can find nothing, because "where does someone find stuff?" has no answer. Donna holds her community's trust and her campaign's institutional memory, on a phone. The impact evidence — the thing funders pay for — lives scattered in anecdotes, voice notes and inboxes, unharvested.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged: eliminate / contain / preserve)*:
1. Knowledge trapped in three heads while the team grows — "where does someone find stuff?" has no answer — **eliminate**
2. Every campaign rebuilt semi-from-scratch despite the journeys being 70% standard — **eliminate**
3. Impact going unmeasured because evaluation is the most deferrable task on a tiny team — **eliminate**
4. The reporting treadmill (board, funders) eating one to two days a week of the director's time — **eliminate**
5. Voice erosion: their work *must* sound human and relational; over-AI'd output is mission damage, not just quality damage — **contain**: AI drafting is allowed, the "never the last draft" rule is the containment, and the human pass is non-negotiable
6. Funders' hunger for numbers vs. the inherently relational work — **contain**: a permanent translation conflict; the AI helps quantify without letting metrics redefine the mission
7. The hours spent hand-holding groups through things a tool could do — **preserve**: the hand-holding *is* the methodology; trust transfers through it. Automate behind it, never instead of it
8. The ethics screen slowing tool adoption — **preserve**: the values veto is identity, not bureaucracy

**Conflicting interests:**
- The groups need hand-holding and human warmth; the org needs leverage and standardisation
- Funders need legible impact numbers from inherently relational work
- The ethics screen narrows the tool options the budget screen has already narrowed
- Ruth needs to be less central; everything the org has built routes through her being central

*Contain-class: none of these resolve — the org's craft is holding them, and the AI's job is to make the holding cheaper.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Maya's arrival is a live substitution test in reverse — a new node joining an undocumented system — and the org is failing it gently (week one was warm; week two was "ask Ruth"). Swap *Ruth* and the org effectively halts: methodology, funder trust and political judgment all leave at once; the board would be governing a shell. Swap *Donna* — volunteer burnout, the commonest event in the whole network — and her campaign likely dies, because the group's memory lives on her phone: the strongest argument that what the hub leaves behind must be an *artefact*, not a person's habits.

**Afterlife:** This constellation's afterlife is its mission statement: the entire point is what remains after the hub steps away. Today, what persists with a group is a template, some habits and whatever Donna remembers; what persists at the hub is scattered anecdote. The funder edge persists across grants — Naomi moves foundations and remains a contact; impact evidence harvested once funds the next three applications. An org whose product is afterlife currently has no system for its own.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Become the organisational memory ("Notion, but easier"): weekly priorities surfaced without ceremony, knowledge findable by the new hire. Compress the governance treadmill — board reports and funder reports drafted from accumulated knowledge, not reconstructed each cycle. Productise the templates: a campaigner briefs in their campaign, out comes a six-month email journey *they* own. Help quantify qualitative impact. Respect the bright lines absolutely: trauma-adjacent material untouched, the human voice preserved, the values screen passed.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (count of collaboration archetypes per seat; 3+ signals structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Ruth | Conductor · Coordinator · Strategist · Sponsor | **4 — the founder-hub pathology, nonprofit edition: mission, money and memory in one head** |
| Sam | Specialist Maker · Coordinator (campaign-level) | 2 |
| Maya | Specialist Maker · Newcomer | 2 — the onboarding test case |
| Donna | Client Owner · Conductor (of her own volunteer constellation) | 2 — held in evenings and weekends |
| Naomi (funder) | Gatekeeper · Sponsor | 2 — both at once, which defines the edge |

The product's first job: get the org's memory out of Ruth's head — for Maya's sake immediately, for the mission's sake permanently.

### Ruth Calder-Jones, 45 — Director *(The Relational Engine)*
**Sheffield**, works from the kitchen table with a dramatic dog. Talks her thinking out loud — uses dictation constantly, "blurts" at ChatGPT and asks it to organise her back. Drafts awkward funder emails with AI and rewrites them to sound like herself; would never send anything unchecked ("never the last draft"). Did the AI course, thrived for three weeks, got lost in week four — her ceiling isn't curiosity, it's the absence of anyone in-house to ask. **Attitude to AI:** enthusiastic 4–5, impact so far 2: the gap is support, not will. **Winning her looks like:** the reporting treadmill drops to half a day, her blurts become organised institutional memory, and nothing the org sends has lost her voice.

### Sam Whitaker, 33 — Campaign Lead *(The Front Line)*
**Manchester.** Holds the live state of every active campaign; the person Donna actually messages at 9pm. Half the team's AI-course alumni; uses it for press-list research and first drafts. **Attitude to AI:** practical, group-protective — anything that touches the groups gets triple-checked for warmth. **Winning him looks like:** campaign state stops living in his thumbs, and the 70%-standard journeys assemble themselves so his time goes to the 30% that's relationship.

### Maya Osei, 29 — Digital Campaigner *(The New Capacity)*
**Birmingham.** Joining from a charity that *banned* AI outright — where, she reports, everyone simply used it in secret. Carries both digital fluency and a sharp sense of how trust breaks when tools are imposed. **Attitude to AI:** fluent and pro-transparency — the "use it freely, but tell each other" house rule is exactly the policy her last employer needed. **Winning her looks like:** she can self-serve the org's knowledge in week one, and her digital instincts upgrade the templates rather than queueing behind Ruth's bandwidth.

### Donna Pryor, 52 — Volunteer Campaign Lead *(The Spoke That Stays)*
**Rotherham.** Runs a health-justice campaign evenings and weekends from WhatsApp and a shared Gmail, around shifts as an NHS administrator. **Attitude to AI:** none — attitude to *tools*: anything left with her must work on a phone, cost nothing, and never make her supporters feel processed. **Winning her looks like:** the journey the hub taught her keeps running without the hub, and nothing about it feels like software.

### Naomi Fenwick, 41 — Programme Officer *(The Sympathetic Gatekeeper)*
**London.** Believes in the work; answers to a trustee board that believes in numbers. **Attitude to AI:** agnostic; allergic to obviously-generated grant reports, which she now receives weekly from other grantees. **Winning her looks like:** reports that arrive on time, in Ruth's voice, with the qualitative story *and* a number she can put in front of trustees.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Org ↔ campaign groups (Donna) | None — covenant of solidarity | Slow-built, deep → **durable but absolute**: one "processed" moment destroys it permanently. Sustained by: warmth, showing up. Eroded by: anything that smells automated | WhatsApp, evenings | The sacred edge: AI works *behind* it, preparing what humans deliver warmly — never on it |
| Org ↔ funders (Naomi) | Grant agreements | Good → **strained by legibility gap**: relational impact vs. numerical reporting | Email + portals + annual visits | The highest-leverage edge to automate; also where voice erosion would be noticed first |
| Ruth ↔ Sam | Employment | Deep → stable | Slack + calls | Shared context so habitual that Maya can't see into it |
| Ruth ↔ Maya | Employment, new | Warm → **forming**: the onboarding edge, currently running on Ruth's bandwidth | Slack | Every unanswered "where do I find…" spends new-hire enthusiasm |
| Ruth ↔ the board | Governance | Confident → stable, paper-hungry | Quarterly packs | Consumes one to two days a week that the mission needed |
| Donna ↔ her volunteers | None | Personal → **the network's most fragile layer**: burnout is the commonest failure in the whole system | WhatsApp | Invisible to the org until a campaign goes quiet |
| **Ruth ↔ AI** | — | Enthusiastic, blurt-native, support-starved | Dictation + chat | Wants a colleague that organises her thinking; abandons tools that need ceremony |
| **Maya ↔ AI** | — | Fluent, transparency-principled | In-product | Will champion governed use; will not tolerate surveillance dressed as collaboration |
| **Donna ↔ AI** | — | Unknowing, phone-only | Behind WhatsApp | Must be invisible or weightless; a login is a barrier, a cost is a veto |
| **Org ↔ AI (policy)** | — | "Use freely, tell each other" | House norm | The transparency norm is a product feature waiting to exist: AI use visible to teammates by default |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The group edges define the hardest constraint in the library: total automation *behind* the edge, zero perceptible automation *on* it. Warmth is the deliverable.
- The funder edge is the beachhead — formal, cyclical, evidence-hungry, and nobody's sacred ground. Reports drafted from accumulated knowledge win Ruth a day a week immediately.
- The "tell each other" norm is a design gift: build AI-use transparency in as a first-class feature and this org's policy becomes the product's differentiator for every values-driven account behind it.
- The trauma red line is absolute and architectural: some content classes must be untouchable by design, provably, not by promise.
