# Diana Pelham
**Founder & Senior Agent, Pelham Literary · 56 · Bloomsbury**
*The Forty-Year Folklore — from Pelham, ACRE Persona 38*

> “The archive room holds contracts from 1987 that still pay royalties and still block deals. Sixty per cent of it is indexed. The other forty per cent is me, remembering. The agency's sale price, my authors' pensions and my own succession all live in that gap — and I've spent a decade not looking at it.”

## Her world
Diana's days split between the craft (reading, editing judgement, the deal) and the duty (seventy careers' worth of attention, rationed). Her quiet shames are systemic: royalty statements unaudited for want of decoding-hours, rights unexploited for want of bandwidth, and an archive whose 40% unindexed remainder is her memory — which makes succession, the agency's existential question, currently unanswerable.

## What she's trying to do
- Keep the curation standard absolute — the agency's brand *is* her taste and her honesty
- Get the archive indexed, the statements audited, the backlog worked
- Make the agency survivable without her — for the authors' sake, eventually her own

## What gets in her way
- Decades of paper holding live rights and obligations
- Statement-decoding craft concentrated in one royalties manager
- Her instinctive suspicion of anything that smells like the slop now flooding submission inboxes

## How she feels about AI
Suspicious by aesthetics, persuadable by archaeology: the machine that reads forty years of contracts and finds the unsold Brazilian rights is doing scholarship, not slop. Submission letters stay human, forever, on principle.

## In her own words
> “Index the archive, audit the statements, rank the backlog. Touch the taste and we're done; do the archaeology and you've saved the agency.”

## Win her by
The archivist posture: contracts parsed with provenance, found money surfacing season after season, succession made thinkable — and not one machine-written sentence near an author or an editor.
