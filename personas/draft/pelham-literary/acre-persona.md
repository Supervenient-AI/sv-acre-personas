# ACRE Persona 38 — The Long-Contract Matchmaker
### Pelham Literary — a 6-person literary and speaker agency where the boundary objects outlive everyone who signed them

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #30 (Family 8, matchmakers); not yet interview-grounded.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Rights Custodian
**Instance:** Pelham Literary, Bloomsbury. Founder-agent, two associate agents, a rights manager, a royalties/contracts manager, an assistant. ~70 authors (novelists, narrative non-fiction, a growing speaker roster), 15% domestic / 20% elsewhere, and a back-catalogue of contracts stretching to 1987.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | The matchmaker shape at geological pace: agent between authors and publishers (then film scouts, foreign houses, festivals, speaker bookers) — with deals that take months, contracts that run decades, and royalties that trickle back through systems built when fax was modern. |
| Tooling | Email (the industry's bloodstream), Word documents with tracked changes (the negotiation medium), a rights database that is 60% accurate, royalty statements arriving as PDFs designed to discourage reading, book-fair spreadsheets, and an archive room with actual paper. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The agents per deal; the royalties manager per statement-season; the rights manager across a permanent backlog of un-exploited territories and reverted rights nobody has re-sold. |
| Buying behaviour | Tradition-bound and margin-thin; buys almost nothing, changes slower than its contracts. But royalty-audit horror stories and the rights-backlog guilt are live wounds money already touches. |
| What failure costs | A rights clause fumbled costs an author (and the agency's 15–20%) for the *life of copyright* — failure here compounds for seventy years. A royalty under-reporting un-audited is silent theft. An author feeling unloved leaves — with their backlist's future. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A matchmaker constellation in slow motion: the agency synchronises author-side truth (ambition, finances, fragile confidence) with publisher-side truth (real enthusiasm, real budgets, internal politics) across deals measured in months and contracts measured in decades — while the rights estate, the true asset, silently accrues exploitation debt.

**Cast:**
- **Diana Pelham, 56** — founder & senior agent (Bloomsbury)
- **Aoife Lennon, 31** — rights manager; seller of territories, keeper of the backlog (Bloomsbury)
- **Cleo Marchbank, 47** — author: mid-list literary novelist, growing speaking career, royalty statements unread in a drawer
- **Charlotte Spear, 38** — commissioning editor at a mid-size publisher (the recurring counterparty)
- **Plus:** the associate agents, the royalties manager, foreign co-agents (a global sub-network), film/TV scouts, festival and speaker bookers, publishers' contracts departments

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The book fairs (London, Frankfurt)** — the rights market's twice-yearly pulse; deals queue toward them and momentum dies between them
- **Publishers' royalty systems** — statements twice yearly, in arrears, in formats apparently designed by adversaries
- **Copyright law and the contract archive** — clauses signed in 1994 governing audiobook rights nobody imagined; the past as a live counterparty
- **The market's taste** — what publishers think readers want, shifting underneath every submission

**Topology:** The Bowline bowtie at publisher-deal level, nested inside a *rights lattice*: every book spawns dozens of separable rights (territories, languages, formats, film, audio, speaking) each potentially its own matchmaking process with its own counterparty — most of them dormant, which is the backlog. The author edge is the deep one; the publisher edges are recurring; the rights edges are combinatorial.

**Lifecycle (per book, 2–4 years to publication; per contract, decades):**
- *The submission:* the manuscript goes out; the agency's curated enthusiasm meets editors' private reality; auctions or silences follow.
- *The deal & the contract (the slow crunch):* months of tracked-changes negotiation over clauses whose value reveals itself in decade-scale hindsight.
- *Publication & the rights cascade:* foreign, audio, film options — Aoife's market, fair by fair.
- *The long tail:* royalty statements, earn-out (or not), option clauses binding the next book, rights reverting unexploited.
- *Midpoint pattern, per author:* the career wobble — book three underperforms, the publisher cools, the author despairs — where agenting becomes management of a human being's creative identity (the Orbit pastoral layer at literary pace).
- *Trust curve:* the author's trust is profound and slow — built over years, spent by inattention (the unread statement, the unsold territory, the unanswered email in week three); publisher trust is professional and portfolio-based (Diana's submissions get read first because they're reliably good).

**Cadence:** Email at publishing pace (days, not hours), fair-season frenzies, statement-season slogs (March and September), the author's occasional 9pm wobble call, and option-clause clocks ticking silently in filing cabinets.

**Boundary objects:** The manuscript, the submission list (who's seen it, who's passed — market intelligence that evaporates), the deal memo, **the contract** (the longest-lived boundary object in the entire library — septuagenarian clauses still paying or still blocking), the royalty statement (adversarially formatted truth), the rights grid (which territories/formats are sold, free, reverted — at 60% accuracy), the option clause.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On the agents per deal and per author-soul; on the royalties manager seasonally (decoding statements against contracts); on Aoife permanently — the rights backlog is articulation debt made commercial: every unexploited territory is a coordination task nobody got to.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Diana holds forty years of market folklore and every editor's true tastes. Aoife holds the rights grid's real state (the database holds its aspiration). The royalties manager holds the statement-decoding craft. Cleo holds her own backlist's history vaguely and trusts the agency to hold it precisely (it holds it at 60%). Charlotte holds her publisher's internal politics. The contract archive holds the actual truth, on paper, unindexed.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. The rights backlog: territories and formats unsold not by decision but by bandwidth — **eliminate**; this is inventory rotting in a warehouse nobody inventories
2. Royalty under-scrutiny: statements unaudited against contract terms because decoding them takes craft-hours nobody has — **eliminate**
3. Rights-grid inaccuracy: the database vs. the paper archive vs. reality — **eliminate**
4. Submission-intelligence evaporation: who passed, why, in which season — folklore lost between books — **eliminate**
5. Option-clause and reversion clocks unwatched — **eliminate**
6. The author-attention drought: quiet authors quietly leaving — **contain** the portfolio-attention economics; **eliminate** the inattention's invisibility
7. Diana's editorial honesty ("this draft isn't ready, and I won't send it out") — **preserve**: the agency's brand *is* its curation; volume would destroy it
8. The publisher's adversarial statement formats — **contain**: their systems won't change; the decoding can be industrialised

**Conflicting interests:**
- The author wants advocacy and attention; the agency's economics ration both across seventy clients
- The publisher wants rights broad and cheap; the agency exists to keep them narrow and dear
- Aoife's backlog wants exploitation; some authors want control, silence or rest
- The agency's commission follows the deal; the author's interest sometimes counsels refusing it — the Bowline integrity tension at literary stakes

*Contain-class, with the matchmaker asymmetry again named for protection: the author must never see the editor's blunt pass-note raw; the editor must never see the author's desperation. Both truths, scoped.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap the assistant: fine. Swap *the royalties manager*: statement-season craft (which clauses to check against which publisher's known habits) leaves; under-payments go undetected for cycles. Swap *Aoife*: the gap between the rights database and reality becomes unbridgeable; the backlog fossilises. Swap *Charlotte* (editors move constantly): Cleo's in-house champion vanishes mid-list — the orphaned-author event, publishing's most common career wound; the agency's job is surviving it. Swap *Diana*: the folklore, the editor-trust network and seventy author relationships — succession is the agency's existential question, and the archive's 60% accuracy is why it can't currently be answered.

**Afterlife:** The longest in the library, literally: contracts outlive authors (estates become clients), rights revert and re-sell across generations, and the backlist is the pension — the agency's and the author's. Every deal's afterlife is a revenue stream needing periodic attention forever. Submission intelligence and editor-taste folklore compound across decades — or evaporate per retirement. The archive room *is* the business; it's just unreadable.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Read the archive: every contract parsed — rights granted, territories, reversion triggers, option clauses — into a rights grid that is finally *true*, with clocks on everything. Audit the statements: each royalty PDF decoded against its contract's actual terms, discrepancies flagged (found money, season after season). Work the backlog: unexploited rights surfaced as ranked opportunities for Aoife's judgement. Keep submission intelligence alive across books and years. Watch the author-attention economics — who hasn't been called — without ever automating the call itself. And honour the pace: this constellation's trust is slow; the product should feel like a meticulous archivist, not a growth hacker.

### Diana Pelham, 56 — Founder & Senior Agent *(The Forty-Year Folklore)*
**Bloomsbury.** Started as an editorial assistant in 1989; has placed two Bookers and nursed a hundred careers through the wilderness. Negotiates like silk over steel. **Attitude to AI:** instinctively suspicious (the industry's soul is taste), pragmatically curious (the archive guilt is real). **Winning her looks like:** the contracts finally indexed, the statements finally audited, succession finally thinkable — and not one submission letter written by a machine.

### Aoife Lennon, 31 — Rights Manager *(The Lattice Keeper)*
**Bloomsbury.** Sells Korean rights at Frankfurt by day, reconciles the grid by night. Knows the backlog's shame intimately: a bestseller's Brazilian rights sat free for six years. **Attitude to AI:** the agency's one enthusiast — contract parsing and opportunity-ranking are exactly her bottleneck. **Winning her looks like:** the grid is true, the clocks tick visibly, and fair meetings start from ranked opportunity lists instead of memory.

### Cleo Marchbank, 47 — Author *(The Mid-List Soul)*
**Norwich.** Four novels, decent reviews, modest sales, a surprising second income emerging from festival and corporate speaking. Trusts Diana completely; understands her own contracts not at all. **Attitude to AI:** wary in public (her union has views), privately grateful for anything that means her statements get *read*. **Winning her looks like:** her backlist works harder (territories sold, speaking gigs flowing), her money arrives explained, and her agent's attention feels undiminished.

### Charlotte Spear, 38 — Commissioning Editor *(The Counterparty Champion)*
**The publisher.** Loves books, fights acquisition boards, moves house every three years (the industry's churn). **Attitude to AI:** her publisher has a policy and a working group; she has a slush pile and no time. **Winning the edge looks like:** Pelham's submissions stay the reliably excellent ones, the contracts negotiate efficiently, and nobody sends her a machine-written pitch letter — she can tell.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Diana ↔ Cleo | Agency agreement (career-long) | Profound, slow → **inattention-eroded** | Calls + lunches + 9pm wobbles | Career and confidence in trust; the statement-in-the-drawer is the dependency made paper |
| Diana ↔ Charlotte | None — repeat counterparty | Professional-warm → submission-quality-funded | Email + lunches | Adversaries in negotiation, allies in the book; the dual truth held politely |
| Cleo ↔ Charlotte | Publishing contract | Mediated → champion-shaped | Through Diana, then directly once signed | The orphaned-author risk lives here when Charlotte moves |
| Aoife ↔ foreign co-agents | Sub-agency network | Reciprocal, fair-paced → reliability-scored | Email + Frankfurt | A global edge network running on two meetings a year |
| Agency ↔ publishers' royalty systems | The contracts | Adversarial-procedural → **audit-shaped** | PDF statements | Truth arrives obfuscated, twice a year, in arrears |
| Agency ↔ the archive | — | The past as counterparty | Paper | Decades of clauses, 60% indexed: the asset and the liability in one room |
| **Diana ↔ AI** | — | Suspicious, guilt-leveraged | Archivist surface | Taste untouched; archaeology welcomed |
| **Aoife ↔ AI** | — | The champion | Rights grid | Parse, rank, clock — her job's missing half |
| **Cleo ↔ AI** | — | Publicly wary, privately served | Author statements view | Her money, explained; her voice, untouched |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The contract archive is the wedge: parsing decades of paper into a true rights grid with live clocks converts the agency's deepest guilt into its newest revenue — and makes succession (the existential question) answerable.
- Royalty-statement auditing is the matchmaker family's most provable ROI yet: found money, recurring, with the publisher's own contract as the evidence standard.
- The pace constraint generalises the family's lesson: matchmaker trust is the product's host organism — here it moves in years, and the product must move with it. The same disclosure architecture as Bowline and Orbit, at a tempo that punishes any whiff of automation on the human edges.
