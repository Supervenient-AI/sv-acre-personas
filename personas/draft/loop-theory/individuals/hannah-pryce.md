# Hannah Pryce
**Marketing Manager (client-side) · 35**
*The Summoner — from Loop Theory, ACRE Persona 2*

> “Sorry, quick one before I log off—”
> *(It is 5.55pm. It is either nothing or everything. She doesn't know which either.)*

## At a glance
| | |
|---|---|
| Age | 35 |
| Lives | Reading — commutes to a head office two days a week, kitchen-table marketing department the rest |
| Background | Eight years client-side across two consumer brands; first time owning an agency relationship |
| Role | Marketing manager; Loop Theory is “her” agency and her reputation upward rides on it |
| Pressure | A CMO who asks her questions she relays downward within the hour, urgency intact, context removed |
| Channels | Slack into the agency's day (the live wire), email for cover, ChatGPT for everything she's not sure about |

## Her world
Hannah is the agency's weather system: warm, engaged, and capable of detonating an afternoon with one message. Her questions are quick to ask and expensive to answer, and she genuinely can't tell which of them matter — she's relaying pressure from a building the agency never sees. Once she rewrote their long-form article in ChatGPT and sent it back “improved”; it wasn't, and the diplomatic clean-up took a week.

## What she's trying to do
- Look in-control upward: her CMO's confidence is her actual KPI
- Get fast answers without feeling like she's queued behind other clients
- Prove the agency spend in numbers her finance director accepts

## What gets in her way
- Not knowing which of her own questions are urgent
- A board cadence that demands answers faster than marketing produces them
- Her own enthusiasm with ungoverned AI tools

## How she feels about AI
Enthusiastic consumer, no governance instincts. She'll paste anything into anything the night before a board meeting. The agency can't stop that — but it can be more useful than the habit.

## In her own words
> “I just need to know it's handled. If I can see it's handled, I won't ask. Mostly.”

## Win her by
Answers fast enough that she looks good in her own building — without her ever sensing a queue, a filter, or a screen between her and “her” team.
