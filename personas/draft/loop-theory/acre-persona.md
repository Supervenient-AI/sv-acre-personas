# ACRE Persona 2 — The Agency-Led Account
### Loop Theory — a 3-person growth marketing agency running a client account with rotating freelancers

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Post-Contraction Boutique Agency
**Instance:** Loop Theory, growth/performance marketing, Bristol/London/remote. 3 full-time staff plus roughly the same again in freelancers (two regulars, a rotating cast behind them). Recently shrank from a bigger team; the founders now do client work *and* run the company.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Networked by necessity: delivery capacity lives outside the payroll. Every client account is a small constellation of employees, freelancers and client-side contacts. |
| Tooling | Google Workspace + Slack; paid Claude subscription shared informally; dashboarding tools are the biggest software outlay. The client's channel always wins. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The founders — in the evening shift after client work. The account exec absorbs the real-time fragments. |
| Buying behaviour | Will trial anything that promises to relieve the founders, but has been burned: a reporting-automation tool got better, then worse, then quietly died, and the team had stopped using it before the vendor folded. They will test a new product hard and tell you exactly why it broke. |
| What failure costs | Continuous accounts die slowly: a missed signal, a stale report, a burnt-out founder — then a "we're reviewing our agencies" email. Losing one client of five is a 20% revenue event. |

---

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** An agency hub running a continuous client account — not a one-off project — with freelancers attached per-workstream and a client contact who can summon attention at any moment.

**Cast:**
- **Marcus Bell, 41** — founder, strategy + everything (Bristol)
- **Leila Fontaine, 38** — co-founder, delivery + content (London)
- **Jess Adeyemi, 26** — account exec (remote, Sheffield)
- **Tom Rourke, 33** — freelance paid media specialist (Leeds)
- **Erin Walsh, 31** — freelance designer (Cardiff)
- **Hannah Pryce, 35** — client-side marketing manager (the live wire)
- Client-side finance contact (invoices only)

**External immovable nodes** (outside the working team, veto power over it):
- **The ad platforms** (Meta, Google) — algorithm changes, ad-review rejections and policy updates arrive unannounced and re-write the media plan overnight; no one in the constellation can appeal to them on a human timescale
- **The client's CFO / budget cycle** — invisible to the agency until Hannah's tone changes; quarterly re-forecasts can halve spend mid-campaign
- **The annual agency review** — the gate the whole account ultimately lives or dies by

**Topology:** Agency-led hub, but with a live wire to the client: Hannah's Slack/email reaches straight into the agency's day and detonates priorities. Freelancers hang off individual workstreams and rarely see the whole account.

**Lifecycle:** A continuous account has no project arc — its shape in time is **nested pulses**:
- *Monthly:* the reporting cycle. Numbers close, the narrative gets written late, the report lands, Hannah forwards it upward and questions bounce back down.
- *Quarterly:* planning and re-forecast — the client's internal politics briefly become the agency's problem.
- *Annually:* contract renewal, the account's midpoint-reset equivalent: the frame set at pitch ("you're our growth partner") gets re-examined, and whatever resentments accumulated unspoken get invoiced emotionally.
- *Per-workstream:* miniature project lifecycles — brief, freelancer assembly (onboarding from scratch, every time), delivery, dissolution — running constantly inside the continuous relationship.
- *Trust curve:* won at pitch, spent in the gaps between reports. Each unanswered 5.55pm ping, each "improved" deliverable dispute, each resourcing wobble draws it down; a good quarterly review only partially refills it.

**Cadence:** Continuous, punctuated by the client's "throwaway" questions — quick to ask, expensive to answer, impossible to triage by urgency from the message alone.

**Boundary objects:** The monthly performance report (numbers + written narrative — the account's heartbeat), the media plan, the dashboard (looked at by everyone, trusted by no one alone), the timesheet (a work of collective fiction completed three weeks late), the brief per workstream.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Jess in real time (forty triage decisions a day, no information) and on Marcus at night (capacity, sequencing, who's on what). Neither is recognised as work; both are invisible until they fail.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Marcus holds the account's strategic history and the client's political map. Tom holds the campaign configuration — locked inside ad platforms the rest of the team never opens. Leila holds the brand voice. Jess holds the unwritten codex of which Hannah pings are real. The dashboard holds the numbers, but the *narrative* of why they moved exists only in whoever wrote last month's report. No shared system holds the account.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged: eliminate / contain / preserve)*:
1. Client comms triage — every message treated as urgent because nobody knows which ones are — **eliminate**
2. Each person disappearing into their own private AI chats; insight never returns to the team — **eliminate**
3. Freelancer onboarding repeated from scratch per workstream — **eliminate**
4. Resource whiplash: over-profitable with a burnt-out team, or comfortable and unprofitable — never level — **eliminate** (an information failure: nobody can see capacity)
5. Timesheets reconstructed from memory, "invariably wrong" — **eliminate**
6. The workslop loop — Hannah rewriting agency copy in ChatGPT and sending it back "improved" — **contain**: the client's right to push back on creative is legitimate and must reach the team; what must go is the silent rewrite that destroys provenance
7. Marcus interrogating every number the AI or the team produces — **preserve**: his scepticism is the account's quality control, not friction to be smoothed

**Conflicting interests:**
- Hannah wants instant responsiveness and a vendor who makes *her* look good upward
- The founders want protected thinking time and a profitable account
- The freelancers want clean briefs and to bill for the onboarding nobody scopes
- Jess absorbs the gap between all three and wants any of them to notice

*All contain-class: the AI's job is to keep them anchored to artefacts — requests triaged visibly, onboarding scoped and chargeable, capacity made legible — so service tension never becomes relationship rupture.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap Jess (the likeliest churn — young, underpaid, absorbing everything) and client pings hit the founders raw for a month while the new exec learns Hannah's tells from nothing, because the codex was never written down. Swap Tom mid-campaign and the constellation discovers his platform configurations are the only true record of the media plan: access handover, naming conventions, audience logic — weeks of archaeology. Swap *Hannah* (she's ambitious; she'll move) and the account's trust resets to zero with a stranger who didn't choose this agency — the renewal gate arrives early.

**Afterlife:** A continuous account's afterlife question is *renewal*, asked annually. Edges that persist regardless: Marcus↔Tom and Marcus↔Erin carry to every other account the agency runs — freelancer trust is agency infrastructure. If the account is lost, the strategic history leaves in Marcus's head and the campaign archaeology leaves in Tom's platform logins; the agency retains almost nothing structural to make the *next* pitch smarter. Nothing is currently archived between workstreams, which is why onboarding repeats.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Sit *in Slack* as a contributor, not in a separate app. Triage inbound client requests (urgent / needs thinking / can hold) without ever visibly gatekeeping the client. Hold account knowledge so freelancers onboard themselves. Estimate timesheets from observable activity and ask for a 5-second confirm — "a quicker wrong is better than a slower wrong." Surface the resourcing picture before it becomes a crisis.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (count of collaboration archetypes per seat; 3+ signals structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Marcus | Conductor · Strategist · Coordinator · Sponsor | **4 — the player-manager pathology as a number** |
| Leila | Creative Lead · Production Realiser | 2 |
| Jess | Coordinator (frontline) · Gatekeeper (triage, unauthorised) | 2 — both halves unrecognised |
| Tom | Specialist Maker · Peripheral Expert | 2 |
| Erin | Specialist Maker | 1 |
| Hannah | Client Owner · Gatekeeper (of the renewal) | 2 |

The product's first job: take Coordinator off Marcus and give Jess's invisible Gatekeeper role the information it has always lacked.

### Marcus Bell, 41 — Founder *(The Player-Manager)*
**Bristol.** School-run hours, then a second shift at 9pm. Treats AI as "a fairly experienced mid-tier colleague — brilliant but often stupid": he'll delegate to it but interrogates every number. Wants the first 20% and last 20% of any deliverable to stay human, partly conviction, partly self-respect. Constantly slightly wrong about capacity. Has "try Claude's new features" on his list every week and never gets to it. **Attitude to AI:** committed sceptic — uses it daily, trusts it conditionally. **Winning him looks like:** the resourcing picture appears without him building it, and the AI shows its working when he challenges a number.

### Leila Fontaine, 38 — Co-founder *(The Delivery Engine)*
**London.** Has quietly vibe-coded a booking tool for a side project; the pragmatic experimenter of the pair. Holds the brand voice for every client and the content quality bar. **Attitude to AI:** would let it draft, never let it send. **Winning her looks like:** drafts arrive in the client's actual voice because the AI learned it from the account history, and nothing goes out without her hand on it.

### Jess Adeyemi, 26 — Account Exec *(The Shock Absorber)*
**Remote, Sheffield.** First responder to every client ping; decides forty times a day what's urgent with no information. The role that benefits most from triage and gets asked least. Building a career on being indispensable and is quietly exhausted by what that costs. **Attitude to AI:** open, fluent, unsentimental — her generation's default tool. Wary only of anything that makes her invisible work *more* invisible. **Winning her looks like:** triage stops being guesswork, and the record finally shows how much she was holding.

### Tom Rourke, 33 — Freelance Paid Media *(The Repeat Spoke)*
**Leeds, three other clients.** Sets up campaigns in Meta/Google — a time-slog of pure configuration. Bills by the day; unscoped onboarding and "quick calls" eat his margin. **Attitude to AI:** practical, but wary of anything that watches how long things take him — he sells days, and the product must never become the client's stopwatch. **Winning him looks like:** he opens a new workstream and the account context is already there; onboarding time becomes billable scope or disappears.

### Erin Walsh, 31 — Freelance Designer *(The Per-Workstream Visitor)*
**Cardiff.** Works across four agencies; lives in Figma, communicates in annotated frames, allergic to long Slack threads. Sees Loop Theory two days a month and re-learns the account each time. **Attitude to AI:** uses it for ideation, distrusts it near final artwork. **Winning her looks like:** every brief arrives with the brand assets, past creative and feedback history attached — no archaeology, no twenty-minute context call.

### Hannah Pryce, 35 — Client Marketing Manager *(The Summoner)*
**Client-side.** Asks questions in Slack at 5.55pm that are either nothing or everything. Doesn't know which herself — she's relaying pressure from above. Once rewrote the agency's long-form article in ChatGPT and sent it back "improved"; it wasn't, and saying so cost a week of diplomacy. **Attitude to AI:** enthusiastic consumer, no governance instincts. **Winning her looks like:** she gets answers fast enough to look good in her own building, without ever feeling managed, queued or screened.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hannah ↔ agency | Retainer, annual renewal | Good → **eroding in the gaps**: each slow response and disputed rewrite draws down pitch-day capital. Sustained by: results, responsiveness. Eroded by: silence, surprises at renewal | Her Slack, her cadence | All the power: channel, cadence and renewal are hers. The AI must moderate flow without ever appearing to gatekeep the client |
| Marcus ↔ Leila | Co-founders | Deep → **stable but untested on capacity**: each assumes the other is tracking it; neither is | Everything, constantly | The unexamined edge — shared context so total that gaps in it are invisible |
| Marcus ↔ Tom | Day rate, repeat | Warm, 3 yrs → **solid but renegotiated from scratch each workstream**: the edge has no memory either party maintains | Slack + calls | Tom's platform configs are the real media plan; Marcus can't see them |
| Marcus ↔ Jess | Employment | Good → **fraying invisibly**: her absorption is mistaken for capacity | Slack | Jess knows the client's tells; Marcus holds the authority her triage decisions need |
| Jess ↔ Hannah | None (role-to-role) | Functional → **load-bearing and unrecognised**: the account's real-time interface | Slack | Jess triages with no information; Hannah doesn't know Jess is triaging at all |
| Agency ↔ Erin | Per deliverable | Professional → **shallow by design**: re-onboarded each visit, never accumulates | Email + Figma | Context asymmetry: the account knows nothing of her, she of it |
| Agency ↔ client finance | PO / invoices | Transactional → flat | Email | Invoices vanish into a process nobody owns; chasing costs relationship capital |
| **Marcus ↔ AI** | — | Conditional, test-everything | In-product + Slack | Trusts it as a colleague precisely to the degree it admits what it doesn't know — he's watched one tool die of overpromising |
| **Jess ↔ AI** | — | Open, quick to adopt | Slack | The highest-leverage human–AI edge: triage support transforms her day first |
| **Tom ↔ AI** | — | Wary-by-position | In-product (conscripted) | Reads anything observational as a stopwatch on his day rate until proven otherwise |
| **Hannah ↔ AI** | — | Unaware it exists, ideally | Invisible | The AI must never be perceptible as a screen between her and "her" agency |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The Hannah edge is the account: every product behaviour on it must increase her sense of access while actually rationalising the flow. If she ever feels queued, the product has failed at the only edge with renewal power.
- The Marcus↔Tom and agency↔Erin edges both need the same thing — a persistent account memory neither side maintains — which is also exactly what fixes failure mode 3.
- The team↔AI edge is trust-fragile by history: one confident wrong answer replays the death of the last tool. Uncertainty display isn't a feature here; it's the adoption mechanism.
