# ACRE Persona 12 — The Cross-Border Founder Hub
### Fenn the Label — a 3-person fashion label coordinating pattern cutters, samplers and a Portuguese factory

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #4 (Family 1, hub-and-spoke); ◆ adds the cross-border, cross-timezone, cross-language edge.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Calendar-Hostage Label
**Instance:** Fenn the Label, Hackney. Founder/designer, a production-and-ops manager, a part-time e-commerce exec. £800k turnover (60% DTC, 40% wholesale), two collections a year plus drops. Profitable in good seasons, hostage to one bad one.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Everything made crosses two borders before it's sold: design in London, pattern and sample in London freelancers' studios, production in Porto, fabric from Italy via an agent. The "team" spans four companies, three countries, two languages. |
| Tooling | Adobe + spreadsheets for tech packs, WhatsApp for everything that matters, email for everything formal, a Shopify backend, and a production tracker that is a Google Sheet named "PROD SS26 REAL FINAL". |
| Who carries coordination cost | The founder and the production manager between them, with the timezone gaps doing silent damage overnight. |
| Buying behaviour | Cash-tight and calendar-driven; will adopt anything that de-risks a production deadline, in week one, on a phone. Churns tools that demand desk time she doesn't have. |
| What failure costs | Miss the wholesale delivery window and stockists cancel orders *and* trust; a fabric delay compounds through sampling into production into the season. Failure is seasonal and unforgiving — there is no "late" in fashion, only "next year". |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A founder-hub creative-production chain stretched across borders, languages and timezones, converging twice a year on delivery windows set by an industry calendar nobody controls.

**Cast:**
- **Saskia Fenn, 34** — founder/designer, the hub (Hackney)
- **Priya Nair, 31** — production & ops manager (Walthamstow)
- **June Okonkwo, 58** — freelance pattern cutter, thirty years in the trade (Tottenham)
- **Catarina Lobo, 41** — production manager at the Porto factory
- **Plus:** an East London sampling studio, an Italian fabric agent (Milan), the e-comm exec, freight forwarders, ~14 wholesale stockists

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The fashion calendar** — buying windows and delivery windows set industry-wide; the entire constellation exists to hit them
- **Customs and freight (post-Brexit)** — paperwork with veto power, fabric stuck at borders, duties discovered late
- **Fabric mills' minimums and schedules** — the Italian mill weaves when it weaves, in quantities it chooses

**Topology:** Hub-and-spoke with the longest spokes in the library: London ↔ Porto runs on WhatsApp, in second-language English, across a one-hour offset that somehow always becomes a one-day delay. The factory is itself a hub (other labels' orders queue around Fenn's) — invisible contention.

**Lifecycle (per season, ~9 months, two overlapping):**
- *Design (months 1–2):* sketches, fabric selection via the agent — commitments made against swatches and hope.
- *Pattern & sample (months 3–5):* the London loop — June's patterns, the sample studio's iterations; each round trip costs a week and the calendar doesn't pause.
- *Midpoint reset:* the sales-sample deadline collides with reality; the range gets cut 20% in one brutal afternoon, usually the week Saskia is at a trade show.
- *Production (months 6–8):* the Porto window — booked months ahead, lost if tech packs or fabric arrive late, and shared with bigger labels who outrank Fenn in any queue conflict.
- *Delivery crunch:* freight, customs, stockist windows, DTC launch — converging in two weeks of WhatsApp at all hours.
- *Trust curve:* Catarina's goodwill is the label's most valuable intangible — built across seasons of honest forecasts, spent by every late tech pack and panicked change; the *factory's* trust determines whose order slips when the queue tightens.

**Cadence:** WhatsApp continuous (London-internal, and London↔Porto in careful English), email for tech packs and POs, a weekly Porto call during production, silence from the fabric agent until there's a problem.

**Boundary objects:** The tech pack (the season's constitution — every ambiguity in it becomes a garment defect three months later), the sample (annotated physically, photographed endlessly), the production tracker sheet, the lab-dip/strike-off approvals, the size grade, the PO.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Priya — translating design intent into factory-consumable precision, chasing approvals across three countries, and reconciling the tracker with the truth. Saskia carries the decision load; the overnight gaps carry the delays.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** June holds fit knowledge no document captures ("this cloth needs 0.5cm more ease"). Catarina knows the factory's real capacity and where Fenn truly sits in the queue. The agent knows the mill's actual schedule. Saskia holds the brand's eye. Priya holds the tracker, which holds last week. The most important facts in the constellation are all one language and one timezone away from where decisions get made.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Tech-pack ambiguity surfacing as production defects months later — **eliminate**
2. Approval latency: a lab dip waiting three days in an inbox costs a production slot — **eliminate**
3. Lost-in-translation changes: a WhatsApp instruction in idiomatic English, executed differently in Porto — **eliminate** (a channel/language failure, not a competence one)
4. Queue invisibility: Fenn discovers it lost its production slot only when the date slips — **contain**: the factory's right to sequence its own floor is real; the silence about it is not
5. The range-cut panic at sales-sample deadline — **contain**: cutting is a legitimate commercial conflict (design ambition vs. cash); the panic timing is the eliminable part
6. June's "this won't grade above a 14 without re-drafting" — **preserve**: the pattern room's no is fit quality

**Conflicting interests:**
- Saskia wants late design freedom; the factory wants frozen packs early
- The factory sequences for its biggest clients; Fenn needs to matter more than its volume justifies
- The agent serves the mill's interests as much as the label's
- Stockists want exact delivery windows; DTC wants newness on a different rhythm
- Priya needs honesty about delays that everyone upstream softens

*Contain-class: the AI keeps commitments, changes and queue positions explicit and timestamped across the border — so the relationship can stay warm while the record stays cold.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap the sampling studio: a season of fit drift. Swap June and the label's fit DNA — never written down — leaves; the first re-drafted block will fit differently and customers will feel it without knowing why. Swap *the factory* (it happens: factories drop small labels) and the label nearly dies: re-onboarding production knowledge takes two seasons. Swap Saskia: there is no label. Swap Priya mid-season: the tracker's gaps become visible all at once.

**Afterlife:** Seasons are repeat games with everyone: the factory's queue position, the mill's minimums flexibility, the stockists' reorders all price in last season's behaviour. The tech-pack archive *is* the label's product memory — re-cut styles, repeat fabrics, graded blocks. Today it's a folder of PDFs and June's muscle memory; if the label ever sells, that archive is the asset.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Hold one live tech pack per style with every approval, change and translation tracked. Chase approvals across timezones automatically — the overnight gap should be where chasing happens, not where delay accumulates. Keep a single production tracker fed from WhatsApp, email and the factory's updates, in both languages. Surface the calendar maths continuously: "this lab-dip delay costs the Porto slot on the 14th." Make the label cheap to work with, so the factory's queue favours it beyond its volume.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (3+ = structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Saskia | Conductor · Creative Lead · Client Owner (to suppliers) · Sponsor | **4 — founder-hub, stretched across borders** |
| Priya | Coordinator · Production Realiser (London end) · Gatekeeper (what reaches Porto) | **3 — the overloaded translation layer** |
| June | Specialist Maker · Strategist (fit memory, uncredited) | 2 |
| Catarina | Production Realiser · Gatekeeper (the queue) | 2 |

### Saskia Fenn, 34 — Founder *(The Eye)*
**Hackney.** Saint Martins, then five years at a mid-size label, then out on her own. Designs in sketchbooks, decides on instinct, lives on WhatsApp voice notes. **Attitude to AI:** uses ChatGPT for copy and grading maths; would delegate logistics entirely if she trusted the picture. **Winning her looks like:** she sees the whole season's true state in one glance, and her 11pm voice note becomes Porto's clear morning instruction.

### Priya Nair, 31 — Production Manager *(The Translation Layer)*
**Walthamstow.** Ex-buying office at a high-street chain; bilingual in design-speak and factory-speak. The only person who has read every tech pack. **Attitude to AI:** keen and precise — wants automation of the chasing, never of the judgement. **Winning her looks like:** the tracker updates itself, approvals never sleep in inboxes, and she stops being the human SLA between two countries.

### June Okonkwo, 58 — Pattern Cutter *(The Fit Memory)*
**Tottenham.** Thirty years cutting for everyone from Savile Row diffusion to market stalls. Works on paper and a 1980s pattern hook; her blocks *are* the label's fit. **Attitude to AI:** serene indifference. **Winning her looks like:** nothing changes at her table, but her annotations get photographed, kept and honoured — and nobody re-asks what she told them in 2024.

### Catarina Lobo, 41 — Factory Production Manager *(The Queue Sovereign)*
**Porto.** Runs 40 machinists across a dozen labels' orders. Direct, professional, fonder of Fenn than its order volume merits — for now. **Attitude to AI:** pragmatic; the factory runs its own systems and will feed status to a label's tool if it reduces the WhatsApp chaos aimed at her. **Winning her looks like:** complete tech packs, on-time fabric, instant approvals — Fenn becomes the small label that's *easy*, which is the only durable queue priority money can't buy.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Saskia ↔ Catarina | POs, seasonal | Warm, 4 seasons → **goodwill-funded**: spent by late packs and panic changes; the factory's patience is finite and unpriced | WhatsApp + weekly call | **The ◆ edge: cross-border, cross-language, cross-timezone** — ambiguity compounds overnight; the queue position is invisible |
| Saskia ↔ June | Day rate, decades-style loyalty | Deep → **succession-shaped**: she will retire; the fit memory has no heir | In person, paper | Fit knowledge is oral tradition; the label's product DNA is one person's hands |
| Priya ↔ Catarina | Operational | Professional, growing → the real working edge | WhatsApp + email | Two production minds separated by a border and other people's panic |
| Saskia ↔ fabric agent | Commission | Cordial → **structurally conflicted**: he answers to the mill too | Email, slow | Information asymmetry by profession; delays surface at maximum cost |
| Label ↔ stockists | Wholesale orders | Conditional → **window-fragile**: one missed delivery cancels next season's order | Email + B2B portal | Their calendar is law; their patience is zero; they talk to each other |
| **Saskia ↔ AI** | — | Eager, phone-only | In-product | Voice-note-native input or nothing |
| **Priya ↔ AI** | — | The power user | In-product | Automate the chase, surface the maths, leave the judgement |
| **Catarina ↔ AI** | — | Willing if it reduces inbound chaos | WhatsApp/email bridge | A supplier-side window that costs her nothing and quiets her phone |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The ◆ cross-border edge needs first-class treatment: translation-aware messaging (idiom flattened, instructions confirmed back), timezone-aware chasing, and changes that arrive as structured updates, not conversational fragments.
- Queue position at the factory is the constellation's most consequential invisible fact. The product can't demand it — but by making Fenn the factory's easiest client, it *earns* it. "Cheap to work with" is a designable property.
- June's edge is an archive problem disguised as a personality: capture the annotations and decisions around her work without asking her to change one habit.
