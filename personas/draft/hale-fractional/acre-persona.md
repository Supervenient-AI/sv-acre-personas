# ACRE Persona 29 — The One-Person Constellation Set
### Hale Fractional — a portfolio CFO who is a senior node in five unrelated organisations at once

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #21 (Family 5, embedded workers); ◆ adds the one-person-many-constellations topology.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Portfolio Executive
**Instance:** Hale Fractional Ltd — Margot Hale, fractional CFO, plus a part-time virtual assistant. Five clients (a SaaS scale-up, a manufacturer, an agency, a charity, a family business), one to three days a month each, plus ad-hoc crises. Day rate that makes every wasted hour visible.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | ◆ Inverted entirely: not an organisation containing constellations, but a person *distributed across five of them* — senior in each, employed by none, context-switching as a way of life. The Halyard & Vance condition at executive altitude. |
| Tooling | Five clients' stacks (two Xeros, a Sage, a NetSuite, a spreadsheet empire), five email addresses plus her own, five Slacks/Teams, her own master spreadsheet and a notebook that is the true system of record. |
| Who carries coordination cost | Margot, alone, in the switching gaps — the cost is cognitive, unbilled and invisible to all five clients, each of whom assumes they have more of her than they do. |
| Buying behaviour | Buys for herself, instantly, if it extends her capacity (her product *is* her capacity); ruthless about anything that adds per-client setup. Each client's tooling is imposed, not chosen. |
| What failure costs | Cross-contamination is the nightmare (the wrong company's numbers in the wrong board pack); below that, a deadline missed in the switching noise (five HMRC calendars), and the slow failure: a client who feels like a timeshare rather than a CFO relationship. |

---

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** Five separate constellations sharing one critical node: a senior executive whose working memory, calendar and judgement are partitioned across unrelated organisations — where the product's memory becomes, quite literally, her working memory.

**Cast:**
- **Margot Hale, 48** — fractional CFO; the shared node (Marlow)
- **Keris Bowen, 35** — her virtual assistant, two days a week (Swansea, remote)
- **Olly Trent, 38** — founder of the SaaS scale-up (most demanding client)
- **Denise Fairburn, 56** — financial controller at the manufacturer (her hands at that client)
- **Plus, per client:** founders/MDs, bookkeepers, auditors, the charity's treasurer-trustee, banks, and each client's own supplier constellation

**External immovable nodes:**
- **Five statutory calendars** — VAT, payroll, year-ends, Companies House: the same deadlines ×5, interleaved
- **Each client's board/investor cycle** — five board packs on five rhythms, none of which consult each other
- **Banks and lenders** — covenant reporting and renewal dates per client
- **Her own capacity** — the immovable node *is the person*: 20 working days a month, five claims on each

**Topology:** ◆ A star of stars: Margot at the centre of five hub-and-spoke fragments that must never touch. Each client sees a hub (their CFO); she lives the truth (five part-time hubs sharing one head). The walls between segments are load-bearing: confidentiality, conflicts, and sanity all depend on the partitions holding.

**Lifecycle:** Five client lifecycles plus her own:
- *Per client engagement:* intense onboarding (untangle the books, win the team), steady cadence (monthly close → board pack → the day on site), occasional crises (fundraise, covenant breach, fraud scare) that annex unplanned days — and eventual graduation (client outgrows fractional and hires full-time: success that fires her, every time).
- *The monthly interleave:* five closes and five packs in the same ten working days — the recurring crunch nobody designed.
- *Midpoint pattern, per client:* around month nine the honeymoon ends — the founder's "just quickly" requests exceed the days bought; the re-scoping conversation happens or resentment compounds.
- *Her own arc:* pipeline anxiety in quiet months, capacity crisis in loud ones, and the unaskable question of what the business is worth without her (nothing — unless the method gets externalised).
- *Trust curve:* each client's trust is *personal* to Margot and built fast (senior hire credibility); spent by anything that reveals the timeshare reality — a slow reply, a detail confused with another client's. The five curves never synchronise: someone is always mid-crisis.

**Cadence:** The week is a timetable of identities: Monday = SaaS, Tuesday = manufacturer, Wednesday = her own business plus the charity… punctured constantly by cross-bleed ("quick question" from Thursday's client on Monday). Context-switching is the metabolism.

**Boundary objects:** Five board packs (the deliverable), five management-accounts closes, the cash-flow forecasts (each client's heartbeat), her master calendar (the only place all five worlds coexist), the engagement letters that define days-per-month everyone ignores.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** Entirely on Margot, at the seams — re-loading context per switch, tracking five sets of promises, deadlines and politics. Keris absorbs the schedulable fraction. The switching tax is the business's largest unmeasured cost.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Margot holds five companies' financial truths, politics and passwords — partitioned by willpower. Keris holds the calendar and the chase-list. Each client's controller/bookkeeper holds their company's operational detail. *No client knows the other four exist in any detail* — a legitimate, total information asymmetry. Her notebook holds the cross-client method (what worked at one client, anonymised into the next) that is her actual product.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Context-reload tax: every switch costs 20 minutes of "where was I with these people" — **eliminate**; ◆ the persona's defining friction
2. Cross-bleed risk: the wrong context surfacing in the wrong client's channel — **eliminate** with prejudice (partition integrity is existential)
3. Deadline interleave collisions: five calendars converging on the same week, seen too late — **eliminate**
4. Scope creep per client ("just quickly" exceeding bought days) — **contain**: founder neediness is the service's nature; the ledger of days makes it priceable
5. The capacity ceiling: growth means cloning herself, which the model forbids — **contain** structurally; the product raises the ceiling without pretending to remove it
6. Her "no" to a founder's optimistic forecast — **preserve**: the entire value of a fractional CFO is independence from the founder's reality distortion; any tool that softens her challenge devalues the product she *is*

**Conflicting interests:**
- Each client wants more of her than they bought; she has exactly 100% to sell five times
- Founders want their CFO loyal and always-on; the model is structurally non-exclusive
- Her independence (the product) vs. each founder's desire for an ally (the temptation)
- Keris needs predictability; the crisis-annexation pattern destroys it monthly

*Contain-class: the over-claim on her time is permanent and flattering; the visible days-ledger converts it from resentment into re-scoping revenue.*

**Substitution stress-test:** ◆ The inverse case: in every client's constellation, *Margot is the substitution risk*. If she's ill for a month, five organisations lose their financial brain simultaneously with no backup — her continuity plan is the question every sensible client should ask and none do. Swap Keris: the calendar and chase-list wobble for a fortnight. Swap a client's bookkeeper: that client's data quality drops and Margot's days there stop being strategic. Client graduation (the planned substitution — her replacement by a full-time hire) is the model working; the test is whether her systems hand over cleanly or reveal that the CFO function was a person, not a process.

**Afterlife:** Engagements end by graduation or outgrowth; the afterlife is referral-shaped — founders move companies and re-hire her; the VC who saw her board packs recommends her twice a year. Her cross-client method compounds privately: every engagement makes the next one sharper, but only inside her head. The unbuilt asset: a method externalised well enough to license, delegate or sell — the difference between a career and a business.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Be her working memory across the partitions: per-client context capsules ("where you left off, what you promised, what's moved since Tuesday") loaded at every switch, partition-locked by architecture so cross-bleed is impossible rather than unlikely. One master view of five statutory and board calendars, collision-flagged months out. The days-ledger per client, visible to both sides. Crisis-mode reflow (when one client annexes a week, the other four's commitments re-sequence with drafted holding messages). Capture the method: every close, pack and decision pattern accumulating into reusable templates — her practice, externalised at last. And serve one user magnificently: this is the library's purest single-human account.

### Margot Hale, 48 — Fractional CFO *(The Partitioned Mind)*
**Marlow.** Ex-FTSE-250 divisional FD who traded the politics for the portfolio. Brilliant in the room, ruthless with her calendar, quietly tired in the gaps between identities. Runs on her notebook, her master spreadsheet and a memory she no longer fully trusts at five-client scale. **Attitude to AI:** the library's most motivated single user — she *is* the coordination problem and the budget holder in one person; already uses ChatGPT heavily and feels the partition risk in her bones. **Winning her looks like:** every switch starts warm, nothing bleeds between worlds, the five calendars never ambush her, and her method starts compounding somewhere other than her head.

### Keris Bowen, 35 — Virtual Assistant *(The Schedulable Layer)*
**Swansea.** Supports three portfolio executives; mistress of the impossible diary. Sees the five worlds' shapes but not their contents (deliberately — Margot partitions her too). **Attitude to AI:** fluent; already drafts the chase emails. **Winning her looks like:** the calendar collisions surface themselves and crisis-reflow stops being a 9pm phone call.

### Olly Trent, 38 — Scale-up Founder *(The 80% Believer)*
**Shoreditch.** Series A, burning £200k a month, treats Margot as his full-time CFO who happens to be elsewhere a lot. **Attitude to AI:** maximal; will message her AI surface at midnight happily. **Winning him looks like:** answers (or honest holding replies) arrive fast enough that the timeshare never shows — and the days-ledger lets him buy more of her like a grown-up instead of stealing it.

### Denise Fairburn, 56 — Client Financial Controller *(The Hands On Site)*
**The manufacturer, Stoke.** Thirty years in the company; knows every cost code by heart. Margot's monthly day works because Denise's other twenty days feed it. **Attitude to AI:** sceptical, precise; will trust what reconciles. **Winning her looks like:** Margot arrives pre-briefed instead of spending the morning catching up, and Denise's preparation is visibly the reason.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Margot ↔ each client | Engagement letter, days/month | Personal, senior-grade → **timeshare-fragile**: any visible context-loss reprices her | Their channels ×5 | Each client sees a hub; she lives five fragments — the asymmetry *is* the product and the risk |
| Margot ↔ Keris | Contractor | High → stable | Shared calendar + WhatsApp | Keris sees shapes, not contents: partition by design |
| Margot ↔ Olly | 3 days/month | Strong → **scope-creeping** | Slack + midnight texts | Founder neediness vs. the ledger; the re-scope conversation lives here |
| Margot ↔ Denise | None (colleague-shaped) | Mutual professional → the leverage edge | Email + the monthly day | Her prep determines whether Margot's day is strategic or janitorial |
| Margot ↔ five boards/investors | Via packs | Reputation-grade → pack-quality-funded | Board meetings | Five audiences who must never receive each other's numbers |
| Client ↔ client (any pair) | **Must not exist** | — | — | ◆ The anti-edges: the partitions are the architecture |
| **Margot ↔ AI** | — | Hungry, partition-paranoid | In-product, everywhere | Working-memory-as-a-service; cross-bleed impossibility is the trust condition |
| **Keris ↔ AI** | — | Fluent | Calendar + drafts | The reflow engine's operator |
| **Olly ↔ AI** | — | Will use anything she offers | Client surface | Holding-reply quality keeps the timeshare invisible |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- ◆ The one-person-many-constellations topology makes the product *the user's mind extension* rather than a team's shared space: context capsules, partition-locked, loaded per switch. The architecture must make cross-contamination impossible, not improbable — one bleed ends trust permanently.
- The days-ledger generalises the scope problem from agencies (Veld's rounds, Loop Theory's whiplash) to a single human's hours: same pattern, purest form.
- This persona is the natural first paying user of the whole library: maximal pain, sole decision-maker, instant ROI maths — and her method-externalisation need points at the product's long game (practices becoming products).
