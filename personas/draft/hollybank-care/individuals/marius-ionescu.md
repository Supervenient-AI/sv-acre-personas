## His world
Marius delivers care in 30-minute units against needs that don't read clocks. His kettle-level knowledge — preferences, baselines, the difference between a bad day and a decline — is the early-warning system for sixty vulnerable people, captured nowhere because capturing it costs minutes that belong to Edna. Families ask for him by name; the rota can't always honour it; everyone loses a little when it can't.

## What he's trying to do
- Give good care in the time given — and steal back minutes from admin to do it
- Get his observations *counted*: spoken in seconds, structured by someone else, acted on visibly
- Train the new ones into carers who stay

## What gets in his way
- Forms designed for compliance, hostile to nuance
- The observation-to-action gap: what he notices versus what reaches the district nurse
- The maths of the job: paid in minutes, valuable in knowledge

## How he feels about AI
Phone-pragmatic: voice notes yes, forms no. Twenty seconds, hands-free, in the car — and visible action on what he reports, or why bother.

## In his own words
> “Let me say it in the car: ‘Edna's not herself, check tomorrow's lunch call.' If that becomes a flag the nurse sees, you've turned my noticing into medicine. That's all I've ever asked the paperwork to do.”

## Win him by
The twenty-second voice pipeline: observation → structure → trend → clinical edge, credited to him — the constellation's most undervalued expert finally heard at system scale.
