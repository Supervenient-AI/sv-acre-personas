# ACRE Persona 19 — The Pure Edge Business
### Linden Translations — a 9-person localisation agency dispatching work across a global freelance network

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #11 (Family 2, agency-led); flagged in the universe doc as "possibly the purest constellation business in existence: the entire company is edge management."*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Network Dispatcher
**Instance:** Linden Translations, London. Founder/MD, five project managers, a vendor manager, two in-house revisers — and a database of ~400 freelance translators across 40 language pairs, of whom ~60 are "first-call". £1.8m; software localisation, marketing transcreation, legal and technical translation.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | The company *is* its edges: no in-house production to speak of — value is created entirely by selecting, briefing, dispatching, quality-controlling and paying external specialists at speed. |
| Tooling | A TMS (translation management system), CAT tools and translation memories per client, email at volume, a vendor database whose "notes" field contains the real company. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The PMs — each running 15–30 small jobs simultaneously, every job a chain of handoffs against a deadline measured in hours. |
| Buying behaviour | Tooling-mature (TMS, CAT, MT) and economically precise: everything is priced per word, so any tool's value must survive per-word arithmetic. MT and AI have already restructured the industry once; no one here is sentimental. |
| What failure costs | Per-job: a missed deadline cascades into a client's product release. Per-relationship: quality wobbles in a language nobody in the office reads — failure is invisible until a client's in-country reviewer finds it. Structurally: AI is eating the bottom of the market; the firm lives on the layers AI can't do alone — judgement, accountability, orchestration. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A dispatch hub running dozens of simultaneous micro-constellations — each job a brief chain of translator, reviser and PM — across timezones, language pairs and per-word margins, where the hub's entire craft is edge selection and edge maintenance.

**Cast:**
- **Carmen Núñez, 47** — founder & MD (London)
- **Becca Hartree, 30** — senior project manager (London)
- **Magda Sowa, 44** — vendor manager; keeper of the network (London)
- **Yusuf Demir, 38** — freelance translator EN→TR, first-call tier (Istanbul)
- **Elise Fournier, 36** — localisation manager at a software client (Paris/London)
- **Plus:** in-house revisers, ~400 networked freelancers, clients' in-country reviewers (the hidden judges)

**External immovable nodes:**
- **Client release schedules** — software ships when it ships; the strings arrive late and the deadline doesn't move
- **The client's in-country reviewers** — employees or distributors who judge quality after delivery, with veto power and local politics attached
- **ISO 17100 / client quality agreements** — mandating two-linguist processes that set the chain's minimum shape
- **Timezones** — the network's blessing (follow-the-sun delivery) and its tax (every clarification round-trip costs a working day)

**Topology:** Hub-and-many-spokes at volume: hundreds of thin, fast, recurring edges rather than few thick ones. Each job is a transient three-node chain (PM → translator → reviser); the *network* — tiered, scored, maintained — is the persistent structure. The client edge carries continuous flow, not projects.

**Lifecycle (per job, hours-to-weeks; per client, perpetual):**
- *Dispatch:* job lands, PM scopes, picks the linguist (the decision the whole business rides on), negotiates the deadline in minutes.
- *In-flight:* queries flow back ("is 'Save' a button or an action here?"); silence is ambiguous — progress or absence, unknowable until the deadline reveals which.
- *QA & delivery:* reviser pass, DTP/engineering check, delivery; the in-country reviewer's verdict arrives days later, or never, or brutally.
- *Micro-dissolution:* the job closes; what persists is the TM (translation memory), the termbase and the linguist's updated score.
- *Midpoint equivalent:* on big projects, the terminology reckoning — mid-project discovery that two translators rendered a key term differently across 30,000 words.
- *Trust curve:* the network's trust in Linden = payment punctuality + query responsiveness + decent deadlines; Linden's trust in each linguist = a score built one job at a time and spent in one missed deadline. Both run on consistency at volume — the purest expression of edge maintenance in the library.

**Cadence:** Continuous, global, asynchronous. PMs work in bursts against other people's clocks; the Istanbul afternoon is the London morning's deadline ally.

**Boundary objects:** The translation memory and termbase (the accumulated linguistic capital of each client relationship — the real moat), the job brief/handoff file, the query sheet (where ambiguity goes to be resolved or to fester), the style guide nobody reads until after the complaint, the quality scorecard.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On the PMs, structurally and by design — the company has industrialised articulation work and sells it. The failure mode isn't that articulation is invisible (it's the product) but that it doesn't scale: every PM carries their slice in their head and their inbox.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Magda holds the network's true state — who's brilliant, who's slipping, who's secretly subcontracting. Each PM holds client preferences ("this client hates formal German") that should live in the style guide and don't. Yusuf holds domain knowledge no metadata captures. Elise holds her company's internal quality politics. The TM holds every sentence ever approved — the one asset that compounds.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Query latency across timezones: a 30-second answer taking 24 hours, twice per job — **eliminate**
2. Linguist-selection by memory and availability rather than evidence — **eliminate** (the scorecard exists; it's just not at the point of decision)
3. Terminology divergence across split jobs — **eliminate**
4. Preference evaporation: client quirks living in PM heads, not the style guide — **eliminate**
5. The in-country reviewer's late, politically loaded verdict — **contain**: their authority is legitimate and local; the product's job is getting their input *before* delivery, not arbitrating after
6. The reviser's "this is fluent but wrong for the audience" — **preserve**: the two-linguist friction is the quality system the certification mandates and the brand depends on
7. Network churn through payment or respect failures — **eliminate**: the network is the company; its maintenance is existential

**Conflicting interests:**
- Clients want native-quality everything, yesterday, at MT prices
- Freelancers want fair rates, real deadlines and answered queries; per-word economics squeeze all three
- PMs want their best linguists available; their best linguists serve five agencies
- The firm needs the TM to compound; freelancers fear the TM is their replacement being assembled from their own work

*Contain-class, with one sharpened by AI: the TM/MT tension — the network's output trains the systems that compress the network's rates — is the industry's defining conflict and must be handled with explicit, honest terms, not buried in vendor agreements.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Any single linguist substitutes in minutes — the network is built for it; quality variance is the cost, absorbed by revision. A *first-call* linguist in a rare pair (Yusuf-tier) does not substitute: his domain register would take years to rebuild, and Magda knows which 30 names are in that category. Swap a PM and their in-head client preferences vanish (failure mode 4 made flesh). Swap Magda and the network's tacit map — the real asset register — goes dark. Swap Elise and the client edge re-learns Linden from procurement spreadsheets.

**Afterlife:** Jobs die in hours; three things compound forever: the TM/termbase (client-owned linguistic capital, contested in every contract), the scorecards, and the network's goodwill. A linguist treated well for a decade answers the 11pm emergency; one stiffed on payment warns forty colleagues in a private forum. Client relationships survive PM churn only to the degree preferences were externalised — which is to say, partially.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Put the evidence at the point of dispatch: linguist scores, availability, domain history, current load — selection in seconds. Kill query latency: answer routine queries from the TM, termbase and style guide instantly; route the genuinely new ones to the client with context. Police terminology consistency across split jobs in real time. Harvest preferences into living style guides automatically. Track the network's health (payment ageing, satisfaction signals, churn risk) as a first-class dashboard. And hold the line the industry needs: linguistic assets used transparently, never silently harvested against the people who made them.

### Carmen Núñez, 47 — Founder & MD *(The Orchestrator)*
**London.** Translator turned agency owner; watched MT eat the commodity layer and repositioned the firm on judgement and accountability. **Attitude to AI:** clear-eyed veteran — AI is neither saviour nor enemy, it's the third restructuring of her industry in twenty years. **Winning her looks like:** the firm visibly sells what AI can't do alone — orchestration, accountability, the network — with margins that survive per-word maths.

### Becca Hartree, 30 — Senior PM *(The Air-Traffic Controller)*
**London.** Twenty-five live jobs across nine timezones before lunch. Inbox-native, spreadsheet-fluent, runs on adrenaline and meticulous handoff files. **Attitude to AI:** adopts anything that answers queries and chases deliveries; trusts nothing with final delivery. **Winning her looks like:** queries answer themselves from the TM, selection takes seconds, and her head stops being the style guide.

### Magda Sowa, 44 — Vendor Manager *(The Network's Memory)*
**London.** Knows four hundred people she's mostly never met: rates, strengths, family situations, which ones go quiet in August. **Attitude to AI:** protective — the network is people, not rows; scoring must never become a black box that delists a human without a conversation. **Winning her looks like:** the network's health is visible and humane — churn risk flagged early, payment issues surfaced before resentment, her judgement augmented rather than replaced.

### Yusuf Demir, 38 — Freelance Translator *(The First-Call Specialist)*
**Istanbul.** EN→TR, fintech and legal domain; serves five agencies, ranks them privately by payment speed and query responsiveness. **Attitude to AI:** uses MT and AI daily as drafting tools — his value is knowing where they're wrong; alert to agencies harvesting his TMs to replace him. **Winning him looks like:** queries answered in minutes, payment on the nail, honest terms about how his work trains what — and briefs that respect his expertise instead of his word rate.

### Elise Fournier, 36 — Client Localisation Manager *(The Continuous Client)*
**Paris/London.** Owns 14 languages for a software firm; judged on release-day quality in languages she doesn't speak. **Attitude to AI:** sophisticated — runs her own MT-evaluation pilots; respects vendors who are honest about where AI sits in their chain. **Winning her looks like:** terminology consistency she can demonstrate to in-country reviewers, transparent process, and zero release-day surprises.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Linden ↔ Elise | MSA, continuous flow | Strong → **proof-hungry**: renewed by demonstrated consistency, not relationships alone | TMS portal + email | She's accountable for quality she can't read; the firm's evidence is her armour |
| PM ↔ linguists (per job) | PO per job, framework terms | Thin, fast, recurring → **consistency-built** | Email + TMS | Hundreds of these; each one's health is a scorecard plus a payment history |
| Magda ↔ the network | Framework agreements | Deep, asymmetric → **respect-fragile**: one payment scandal travels the whole network | Email + the grapevine | She holds their livelihoods' tap; they hold the firm's entire production capacity |
| Linden ↔ in-country reviewers | None | Distant → **verdict-shaped** | Via Elise, post-delivery | The hidden judges; pre-delivery contact is the unmade fix |
| Yusuf ↔ revisers | Per job, anonymous-ish | Professional → **friction by design** | Query sheets + tracked changes | The mandated two-linguist tension; preserve-class |
| **Becca ↔ AI** | — | Pragmatic maximalist | In the TMS | Query auto-answering is the single biggest latency win in the library |
| **Magda ↔ AI** | — | Humane-scoring conditional | In-product | Network analytics with a human override, or she fights it |
| **Yusuf ↔ AI** | — | Fluent and wary | Job interface | The harvest question must be answered in writing before trust exists |
| **Elise ↔ AI** | — | Evaluating everyone's | Client portal | Wants the AI chain documented, not hidden |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- This is edge management as a *business model*: scorecards, payment health, query latency and preference capture aren't features here — they're the entire operating system. If ACRE's edge layer is real, this persona is its native habitat.
- The query loop is the highest-frequency coordination failure in the whole library (dozens daily, each cheap, collectively definitional): auto-resolution from accumulated linguistic assets is the wedge.
- The TM/harvest tension is the trust frontier: the product that handles "whose linguistic capital is this and what may train on it" honestly wins a network that has been burned before.
