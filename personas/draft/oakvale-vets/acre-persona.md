# ACRE Persona 45 — The Clinical Record That Crosses Companies
### Oakvale Veterinary Referrals — a specialist centre in a mesh of first-opinion practices, labs and insurers, with one anxious owner at the middle

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #37 (Family 10, care & clinical); not yet interview-grounded. Completes the target-universe set.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Referral-Network Node
**Instance:** Oakvale Veterinary Referrals, near Derby. A 35-person specialist centre: four specialist clinicians (orthopaedics, soft tissue, medicine, imaging), residents, nurses, a referrals coordination team, client care, admin. Receives cases from ~90 first-opinion practices across the East Midlands; sends them back with the interesting part done.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | A permanent clinical mesh across business boundaries: every case arrives from another company (the referring practice), runs through third parties (labs, imaging, out-of-hours providers), is part-paid by a fourth (the insurer), and returns — with the clinical record crossing a boundary at every step. |
| Tooling | The centre's practice management system; the referrers' ninety *different* systems; referral letters as PDFs and faxes-in-spirit; lab portals; insurer claim portals; phones for everything that matters clinically. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The referrals coordination team — intake, records-chasing, scheduling, discharge-letter flow — plus the clinicians themselves, dictating correspondence that is half care, half network maintenance. |
| Buying behaviour | Clinically conservative, corporately consolidating (the sector's quiet revolution — many referrers now belong to chains with their own preferred partners); buys what speeds intake or strengthens referrer loyalty. |
| What failure costs | Clinically: a case arriving without its history, a discharge plan not reaching the referrer before the patient's next dose — animals harmed by handoff gaps. Commercially: referrer loyalty is the entire pipeline; one practice that feels poached or ignored re-routes fifty cases a year. Emotionally: the owner — paying or claiming thousands for a family member — experiences every coordination failure as cruelty. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A standing clinical mesh in which cases (a patient, its record, its money) flow from first-opinion practices to a specialist hub and back — every transition crossing business boundaries, with insurers gating the money, labs gating the data, and an owner whose trust is distributed across parties who coordinate imperfectly about their animal.

**Cast:**
- **Miguel Santana, 47** — specialist orthopaedic surgeon & clinical director (Derby)
- **Dawn Petty, 42** — head of referrals coordination; intake's air-traffic control (Derby)
- **Laura Brignall, 34** — first-opinion vet at a four-vet practice; the referring edge (Loughborough)
- **Scott Meadows, 39** — owner of Biscuit, a five-year-old Labrador with a torn cruciate and an insurance policy Scott half-understands
- **Plus:** the other specialists, residents and nurses, ninety referring practices, external labs, the imaging providers, insurers' claims teams, out-of-hours emergency providers

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The RCVS and clinical governance** — professional conduct, clinical records standards, the referral etiquette that is half regulation, half honour code
- **The insurers** — pre-authorisations, claim assessments, direct-payment decisions: the money gate that times treatment more often than anyone admits
- **The labs and imaging providers** — results on their schedule, in their portals
- **The patient's condition** — a cruciate can wait a week; a GDV cannot; clinical urgency overrides every queue

**Topology:** A hub in a mesh of hubs: each referring practice is its own constellation (vets, nurses, clients) that periodically hands a case across the boundary; Oakvale is the shared specialist node of ninety of them — making *referrer relationship management* the commercial layer beneath the clinical one. The case itself is the travelling constellation: patient + record + owner + money, moving between companies.

**Lifecycle (per case, days–months; per referrer edge, years; per patient, a lifetime of episodes):**
- *Referral & intake:* Laura's letter (complete or not), the records chase (often the bottleneck — history scattered across systems), triage, scheduling against urgency.
- *The workup & the estimate:* diagnosis meets money — the insurer's pre-authorisation, Scott's excess, the conversation no one trained Miguel for.
- *Treatment:* surgery, hospitalisation, the owner's daily-update hunger (the Julie Cartwright vigil, with visiting hours).
- *Discharge & the return:* the discharge letter — the boundary object that returns the case; its speed and clarity *are* the referrer relationship; aftercare instructions splitting between Oakvale, Laura and Scott's kitchen.
- *The claim's afterlife:* insurer paperwork trailing weeks behind the clinical story.
- *Midpoint pattern, per case:* the complication or the finding-something-else — re-consent, re-estimate, re-authorisation; the constellation's stress test.
- *Trust curve:* Laura's trust in Oakvale is built per discharge letter (fast, clear, generous with credit) and spent by silence or poaching-smell (a centre that markets to her clients directly is dead to her). Scott's trust is built in updates and honesty about money. The insurer's trust is documentary.

**Cadence:** Intake daily, theatre lists weekly, discharge letters in the evenings (dictated, transcribed, delayed), referrer phone advice constantly (free, valuable, untracked — the goodwill economy), claims paperwork in batches.

**Boundary objects:** The referral letter (the case's passport, quality-variable), the clinical history (scattered across systems with no interoperability — the sector's defining gap), the estimate (where medicine meets money in front of a stressed owner), the pre-authorisation, **the discharge letter** (the relationship made paper), the lab results, the claim form.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Dawn's team — records-chasing, scheduling, insurer liaison, owner communication; on the clinicians' correspondence load (every case generates letters that are both clinical record and network maintenance); on Laura, returning-side, decoding specialist plans for kitchen-table delivery.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Laura holds Biscuit's lifetime context and Scott's circumstances. Miguel holds the specialist judgement and the complication's story. Dawn holds the network's folklore — which practices send complete histories, which insurers pay direct, which owners need the gentle version. Scott holds the daily reality of aftercare compliance (the plan's success lives in his kitchen). The record — the thing that should hold everything — is split across two practice management systems, a lab portal and an insurer's claim file, none of which speak.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. The records chase: cases arriving without complete history; intake stalled on another company's admin — **eliminate**; the sector's defining handoff failure
2. Discharge-letter latency: the case home before its instructions — **eliminate**; clinically dangerous and commercially corrosive at once
3. The money-treatment tangle: insurer authorisation timing distorting clinical sequencing, surprising Scott at the worst moments — **contain** the insurer's gate (it's their money); **eliminate** the surprise (estimate and authorisation status visible to Scott throughout)
4. The free-advice economy untracked: referrer phone consults — the loyalty engine — invisible and unresourced — **eliminate** the invisibility
5. Referrer-relationship drift: a practice quietly re-routing cases after one slow letter — **eliminate** the blindness (the edge's health is measurable in referral flow)
6. Miguel's "I won't operate until the weight's down" and Laura's right to disagree with the plan — **preserve**: clinical candour across the boundary is the patient's protection; etiquette must never soften it into ambiguity
7. Owner-update vacuum during hospitalisation — **eliminate** (the Julie pattern, confirmed across species)

**Conflicting interests:**
- Laura wants her case back (and her client's loyalty intact); Oakvale's aftercare instincts want to keep it — referral etiquette is the truce
- The insurer wants cost control; the clinicians want clinical freedom; Scott wants Biscuit fixed and the claim to just work
- The centre wants complete referrals; the referrers' margins want minimal admin
- Corporate consolidation wants captive referral flows; independent practices want choice

*Contain-class: the referral-etiquette economy — who owns the client, who gets the case back — is the mesh's permanent politics; the product anchors it to fast, generous, visible handoffs.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap a nurse: absorbed. Swap *Dawn*: the network folklore — ninety practices' quirks, insurers' tempers, the goodwill ledger — leaves; intake friction rises within weeks (the quirk-knowledge pattern's final confirmation). Swap *Miguel*: a specialist's caseload re-books months out; his referrer relationships (built on phone advice and good letters) partially follow him. Swap *Laura* (first-opinion churn is high): Biscuit's lifetime context thins to the written record — which is exactly why the written record's completeness matters. Swap *the insurer's stance* (policy tightening, sector-wide): every estimate conversation in the building changes overnight. ◆ Note the pattern's final form: in clinical meshes, the substitution cost is always *context about a patient* — and the patient can't re-tell it.

**Afterlife:** Cases close; patients return (the cruciate's other knee, statistically); the record's completeness at re-presentation is the previous episode's afterlife. Referrer edges compound per discharge letter for years — the centre's pipeline *is* its accumulated handoff quality. The claim file outlives the clinical story. Scott's review ("they rang us every morning") recruits the next owner; Laura's experience recruits the next fifty cases. The free-advice goodwill ledger, never written down, is the network's true balance sheet.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Run intake as a pipeline: referral received → history assembled (chased across systems automatically) → triaged → scheduled, with the records chase done by something tireless. Get the discharge letter out *before the patient*: drafted from the clinical record at discharge, clinician-approved, in the referrer's hands by the time Biscuit's in the car. Track the money lane in parallel: estimate, authorisation and claim status visible to Scott throughout — never a surprise at the desk. Log the free-advice economy and surface referrer-edge health (flow trends, letter latency) before drift becomes loss. Keep the owner informed daily during hospitalisation, warmly, at scale. And respect the mesh's etiquette: the case, the client and the credit go home to Laura — visibly, every time.

### Miguel Santana, 47 — Clinical Director & Surgeon *(The Specialist Node)*
**Derby.** Boarded surgeon, theatre three days a week, leadership in the gaps; his discharge letters are legendary when they're not late. **Attitude to AI:** clinically rigorous, dictation-native — drafting from the record is an obvious win; clinical judgement is his. **Winning him looks like:** correspondence stops eating his evenings, the history is always there at consult, and the complication call to Laura is the only phone work left.

### Dawn Petty, 42 — Head of Referrals Coordination *(The Network's Memory)*
**Derby.** Fifteen years; knows ninety practices' habits and every insurer's mood. The records chase is her team's life. **Attitude to AI:** immediate — chasing, assembling and status-keeping is the job's whole friction. **Winning her looks like:** histories assemble themselves, the folklore gets banked under her name, and intake stops being archaeology with a deadline.

### Laura Brignall, 34 — Referring Vet *(The Returning Edge)*
**Loughborough.** Four-vet independent practice; refers where her cases are treated well *and returned well*. Decodes specialist plans at kitchen tables for a living. **Attitude to AI:** pragmatic user already (notes, client comms); judges referral centres partly on how easy they are to work with — which is now a product property. **Winning her looks like:** referrals take five minutes, the letter beats the patient home, her client comes back loyal to *her* — and the centre's system never once emails her client directly.

### Scott Meadows, 39 — The Owner *(The Family's Proxy)*
**Shepshed.** Biscuit is the children's dog and the household's heart; the £6,800 estimate is the largest invoice of Scott's year and the insurance claim is a fog. **Attitude to AI:** invisible-is-fine — he wants morning updates, money clarity and his dog back. **Winning him looks like:** he always knows how Biscuit is and where the claim stands, the desk never surprises him, and the aftercare plan lands in his kitchen in words the whole family can follow.
