> “Thirty years of building and I've never once been late because of the building. It's always the sofa. Or the tiles from Italy. Or somebody changing their mind in week twenty.”

## His world
Mick's site runs on sequencing he holds in his head and loyalty he's built over decades — the plasterer answers his call on a Sunday because Mick paid him on time through two recessions. Designers' precious deliveries and clients' Saturday brainwaves are weather he works around. He's not anti-system; he's anti-*admin* — any tool that adds typing to his day has misunderstood his day.

## What he's trying to do
- Keep the job moving: trades sequenced, materials there, decisions made before they're blockers
- Work around the designer's precious deliveries without his programme dying
- Finish, get paid, get the next job from the recommendation

## What gets in his way
- Delivery schedules that are fiction by Wednesday
- Decisions that wait a week and then arrive as three contradictory texts
- “Mick said we could just—” coming back to him as a designer's grievance

## How he feels about AI
He doesn't. He feels about *mornings*: anything that makes 7am simpler is good, anything that adds a login is dead. If the schedule he's sent is finally true, he won't ask how.

## In his own words
> “Send it to the WhatsApp. If I have to download something, I'm retiring instead.”

## Win him by
A delivery schedule that's actually true, decisions that arrive before they're blockers, and a product he never knowingly meets — just a job that runs smoother and a designer who rings him less.
