# ACRE Persona 41 — The 2am Install
### Vantage Exhibits — an 18-person exhibition stand builder whose every project ends in a venue at two in the morning

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #33 (Family 9, seasonal/pulse); not yet interview-grounded.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Deadline-Convergent Fabricator
**Instance:** Vantage Exhibits, Coventry. MD, two designers, a project manager, workshop team of eight (joiners, fabricators, graphics), two site crews, ops/admin. ~70 stands a year — UK shows mostly, a growing run of Frankfurt, Barcelona and Dubai — for exhibitor clients from scale-ups to plcs.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Per stand: client marketing team + designer + workshop + graphics + logistics + venue + show organiser — a compressed Sorrel-to-Saltgate chain whose every deadline is somebody else's show opening at 9am, with the install crew absorbing all accumulated upstream slippage at 2am the night before. |
| Tooling | CAD and render tools (design), workshop drawings (print and pencil), a job-bag system per stand, email + WhatsApp, the organisers' exhibitor manuals (200-page PDFs of venue law), carnets and shipping docs for international. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The project manager across all live stands; the install crew chief at the sharp end, where every unresolved question from the previous six weeks becomes a 2am improvisation. |
| Buying behaviour | Job-costed and margin-watchful; show season (spring, autumn) leaves zero adoption bandwidth — tools get trialled in summer or never. |
| What failure costs | The show opens at 9am whether the stand is finished or not — there is no extension, ever (the Gorse/Larkspur immovable date, with forklifts). A failed install in front of the client is unrecoverable; a venue-rules breach (fire regs, rigging) can get the stand shut; international, a stuck truck at customs is a no-show at €60k. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A pulse-per-project constellation: each stand is a six-week chain — brief, design, client sign-off, fabrication, graphics, logistics — converging on a venue's build window measured in hours, governed by organiser and venue rules nobody reads until they bite, ending in a physical install where all accumulated ambiguity is paid for at 2am.

**Cast:**
- **Dean Maguire, 46** — MD; quoter, closer, occasional forklift driver (Coventry)
- **Sasha Reeve, 33** — senior designer; renders that win the job and haunt the workshop (Coventry)
- **Mitch Crowley, 51** — workshop foreman & install crew chief; the 2am sovereign (Nuneaton)
- **Elena Diaz, 36** — client: marketing manager at a medical-devices scale-up; three shows a year, her year's budget on this stand
- **Plus:** the PM, graphics, the second crew, freight partners (a Saltgate-shaped firm handles international), the show organiser, the venue's ops and H&S, the client's product and sales teams (opinions, late)

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The show opening** — 9am, absolute; the entire library's hardest deadline class, recurring seventy times a year
- **The venue and organiser rules** — build windows, rigging law, fire regs, induction badges, forklift booking slots: the exhibitor manual is statute
- **International customs and carnets** — the Saltgate problem in miniature: paperwork with veto power over trucks
- **The client's internal sign-off chain** — the CEO who sees the render in week four and wants the logo bigger

**Topology:** A job-shop hub running parallel six-week chains (5–12 live at once), each chain ending in a venue the firm doesn't control, sharing one workshop, two crews and one graphics bottleneck. Show season stacks the chains into pile-ups: three installs in one week, two countries apart.

**Lifecycle (per stand, ~6 weeks; per client, annual show calendars):**
- *Pitch & design:* the render sells a dream; the quote prices a guess; both become contracts.
- *Sign-off (the latency trap):* client approval rounds — Elena's internal chain — eating fabrication weeks that don't come back.
- *Fabrication:* the workshop builds against drawings that are 90% resolved; the missing 10% is the 2am content.
- *Midpoint reset:* the CEO render-review — late, senior, structural ("can the demo bar face the aisle?") — re-opening built elements at panic prices.
- *Logistics & the window:* trucks, carnets, venue slots; the build window opens Tuesday 8am and closes Wednesday 10pm and that is the project now.
- *The install (the 2am crunch):* everything converges — crew, freight, graphics, venue inductions, the missing 10%; Mitch improvises; the show opens; nobody remembers it was nearly a disaster except everyone.
- *Breakdown & dissolution:* three days later the stand is dismantled in four hours, half-stored, half-skipped; the job bag closes; the lessons evaporate (the pulse-amnesia pattern, per-project).
- *Trust curve:* the client's trust is made or destroyed *at the show* — the moment of maximum visibility; the workshop's trust in design ("can Sasha's curves actually be built?") and the crew's trust in everyone upstream are renegotiated every job.

**Cadence:** Quote-churn weekly, design rounds per job, workshop scheduling daily, show-season pile-ups, the install nights, and dead Augusts.

**Boundary objects:** The render (the sold dream — Studio Veld's deck with joinery consequences), the working drawings (where dream meets plywood), the job bag (the project's paper soul), the exhibitor manual (venue law, unread), the carnet (international veto paper), the snag-list-at-2am (oral tradition), the reusable stock inventory (what's in the warehouse from past stands — nobody fully knows).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On the PM across the chains; on Mitch at the convergence point — the install inherits every unresolved upstream decision; on Elena client-side, relaying her organisation's opinions into the timeline's shrinking tolerance.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Sasha holds design intent. Mitch holds buildability, the venue folklore (which halls' floors are out of true, which organisers' crews help) and the 2am improvisation craft. Dean holds the client relationships and the quote maths. Elena holds her internal politics and the show's real objective. The warehouse holds reusable stock whose inventory exists in Mitch's memory. The exhibitor manual holds the rules everyone learns at the venue door.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Sign-off latency compressing fabrication: client weeks spent, workshop weeks lost — **eliminate** the invisibility (the latency's cost computed and shown); **contain** the client's right to deliberate
2. The missing 10%: unresolved details deferred to the install — **eliminate**; every 2am improvisation was a week-three decision nobody made
3. Venue-rule ambush: induction requirements, rigging bans, forklift slots discovered on arrival — **eliminate** (the manual parsed at job-open, not at the door)
4. Show-season pile-up blindness: three installs, two crews, one graphics queue — discovered as a collision — **eliminate**
5. The CEO render-review — **contain**: late senior opinion is client physics (the Veld/Gorse ambush pattern); price it, anchor it, never absorb it silently
6. Reusable-stock amnesia: rebuilding what the warehouse already owns — **eliminate**
7. Mitch's "that cantilever won't survive the breakdown crew" — **preserve**: buildability candour at design stage is the firm's quality system
8. International paperwork fragility — **contain** via the freight partner; **eliminate** the late discovery (the Saltgate convergence pattern: docs and trucks must rendezvous)

**Conflicting interests:**
- The render wins jobs; the workshop builds margins; Sasha and Mitch fight the same fight every project, productively
- Elena wants late flexibility; the build window prices it brutally
- The organiser sells build hours; the venue sells rules; the exhibitor pays for both
- Dean quotes to win; the install absorbs what the quote missed

*Contain-class: design-ambition vs. buildability and client-latency vs. the window are the trade's permanent physics — the AI's job is making their costs visible early enough to trade deliberately.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap a fabricator: the workshop absorbs it. Swap *Sasha* mid-design: the dream's logic is in her files but not her head's reasoning; the client feels the wobble at the next round. Swap *Mitch* before an install: the firm's genuine emergency — venue folklore, buildability memory and 2am command are one man (the Mack Stennett pattern, confirmed in a second pulse business); the install runs at double risk and the client must never know. Swap *Elena* mid-job: the internal-politics map resets; sign-off latency doubles. Swap the freight partner before an international: only with a season's notice. The pattern: pulse businesses concentrate their resilience in two or three heads and call it experience.

**Afterlife:** Per stand: photos for the portfolio, stock to the warehouse (inventoried in memory), the job bag to a shelf. Per client: the show calendar repeats annually — the same stand conversation every year, currently restarted from scratch rather than resumed ("what did we learn about Frankfurt's hall 8?"). Venue folklore compounds in crew heads only. The firm's reputation travels the exhibition-industry circuit (organisers recommend builders; builders recommend freight) — the repeat-game web that fills next year's quote book.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Parse the exhibitor manual at job-open: every venue rule, slot and induction requirement converted into the job's checklist before fabrication starts. Compute and display sign-off latency cost live ("approval today = full fabrication; Friday = overtime; Monday = 2am risk"). Resolve the missing 10% by forcing the deferred-decision list into daylight in week three. See the pile-ups: crews, graphics queue and freight across all live jobs, collisions flagged at quote time. Inventory the warehouse for real. Bank the venue folklore and the per-client show history across years (the pulse-memory pattern). And brief the install crew's phones with everything — drawings, snags, venue rules, contacts — because at 2am, the job bag is in the van and the answer had better be in a pocket.

### Dean Maguire, 46 — MD *(The Quote and the Handshake)*
**Coventry.** Shopfitter turned stand-builder; quotes from experience and wins from relationships. Showtime adrenaline addict in recovery, allegedly. **Attitude to AI:** commercially curious, seasonally constrained — show him in August. **Winning him looks like:** the pile-ups surface at quote time, the venue ambushes stop, and the quote maths learns from every job bag instead of just his scar tissue.

### Sasha Reeve, 33 — Senior Designer *(The Dream Seller)*
**Coventry.** Interior-architecture trained; her renders win pitches against firms twice Vantage's size. Fights for the curve, concedes to the plywood, learns every install. **Attitude to AI:** uses render tools' AI already; wants the venue rules and buildability constraints *in* her design environment, not after it. **Winning her looks like:** constraints arrive at concept stage, sign-off rounds are tracked not absorbed, and the dream survives contact with the workshop intact.

### Mitch Crowley, 51 — Foreman & Crew Chief *(The 2am Sovereign)*
**Nuneaton.** Thirty years of installs on three continents; can level a stand on a sloping floor by eye and negotiate a forklift out of a Catalan venue crew with no shared language. The warehouse inventory and the venue folklore live behind his eyes. **Attitude to AI:** none, until the phone in his pocket answers the 2am question — then it's part of the toolkit like the laser level. **Winning him looks like:** the missing 10% list dies in week three, the crew's phones hold everything, and his folklore gets banked with his name on it before his knees retire him.

### Elena Diaz, 36 — Client Marketing Manager *(The Show-Budget Owner)*
**Client-side, Cambridge.** Three shows a year carry her annual pipeline target; the stand is her most visible purchase and her CEO's favourite opinion-surface. **Attitude to AI:** fluent, deadline-driven. **Winning her looks like:** she sees exactly what her internal delays cost before they cost it, her CEO's week-four opinion lands as a priced option not a crisis, and at 9am on show day the stand is simply, boringly, perfect.
