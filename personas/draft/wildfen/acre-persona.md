# ACRE Persona — Worked Example
## Constellation archetype: "The Founder-Hub Creative Sprint"
### Named instance: **Wildfen Oat Co.** — packaging redesign, 10-week sprint to a Sainsbury's listing deadline

> The named individuals below are illustrative. The Constellation is the persona; it survives the substitution of any of them. But the people are how it becomes real — their ages, locations, working hours and tool habits all change how collaboration software *feels*.

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Outsourced-Everything Challenger Brand
**Instance:** Wildfen Oat Co., founded 2022, based in a shared unit in Hackney Wick, London. Four employees: founder, ops manager, sales lead, marketing exec. Turnover ~£1.1m, not yet profitable. Stocked in independents and Ocado; the Sainsbury's listing is the company-maker.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Radically networked: all creative, production and specialist work crosses the company boundary. The packaging "team" is mostly not employees. |
| Tooling | Internally WhatsApp + Google Workspace + a Notion nobody updates. Externally: whatever each supplier prefers. No shared system of record — the system of record is Priya's brain. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The founder. Unbudgeted, invisible, done between 9pm and midnight. |
| Buying behaviour | Founder is champion and economic buyer in one. Bought three SaaS tools this year on a free trial at 11pm; churned from two. Value must be felt within *this* project. |
| What failure costs | A missed print deadline = a missed Sainsbury's launch window = a quarter's revenue and a bruised buyer relationship. Coordination failure is existential. |

## C — Constellation

**Name:** The Founder-Hub Creative Sprint
**Definition:** A small brand-side hub coordinating 3–6 external specialists toward a single creative deliverable with a hard external deadline.

**Cast (this instance):**
- **Priya Shah, 34** — Wildfen founder, the Hub (London)
- **Sophie Okafor, 41** — account director, Marrow & Co branding agency (Leeds)
- **Dave Hartley, 53** — production director, Calverton Print Group (Nottingham)
- **Mara Kovač, 28** — freelance illustrator (Glasgow)
- **Ben Whitford, 46** — freelance copywriter (Margate)

**Topology:** Hub-and-spoke. Nearly everything routes through Priya. Mara and Dave have never spoken; Mara won't learn Calverton's print constraints exist until her artwork bounces off preflight in week six — unless something changes.

**Cadence:** Bursty. Quiet stretches, then deadline panics. Priya's "Monday status email" is out of date by Wednesday. The biggest source of delay is decision latency at the hub: Priya is at a trade show in Amsterdam in week 3 and mid-fundraise throughout.

**Boundary objects:** The packaging mockup (composition to Mara, brand equity to Sophie, dielines and tolerances to Dave, shelf-impact to Priya); the brand guidelines (a 60-page PDF Sophie's studio made in 2023 that Mara has skimmed); the copy deck; the dieline template; and the brief itself — which exists in four subtly different remembered versions.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Priya, the least qualified translator between specialisms and the busiest person in the constellation. Secondary articulation falls on Sophie, who is quietly doing unpaid project management for freelancers she didn't choose, and it's starting to show in her tone.

**Characteristic failure modes:**
1. Version chaos at the hub (`wildfen_carton_v7_FINAL_print2.pdf`)
2. Late discovery of cross-specialism constraints (illustration ↔ dieline ↔ copy length)
3. Decision bottleneck when Priya goes quiet
4. The "two briefs" problem — Marrow & Co and the freelancers working from divergent understandings
5. Blame ambiguity when deadlines slip, straining edges that must survive into the next project

**Conflicting interests (the tension that defines the constellation):**
- Priya wants speed and certainty, and treats every revision as free
- Sophie wants scope protection and creative control; sees freelance artwork as a quality risk to "her" brand
- Dave wants frozen artwork early; Calverton's margin comes from late change orders
- Mara wants creative credit, prompt payment, and not to carry the can for specs nobody gave her
- Ben wants the strategy context he's been excluded from, and sign-off that stays signed off

**What this constellation needs from an AI-in-the-middle:** Relieve Priya. Hold a single live state of the deliverable. Surface Dave's constraints to Mara in week one, not week six. Chase decisions without anyone nagging. Make handoffs explicit. Never make the spokes feel watched.

## R — Roles

### Priya Shah, 34 — Founder, Wildfen Oat Co. *(The Brand Hub)*
**London (Hackney Wick unit; lives in Walthamstow).** Ex-brand manager at a big FMCG company; left in 2022 because she was tired of decks about decks. Works from the unit, a coffee shop, and the 38 bus. Phone-first: WhatsApp voice notes are her native format, sent at 11.40pm with "no rush!" Has 14 apps with notifications on and trusts none of them; trusts her own memory, which is now visibly failing under load. **Attitude to AI:** enthusiastic, over-trusting; already uses a chatbot to draft investor emails. Danger: she'll happily let the AI make calls the spokes expect from a human. **What winning her looks like:** she gets an evening back in week two and tells another founder about it at a trade show.

### Sophie Okafor, 41 — Account Director, Marrow & Co *(The Shadow Coordinator)*
**Leeds.** Eighteen years agency-side; has survived three rebrands of her own agency. Runs her life in Basecamp and colour-coded Google Sheets; allergic to clients' tool whims ("we are not moving platforms for a £30k account"). Professionally warm, privately keeping a tally of the unpaid PM hours she spends chasing Priya's freelancers. Working hours strictly 9–5.30 — and she notices, with an edge, that Priya doesn't respect them. **Attitude to AI:** sceptical-professional. Has seen "game-changing" tools come and go. Will test within a week whether the AI respects Marrow's process or flattens it. **What winning her looks like:** the unpaid coordination disappears from her week while the agency's ownership of the creative relationship stays visibly intact.

### Dave Hartley, 53 — Production Director, Calverton Print Group *(The Constraint-Holder)*
**Nottingham.** Thirty years in print; can tell a coated stock by touch. Email only — prints PDFs to mark them up with an actual pen, scans them back in. Replies within the hour, 7.30am–4pm, never after. Joins projects late by industry convention and is privately exasperated by it: "every job, same surprises." Speaks in tolerances, lead times and dieline gospel. **Attitude to AI:** none, until it saves him a reprint — then evangelical in his own gruff way. Note the internal contradiction: Dave-the-craftsman wants constraints surfaced early; Calverton-the-business profits when they surface late. Your champion inside this spoke is Dave, not his firm. **What winning him looks like:** the artwork passes preflight first time and nobody rings him in a panic.

### Mara Kovač, 28 — Freelance Illustrator *(The Conscripted Periphery)*
**Glasgow (Dennistoun).** Glasgow School of Art, 2019. Works in Procreate on an iPad, often 2pm to midnight; juggles four clients and a personal zine. Found by Priya via Instagram; their entire relationship lives in WhatsApp and a Drive folder called "WILDFEN ASSETS final". Has been burned before: a client once blamed her for a print failure caused by specs she was never sent, and she ate the reprint cost. Invoices politely, twice. **Attitude to AI:** wary-by-position. Her first contact with the product is an invitation she didn't ask for, from the person who controls her payment. Anything that smells like time-tracking, surveillance or unpaid admin and she'll engage exactly as much as contractually unavoidable. **What winning her looks like:** she gets the dieline and print specs in week one without asking, gives feedback by annotating visuals instead of writing paragraphs, and the product costs her less effort than the email threads it replaced — from message one.

### Ben Whitford, 46 — Freelance Copywriter *(The Information-Starved Repeat Player)*
**Margate.** Twenty years in London agencies; went freelance and coastal in 2020. Writes in Google Docs with version names like "carton copy v3 — THE GOOD ONE". The constellation's institutional memory for tone of voice — Priya relies on this and doesn't credit it. Working pattern: deep mornings, swims at lunch, light afternoons. Wry in comments; diplomatically furious when copy he wrote to a 40-word limit meets a dieline that fits 22. **Attitude to AI:** intrigued but territorial — fine with it *routing* his words, watchful for it *rewriting* them. **What winning him looks like:** he sees the strategy, the illustration-in-progress and the dieline before he writes, and sign-off, once given, stays given.

## E — Edges (selected)

| Edge | Contract | Trust | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Priya ↔ Sophie | Retainer + project fees | 2 yrs, solid but budget-strained | Email + monthly call; Priya keeps trying WhatsApp, Sophie keeps not answering it | Sophie holds brand knowledge Priya can't articulate; Priya holds the budget |
| Priya ↔ Dave | Per print run, PO-based | Second engagement, transactional | Email only | Dave knows constraints nobody asks about until too late |
| Priya ↔ Mara | Per deliverable, no formal contract | Warm but new | WhatsApp + Drive | Payment power vs. creative dependency; the informality cuts both ways |
| Priya ↔ Ben | Day rate | Repeat collaborator, 4 projects | Email + shared docs | Ben holds tone-of-voice memory Priya relies on but doesn't credit |
| Sophie ↔ Mara | **None** | Low / wary; quality-control tension | Routed via Priya | The edge most likely to fail silently — Sophie's feedback reaches Mara third-hand and softened |
| Sophie ↔ Dave | **None** | Professional but guarded | Routed via Priya | Each blames the other when artwork fails preflight |
| Mara ↔ Dave | **None** | Don't know each other exist | — | The costliest missing edge in the constellation |
| **Priya ↔ AI** | — | High, eager, over-trusting | In-product | Treats the AI as chief of staff; risk of delegated judgement calls the spokes expect from a human |
| **Mara ↔ AI** | — | Low, defensive at first contact | In-product (conscripted) | Reads the AI as the client's monitoring tool until proven otherwise; the make-or-break adoption edge |
| **Sophie ↔ AI** | — | Sceptical-professional | In-product | Testing whether it respects Marrow's process or flattens it |
| **Dave ↔ AI** | — | Indifferent until proven useful | Email bridge, ideally | Won't adopt a new interface; the AI must meet him in his inbox |

**Design implications of the edge map:**
- The three edges that *don't exist* (Sophie↔Mara, Sophie↔Dave, Mara↔Dave) are where the costliest failures originate — and they're invisible to any individual-level persona. The AI's highest-value move is to give those missing edges a safe, low-friction channel without forcing relationships the parties haven't chosen.
- The constellation spans four working-hour patterns (Priya's 11pm voice notes, Sophie's strict 9–5.30, Dave's 7.30–4, Mara's 2pm–midnight) and three native channels (WhatsApp, Basecamp/email, email-only). Any tool that demands synchronicity or a single channel has already failed four of the five people.
- Dave will never open a dashboard. If the product can't act through email for him, the constellation has a hole in it.
