## Her world
Mara's career runs on her portfolio and her reliability — and on a wariness she earned the hard way, when a client blamed her for a print failure caused by specs she was never sent. She loves the Wildfen job creatively; structurally, she's at the end of every information chain: she won't learn Calverton's print constraints exist until her artwork bounces off preflight, unless something changes.

## What she's trying to do
- Do work she's proud of, get credited for it, and get paid without chasing
- Get the technical specs *up front* — dielines, tolerances, copy lengths — not as a post-mortem
- Protect her hours: feedback by annotated visuals, not essay-length emails

## What gets in her way
- Briefs that arrive as vibes and harden into blame
- Feedback relayed third-hand through the founder, softened into uselessness
- Tools she's conscripted into by someone else's purchasing decision

## How she feels about AI
Wary-by-position. Her first contact with any client tool is an invitation she didn't ask for, from the person who controls her payment. Anything that smells like time-tracking, surveillance or unpaid admin gets exactly the engagement that's contractually unavoidable.

## In her own words
> “If it gives me the dieline in week one and costs me less effort than the email threads it replaced — from the first message — then fine. I'm listening.”

## Win her by
Making her first touch with the product *useful to her*: specs delivered before she asked, annotation-first feedback, and not a single feature that watches how long she takes.
