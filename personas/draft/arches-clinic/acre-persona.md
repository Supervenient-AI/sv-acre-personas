# ACRE Persona 43 — The Constellation Disguised as a Company
### The Arches Clinic — a multidisciplinary private clinic where the "staff" are fourteen self-employed practices sharing a brand

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #35 (Family 10, care & clinical); ◆ new territory: the deliverable is a person's wellbeing and the compliance regime is unforgiving.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Brand-Shaped Federation of Clinicians
**Instance:** The Arches Clinic, Altrincham. Owner (a physiotherapist) plus a practice manager and two receptionists employed — and fourteen *self-employed associate clinicians* renting rooms and diaries under one brand: physios, a sports physician, psychotherapists, a dietitian, podiatry, massage therapy. To patients, one clinic. Legally, fifteen businesses in a building.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | A constellation disguised as a company: every cross-disciplinary referral (physio → sports doc → psych) crosses a *business boundary* the patient can't see — separate clinical responsibilities, separate insurance, separate note systems, one shared reputation. |
| Tooling | A clinic management system (diary, billing) all associates grudgingly share; clinical notes in *per-discipline* systems (and statutory silos — the psychotherapists' notes are rightly untouchable); email and corridor conversations carrying the actual care coordination. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The practice manager (rooms, diaries, billing splits) and — invisibly, clinically — whichever clinician cares most about a shared patient this month. Nobody is *paid* to coordinate care across the associates. |
| Buying behaviour | The owner buys building-level tools; associates buy their own and resist imposition (they're not staff — the Harvest Union consent dynamic with clinical stakes). Anything touching notes triggers regulatory and insurance questions per discipline. |
| What failure costs | Clinically: a referral that falls into the gap between two self-employed diaries is a patient deteriorating un-noticed — the deliverable is a person. Regulatorily: CQC-grade confidentiality breaches, per-regulator professional exposure. Commercially: the brand absorbs every associate's failures; the associates absorb none of each other's. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** ◆ A care pathway running across legally separate micro-businesses inside one brand: referrals, shared patients and corridor handoffs crossing boundaries that confidentiality law makes real and the patient experience must make invisible — with no one contractually responsible for the whole person.

**Cast:**
- **Joel Mercer, 45** — owner, clinic director, practising physio; the brand and its busiest diary (Altrincham)
- **Kirsty Lunn, 36** — practice manager; rooms, billing splits, the front desk's general (Sale)
- **Dr Greg Onyema, 41** — sports & exercise medicine physician; associate, two days a week, the diagnostic hub of half the pathways
- **Marianne Kessler, 52** — psychotherapist; associate, statutorily separate notes, fiercely (correctly) boundaried
- **Plus:** eleven other associates, two receptionists, the patients (each a multi-clinician pathway), GPs (referrers and recipients), private medical insurers (BUPA et al.), the CQC

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The CQC and per-discipline regulators** (HCPC, GMC, BACP…) — fifteen businesses, six regulatory regimes, one inspection-visible brand
- **The insurers** — authorisation codes, session limits, fee schedules: the payment layer that shapes care pathways from outside
- **The GPs** — referral sources and clinical correspondents whose letters set and receive the clinical record's formal layer
- **Confidentiality law itself** — the psych notes *must not* flow; the physio notes *should*; the difference is statute, not preference

**Topology:** ◆ A pathway mesh inside a brand shell: patients flow across associates (referral edges), associates share infrastructure but not liability (tenancy edges), and the clinical truth about one human is distributed across systems that are legally forbidden from full merger. The reception desk is the only node that sees everyone — and it's the least clinically qualified.

**Lifecycle:** Perpetual (the Foxgrove standing-state pattern, clinical edition), pulsing per patient:
- *Per patient pathway (weeks–years):* referral in (GP, insurer, self) → assessment → the cross-associate hops (physio to Greg for imaging review; Greg to Marianne when the knee turns out to be carrying grief) → discharge or the long tail of chronic care. Every hop is a business-boundary crossing wearing a care handoff's clothes.
- *Per associate (years):* recruitment (diary maths and culture fit), establishment (their book grows under the brand), maturity (the "what does the clinic *do* for my 30%?" reckoning — the Brewbird royalty question, clinical edition), and departure — where *their* patients' continuity and *their* notes' custody get negotiated.
- *The annual compliance cycle:* CQC-readiness, insurance renewals per associate, the policies binder.
- *Trust curve:* the patient's trust is in "the clinic" — an entity that legally barely exists; each associate's trust in the others is professional-reputational, built on returned referrals and never-blamed handoffs; one associate's bad outcome spends everyone's brand.

**Cadence:** Diary-driven days, corridor handoffs between appointments (the real care coordination, unminuted), a monthly associates' meeting (attendance: variable, agenda: parking and grievances), insurer authorisation cycles per patient.

**Boundary objects:** The referral (internal — often a corridor sentence; external — a formal GP letter), the clinical notes (per-discipline, deliberately non-merged), the shared diary, the insurer authorisation, the discharge/GP letter (the pathway's formal memory), the billing split, the policies binder (CQC's reading material).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Kirsty operationally; clinically, on *no one by design* — which is the finding. Cross-associate care coordination is everyone's professional instinct and no one's contracted job; it happens in corridors and good intentions, and fails silently in diaries.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Each associate holds their slice of every shared patient. Greg holds the diagnostic overview of pathways he's touched. Marianne holds what the knee was really carrying — and may not share it. Kirsty holds the operational truth (who's actually got capacity, which associate's paperwork lags). Joel holds the brand's reputation map. *The patient is the only node who has attended every appointment* — and nobody debriefs the patient.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. The referral gap: a corridor handoff that never becomes a diary entry — a patient lost between two self-employed practices — **eliminate**; the clinical-grade coordination failure
2. Pathway blindness: no one sees the whole patient journey across associates — **eliminate** the operational blindness; **contain** the clinical-record separation (statutory, correct)
3. The unminuted corridor: real handoffs leaving no trace for the next clinician or the CQC — **eliminate** the tracelessness, preserve the corridor (it's where clinical nuance actually transfers)
4. Insurer-authorisation drag: sessions stalled on authorisation codes — **eliminate**
5. Compliance distribution: fifteen businesses' documents, one inspection — **eliminate** the assembly burden
6. Marianne's "I can't and won't share that" — **preserve**, absolutely: the confidentiality boundary is the discipline working; the product must encode it as architecture (the strongest scoped-truth case in the library — statutory, clinical, non-negotiable)
7. The "what do I pay 30% for?" reckoning — **contain**: the Brewbird value-justification cycle; the clinic's coordination layer *is* the answer, once it exists

**Conflicting interests:**
- Each associate optimises their own book; the pathway needs them to refer onward (sometimes away from their own revenue)
- Joel's brand wants standards; the associates' independence resists exactly that (Harvest Union dynamics with clinical liability)
- The insurers want fewer sessions; the clinicians want enough; the patient wants better
- Confidentiality wants walls; care coordination wants windows; statute decides, discipline by discipline

*Contain-class with one statutory absolute: the notes-separation. The product's scoped-truth architecture must treat clinical confidentiality as physics, not policy.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap a receptionist: a rough fortnight. Swap *Kirsty*: billing splits, diary politics and the compliance binder's real state — the operational federation wobbles for a quarter. Swap *Greg* (associates leave — it's the model): half the pathways lose their diagnostic hub; his patients face the continuity-vs-custody negotiation; referral patterns re-route over months. Swap *Joel*: the brand is him to the GPs and the bank; the federation might dissolve into fourteen sole traders by lease-end. The patient swaps clinicians constantly *within* their pathway — and is the only party who carries their own full history between rooms, in memory, unreliably.

**Afterlife:** Patients discharge and return (chronic care's rhythm); their pathway record — fragmented across systems — is the clinic's clinical memory and its CQC evidence. Departing associates take their book and leave their gaps. GP relationships compound per discharge letter's quality. The brand's reputation is the federation's only truly shared asset — built across thousands of pathways, spendable by any single one.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** A pathway layer above the notes layer: every shared patient's journey visible as *events and handoffs* (referred, booked, seen, discharged) across all associates — without merging a single clinical record. Corridor handoffs captured as referral events in ten seconds. Referral-gap detection: the patient who was handed off but never booked surfaces in days, not at the complaint. Insurer authorisations tracked per pathway. CQC evidence assembled continuously from fifteen businesses' compliance states. And confidentiality as architecture: per-discipline note sovereignty absolute, Marianne's wall built in concrete, the scoped-truth model at its statutory maximum.

### Joel Mercer, 45 — Owner & Clinic Director *(The Brand With a Caseload)*
**Altrincham.** Built the clinic from his own physio practice; recruited the federation one trusted clinician at a time. Sees patients five days a week and runs the business in the gaps — the player-manager pattern with clinical risk. **Attitude to AI:** keen on anything that makes the clinic feel as joined-up as it looks; instantly deferential to any associate's regulatory concern. **Winning him looks like:** pathways stop leaking, the CQC binder builds itself, and the 30% question answers itself with a coordination layer associates would now pay for.

### Kirsty Lunn, 36 — Practice Manager *(The Federation's Operations)*
**Sale.** Ex-NHS practice management; runs fifteen businesses' shared surface area on goodwill and colour-coding. **Attitude to AI:** immediate adopter — authorisation chasing and compliance assembly are her sinkholes. **Winning her looks like:** the diary politics get easier, the insurer drag automates, and inspection-readiness becomes a state rather than a fortnight.

### Dr Greg Onyema, 41 — Sports Physician *(The Diagnostic Hub)*
**Associate, two days a week.** The pathway crossroads: physios refer up, he refers across and out. His clinical overview of shared patients is the closest thing the clinic has to whole-person sight — held in his memory and his letters. **Attitude to AI:** clinically rigorous, dictation-fluent; wants the pathway view he currently reconstructs from correspondence. **Winning him looks like:** every handoff he makes lands (booked, seen, reported back), and the patient's journey is visible to him as events without anyone's notes leaking.

### Marianne Kessler, 52 — Psychotherapist *(The Necessary Wall)*
**Associate.** Twenty-five years of practice; her notes are statutorily separate and will remain so against all product roadmaps. Refers and receives with care, shares conclusions never content. **Attitude to AI:** principled scepticism — anything ambient near her room is a non-starter; a booking-and-handoff layer that *enforces* her boundaries is, grudgingly, welcome. **Winning her looks like:** the system makes her wall stronger and her handoffs smoother simultaneously — proof, if it works, that scoped truth can serve care instead of fragmenting it.
