# Dr Greg Onyema
**Sports & Exercise Medicine Physician, The Arches Clinic · 41**
*The Diagnostic Hub — from Arches, ACRE Persona 43*

> “Half the clinic's pathways cross my desk: physios refer up, I refer across and out. I'm the closest thing this building has to whole-patient sight — and I maintain it by memory and by reading my own old letters. A pathway view exists nowhere. The patient *is* the pathway view, and we don't debrief the patient.”

## His world
Greg's clinical instinct is integrative — the whole patient, across disciplines — inside a structure that legally atomises the record. His handoffs to Marianne share conclusions, never content, exactly as the rules require; his frustration is operational, not statutory: the handoff he makes in a corridor on Tuesday has no system watching whether it became an appointment by Friday.

## What he's trying to do
- Keep his pathways whole: every referral landed, every loop closed, every letter back to the GP on time
- See the journey as events — referred, booked, seen, discharged — across the federation's boundaries
- Protect the corridor consult: it's where clinical nuance actually transfers

## What gets in his way
- Referral gaps: handoffs that never become bookings, discovered at the next appointment or the complaint
- Reconstruction-by-correspondence: his own letters as the only pathway record
- Two days a week of presence against five days of patient need

## How he feels about AI
Clinically rigorous, dictation-native: letters drafted from his dictation, pathway events tracked — welcome. Anything reading clinical content across the federation's walls — never, and he'd say so at the associates' meeting with Marianne nodding.

## In his own words
> “Events, not notes. Show me referred-booked-seen-discharged across the building, and keep every discipline's record sovereign. That's the whole ask, and it's apparently radical.”

## Win him by
The pathway-events layer: his handoffs tracked to completion, his letters drafted from dictation, the walls untouched — whole-patient sight, finally systematised.
