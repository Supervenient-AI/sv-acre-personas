# ACRE Persona 4 — The Principal Designer Mesh
### Harewood Frame Architects — a 70-person practice leading a multi-firm building project under regulatory accountability

## A — Account
**Archetype:** The Regulated Lead Consultant. Harewood Frame, ~70-person architecture practice, London. Microsoft 365 + Teams (transcription increasingly used, Copilot barely), plus a sector-specific AI platform chosen because it's siloed — nothing entered leaks to the cloud, satisfying GDPR and NDAs — and trained to speak architect. There is an AI working group, a stringent approval process, and two IT staff who review every tool's security before anyone may use it. Money is tight ("cutting our cloth for the next six months"), so existing tools get sweated before new ones get bought. What failure costs: post-Grenfell, under the Building Safety Act, the practice is *liable for what gets built*. Mistakes aren't embarrassing; they're litigable.

## C — Constellation
**Definition:** A multi-firm project team — architect as lead consultant and principal designer, external engineers, contractor, client — collaborating intensively across company boundaries that legally and contractually *must not dissolve*.

**Cast:** Architect project lead, practice associate (on 2–7 projects simultaneously), external structural engineer (different firm), M&E consultant (different firm again), contractor's project manager, client's development lead. People rotate off the project for months and return; new joiners arrive mid-stream and take months to catch up despite hours of briefing.

**Topology:** Peer mesh with a designated accountable node. Information flows many-to-many in meetings and email, but responsibility — including the **golden thread** of safety decisions (what was decided, by whom, when, and whether it changed) — concentrates on the architect, who maintains that record manually, separately, on top of the actual work.

**Boundary objects:** Drawings and BIM models, the design access statement (thousands of words, weeks of writing), meeting minutes (hours-long meetings, 20 participants), the golden thread tracker, tender submissions, the brief.

**Failure modes:**
1. Decisions made verbally in meetings that never land in the golden thread
2. Returners and new joiners operating on months-old project state
3. Cross-firm constraint discovery arriving late (engineering reality meeting design intent)
4. The practice's AI cannot be shared outside its boundary — so the *project's* collective knowledge has no home that everyone in the mesh can touch
5. Business development starving: "the thing you're supposed to do when you're busiest, because in three months you won't be"

**Conflicting interests:** The practice must record everything (liability) but cannot share its tools (confidentiality); the other firms have their own equivalents of the same bind; the contractor profits from variations the designers exist to prevent; the client wants speed through a process built for accountability.

**What the constellation needs from the AI:** Project-scoped, not team-scoped. Live inside the practice's security perimeter while serving a cross-firm project: ingest minutes, emails and documents; generate the golden thread as a living record instead of a parallel chore; catch up returners and new joiners ("here's what happened while you were away, here are the five documents that matter"); draft the narrative-heavy submission text in the practice's voice for human curation — never the technical content, which must stay checked by accountable humans.

## R — Roles
**Alec Romer, 44 — Associate Director (The Power User).** Highbury. Top consumer of AI credits in the practice; built his own minute-taking agent and a tender-writing agent trained on three years of submissions; AI-drafted his last design access statement and saved weeks. Trained the model off em-dashes. Bursting with product ideas (a brochure-assembly engine, a building-regs expert); would build them himself if he could code, and knows it. Strategy is his weak spot and he knows that too — "I can only focus on one thing at a time, and the micro detail wins."
**Robbie Quinn, 36 — The In-House Champion.** Introduced every AI tool the practice uses; speaks at industry events about it; will trial anything. Your route in — and your single point of failure if he leaves.
**Sandra Iles, 51 — IT & Security Lead (The Perimeter).** Reviews everything. Not hostile, just unhurried and unimpressible. The product conversation with Sandra is about data boundaries or it doesn't happen.
**External: Tomas Verhoeven, 47 — Structural Engineer (The Peer Node).** Different firm, same project, own tools, own liability. Will join a shared project layer only if his firm's exposure is provably unchanged by it.
**Client-side: Diane Okafor, 39 — Development Lead.** Wants dates. Reads minutes only when something has gone wrong.

## E — Edges
Defining edges: **practice ↔ external firms** (dense daily collaboration across a boundary that must stay legally crisp — the AI serves the project while respecting that every firm keeps its own perimeter); **Alec ↔ Sandra** (enthusiasm metered by governance: the internal edge every feature must survive); **Alec ↔ returning staff** (the catch-up edge — months of context transferred in hours); **practice ↔ contractor** (politely adversarial; the golden thread is partly a defence along this edge).
