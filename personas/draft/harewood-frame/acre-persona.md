# ACRE Persona 4 — The Principal Designer Mesh
### Harewood Frame Architects — a 70-person practice leading a multi-firm building project under regulatory accountability

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Regulated Lead Consultant
**Instance:** Harewood Frame, ~70-person architecture practice, London. Studio culture, project-based structure, associates running 2–7 projects simultaneously.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Mesh-native: every project is a multi-firm team (engineers, contractor, client) collaborating intensively across company boundaries that legally and contractually *must not dissolve*. |
| Tooling | Microsoft 365 + Teams (transcription increasingly used, Copilot barely), plus a sector-specific AI platform chosen because it's siloed — nothing entered leaks to the cloud, satisfying GDPR and NDAs — and trained to speak architect. An AI working group, a stringent approval process, and two IT staff who review every tool before anyone may use it. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The project architect — who also carries the **golden thread** of safety decisions, manually, separately, on top of the actual work. |
| Buying behaviour | Money is tight ("cutting our cloth for the next six months"): existing tools get sweated before new ones get bought. Procurement runs through the AI working group and Sandra's security review; nothing arrives by free trial. |
| What failure costs | Post-Grenfell, under the Building Safety Act, the practice is *liable for what gets built*. Mistakes aren't embarrassing; they're litigable — and the liability outlives the project by decades. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A multi-firm project team — architect as lead consultant and principal designer, external engineers, contractor, client — collaborating intensively across company boundaries while responsibility concentrates, by law, on one node.

**Cast:**
- **Alec Romer, 44** — associate director, project lead (Highbury) — on multiple projects at once
- **Robbie Quinn, 36** — architect, the practice's in-house AI champion
- **Sandra Iles, 51** — IT & security lead (the perimeter)
- **Tomas Verhoeven, 47** — structural engineer, external firm
- An **M&E consultant** (different firm again)
- **Craig Donnelly, 48** — contractor's project manager
- **Diane Okafor, 39** — client's development lead
- Rotating practice staff who leave the project for months and return; new joiners arriving mid-stream

**External immovable nodes** (outside the working team, veto power over it):
- **The Building Safety Regulator / building control** — gateway approvals that stop the project dead until granted; the golden thread exists for their benefit
- **The planning authority** — committee calendars set immovable dates years out; conditions discharge gates construction
- **The practice's PI insurers** — invisible until a claim, shaping what may be recorded, shared and signed

**Topology:** Peer mesh with a designated accountable node. Information flows many-to-many in meetings and email, but responsibility — including the golden thread of safety decisions (what was decided, by whom, when, and whether it changed) — concentrates on the architect.

**Lifecycle:** The RIBA stages give this constellation an unusually explicit shape in time — which makes the informal dynamics underneath easier to miss:
- *Assembly (Stages 1–2):* architect and client first; engineers join as design develops. The fee and scope assumptions set here govern years of work.
- *Design (Stages 2–4):* the mesh at full density — drawing issues, design team meetings, constraint negotiation. Staff rotate off and return; each return costs months of catch-up despite hours of briefing.
- *The contractor joins late (Stage 4–5)* — by procurement convention, after the design his buildability opinions should have shaped. The variations war starts here.
- *Midpoint resets:* every gateway approval and value-engineering exercise re-opens the frame; "we agreed that in 2023" is a daily sentence, checkable only if someone minuted it.
- *Construction (Stage 5):* the mesh tilts adversarial — RFIs, site queries, variations; the golden thread becomes a defence document in real time.
- *Dissolution (Stages 6–7):* practical completion, defects, handover — but the constellation never fully dissolves: liability and the golden thread persist for decades, and the same firms will re-team on the next bid.
- *Trust curve:* presumed from professional reputations and past projects; eroded by missed drawing issues, surprise variations and anything that smells like blame-shifting; rebuilt only across projects, not within one.

**Cadence:** Weekly design team meetings (hours long, up to 20 participants), drawing issue schedules, email in volumes nobody can read, site meetings in construction. Decision latency is governed by meeting cycles: a question that misses Thursday waits a week.

**Boundary objects:** Drawings and BIM models (the same model means geometry to the architect, loads to Tomas, cost to Craig, programme to Diane), the design and access statement (thousands of words, weeks of writing), meeting minutes, the golden thread tracker, tender submissions, the brief.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Alec — chairing, minuting, chasing, sequencing across firms, while also designing. Secondary load on the practice's document controller and on whoever last touched the golden thread. The minutes *are* the articulation work, fossilised.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Alec holds the design intent and its history. Tomas holds the structural rationale — and the margins he's quietly added. Craig holds buildability and cost reality, deployed tactically. Diane holds the client's actual (vs. stated) priorities. The practice's sector AI holds three years of submissions — inside a perimeter no one else on the project may touch. The project's collective knowledge has no home that everyone in the mesh can access; each firm's slice lives behind its own wall.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged: eliminate / contain / preserve)*:
1. Decisions made verbally in meetings that never land in the golden thread — **eliminate**
2. Returners and new joiners operating on months-old project state — **eliminate**
3. Cross-firm constraint discovery arriving late (engineering reality meeting design intent) — **eliminate** the lateness; **preserve** Tomas's blunt structural "no" itself — it is the safety system working
4. Each firm's AI siloed inside its own perimeter, so the *project's* knowledge has no shared home — **eliminate** (carefully: the perimeters themselves are legitimate)
5. Business development starving — "the thing you're supposed to do when you're busiest, because in three months you won't be" — **eliminate**
6. The contractor's variations push against design intent — **contain**: a genuine commercial conflict that cannot be resolved away, only kept anchored to drawings, instructions and the contract
7. Design crit culture — partners tearing into a scheme before it goes out — **preserve**: the abrasion is the quality

**Conflicting interests:**
- The practice must record everything (liability) but cannot share its tools (confidentiality)
- The other firms have their own equivalents of the same bind
- Craig's employer profits from variations the designers exist to prevent
- Diane wants speed through a process built for accountability
- Alec wants design quality; the practice's directors want his hours on fee-earning work

*Contain-class, structurally: these are contractual and statutory positions, not misunderstandings. The AI keeps them anchored — decisions logged, variations priced against instructions, constraints stated early as specs.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Rotate a practice architect off and back (the routine case) and the project pays months of catch-up for hours of briefing — this constellation's substitution cost is *chronic, not acute*, because rotation is constant. Swap Alec and the golden thread's institutional memory fractures at exactly the point the regulator holds the practice liable for it. Swap the structural engineer's *firm* and every constraint negotiated over two years gets re-derived at current prices. Swap Diane and the client's real priorities reset to the contract's stated ones — usually a shock.

**Afterlife:** The longest afterlife in the library: the building stands for decades, the liability with it, and the golden thread is the permanent record a future regulator, insurer or court will read. The mesh re-forms constantly — the same engineers, contractors and clients re-team on the next bid, so every edge carries reputation forward (the Hollywood model with PI insurance). What gets archived is legally mandated; what gets *lost* is everything informal — why decisions were made, which firms were good to work with — which lives only in the associates' memories and leaves when they do.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Project-scoped, not team-scoped. Live inside the practice's security perimeter while serving a cross-firm project: ingest minutes, emails and documents; generate the golden thread as a living record instead of a parallel chore; catch up returners and new joiners ("here's what happened while you were away, here are the five documents that matter"); draft the narrative-heavy submission text in the practice's voice for human curation — never the technical content, which must stay checked by accountable humans.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (count of collaboration archetypes per seat; 3+ signals structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Alec | Conductor · Creative Lead · Coordinator · Gatekeeper (golden thread) | **4 — the accountable-node pathology: design, coordination and liability in one chair** |
| Robbie | Sponsor (internal champion) · Peripheral Expert | 2 |
| Sandra | Gatekeeper | 1 — but it's the gate everything passes through |
| Tomas | Specialist Maker · Gatekeeper (structural sign-off) | 2 |
| Craig | Production Realiser · Hidden Stakeholder (commercial agenda) | 2 |
| Diane | Client Owner · Sponsor | 2 |

The product's first job: take Coordinator and golden-thread Gatekeeper off Alec without taking the *accountability* off him — the law won't allow that, and he wouldn't accept it.

### Alec Romer, 44 — Associate Director *(The Power User)*
**Highbury.** Top consumer of AI credits in the practice; built his own minute-taking agent and a tender-writing agent trained on three years of submissions; AI-drafted his last design and access statement and saved weeks. Trained the model off em-dashes. Bursting with product ideas (a brochure-assembly engine, a building-regs expert); would build them himself if he could code, and knows it. Strategy is his weak spot and he knows that too — "I can only focus on one thing at a time, and the micro detail wins." **Attitude to AI:** maximalist within the perimeter. **Winning him looks like:** the golden thread assembles itself from meetings he was already in, and returners stop costing him briefing days.

### Robbie Quinn, 36 — Architect *(The In-House Champion)*
Introduced every AI tool the practice uses; speaks at industry events about it; will trial anything. Your route in — and your single point of failure if he leaves. **Attitude to AI:** evangelist with credibility to protect — he's spent internal capital on every tool he's championed. **Winning him looks like:** the product makes his next conference talk for him; losing him looks like one overpromise he has to walk back internally.

### Sandra Iles, 51 — IT & Security Lead *(The Perimeter)*
Reviews everything. Not hostile, just unhurried and unimpressible. **Attitude to AI:** a list of questions about data residency, retention and the Building Safety Act, in that order. The product conversation with Sandra is about data boundaries or it doesn't happen. **Winning her looks like:** answers she can file — and a project-scoped sharing model she can draw on a whiteboard for the partners.

### Tomas Verhoeven, 47 — Structural Engineer *(The Peer Node)*
Different firm, same project, own tools, own liability, own PI insurer. Blunt in design team meetings because bluntness is the job. **Attitude to AI:** his firm has its own equivalent conversation happening; he personally won't touch anything that could blur where his firm's advice ends and the architect's record begins. **Winning him looks like:** joining the shared project layer provably changes his firm's exposure not at all — and his early "no" lands in week one instead of at Stage 4.

### Craig Donnelly, 48 — Contractor's Project Manager *(The Commercial Realist)*
Site-based, phone-first, fluent in programme and claim. Politely adversarial by role: his margin lives in the gaps between drawing issues. **Attitude to AI:** indifferent, until the record it keeps starts settling variation disputes — at which point he'll either adopt it or fight it, depending on whose record it vindicates. **Winning him looks like:** RFIs answered in days not weeks, and instructions he can rely on without a paper-chase.

### Diane Okafor, 39 — Client Development Lead *(The Date-Keeper)*
Wants dates. Reads minutes only when something has gone wrong. Carries her own board's pressure invisibly. **Attitude to AI:** consumer-grade enthusiasm, corporate-grade impatience. **Winning her looks like:** a truthful programme view she can forward upward without translation.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Practice ↔ engineer firms | Consultant appointments | Professional, multi-project → **stable but legally crisp**: dense daily collaboration across a boundary that must never blur. Sustained by: reputational repeat game. Eroded by: anything that shifts liability ambiguously | Teams + drawing issues + DTMs | Every firm keeps its own perimeter; the project's knowledge has no shared home |
| Practice ↔ contractor (Craig) | Building contract | Politely adversarial → **deteriorates by variation**: each claim spends it. The golden thread is partly a defence along this edge | Email + site meetings | Craig profits from the gaps the designers are liable for |
| Practice ↔ Diane (client) | Appointment + fee schedule | Good → **strained by programme pressure**: she wants speed from a process built for accountability | Teams + reports | Diane's real priorities vs. the contractual brief — known only to her |
| Alec ↔ Sandra | Internal | Mutual respect → **metered**: enthusiasm vs. governance, replayed per tool | Internal review process | The internal edge every feature must survive before any external edge sees it |
| Alec ↔ returning staff | Internal | Warm → **taxed by rotation**: months of context transferred in hours, repeatedly | Briefings + minutes archaeology | The catch-up edge; chronic substitution cost made personal |
| Practice ↔ building control / BSR | Statutory | Formal → gate-shaped | Portal + formal submissions | Sets dates nobody in the mesh can move; reads the golden thread cold, years later |
| **Alec ↔ AI** | — | High, sophisticated, already building | In-product | The power-user edge: he'll extend the product if allowed, resent it if boxed in |
| **Robbie ↔ AI** | — | Champion | In-product | Adoption flows through him; so does blame for failures |
| **Sandra ↔ AI** | — | Procedural, unimpressible | Review documents | Not an adoption edge — a permission edge. Boundaries, residency, retention |
| **Tomas ↔ AI** | — | Conditionally willing | Shared project layer | Will touch only what provably leaves his firm's exposure unchanged |
| **Craig ↔ AI** | — | Indifferent → contingent on whose record it keeps | Email bridge | The AI's neutrality across the adversarial edge is its credibility |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The defining constraint is *project-scoped sharing across hard firm perimeters*: each firm's data sovereignty intact, one shared project record everyone can trust. Get this wrong in either direction and the constellation rejects it — Sandra blocks it, or Tomas's firm does.
- The practice↔contractor edge is legitimately adversarial. The AI must be the neutral record both sides cite, never an advocate; the moment it reads as the architect's tool for winning disputes, Craig's side stops feeding it.
- The catch-up edge (returners, new joiners) is the cheapest big win: it's internal, Sandra-approved territory, and Alec feels it weekly.
