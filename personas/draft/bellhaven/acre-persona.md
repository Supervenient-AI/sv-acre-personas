# ACRE Persona 7 — The Portfolio Hub
### Bellhaven Capital — a 10-person investment fund coordinating reporting across an autonomous portfolio

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Hub of Hubs
**Instance:** Bellhaven, a 10-person consumer-brands investment fund in Mayfair, sitting atop portfolio companies ranging from a 2,000-person global brand to a 27-person skincare house. Family money behind it; institutional habits in front of it.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Federated: the fund has power but not operational authority. Every important workflow crosses into companies it owns but does not run. |
| Tooling | Microsoft 365 + SharePoint (parts well-organised, the vast majority not), Zoom preference, Xero for the fund's own simple books. Copilot on every laptop, used by almost no one; paid ChatGPT licences for a couple of staff; "people prefer ChatGPT" is now quasi-policy. |
| Who carries coordination cost | Lily, the analyst, at consolidation time; Matthew, the CFO, the rest of the time. The portfolio CFOs carry the inbound burden and resent it quietly. |
| Buying behaviour | Aspirational: ran a senior-leadership AI roundtable with a big-name consultancy and everyone left asking "but what do I actually do tomorrow?" The real digital expertise lives out in the portfolio, and the fund borrows it. Security anxiety is the silent veto on everything. |
| What failure costs | A wrong number in front of the family is a credibility event; a leak of portfolio data is unthinkable; and every quarter the consolidation is late, the fund looks like what it fears being — a small office pretending to be an institution. |

---

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A small centre with power but not operational authority, coordinating standardised information flows from autonomous companies that each have their own systems, formats and opinions.

**Cast:**
- **Matthew Crane, 49** — fund CFO (Highgate)
- **Edward Vane, 58** — founder/principal; presents to the family and investors
- **Lily Okeke, 27** — analyst; does the actual consolidation
- **Nicolas Faure, 52** — CFO of the flagship 2,000-person brand (Paris/London)
- **Emma Lindqvist, 38** — CFO of the 27-person skincare house
- The other portfolio CFOs — each with a different finance stack, chart of accounts, and definition of "net sales"

**External immovable nodes** (outside the working team, veto power over it):
- **The family / LPs** — the audience for everything; their quarterly meeting is the deadline that propagates down through every portfolio company's month-end
- **The auditors** — annual, immovable, and intolerant of the filing entropy everyone else has learned to live with
- **The lending banks** — covenant tests and facility agreements (150 pages, one number needed at a time) with dates set in legal stone

**Topology:** Hub-of-hubs. Each portfolio company is its own constellation; the fund's constellation is made of the *edges to those hubs*. Data flows inward monthly in incompatible formats; questions flow outward repetitively ("why is this number up?" — asked of the same line, by the same people, every quarter).

**Lifecycle:** Perpetual, pulsing on nested financial calendars:
- *Monthly:* management accounts arrive (late, in five formats); Lily aligns them; questions bounce outward; the pack lands.
- *Quarterly:* the investor cycle — consolidation plus narrative, the fund's whole credibility compressed into one meeting with the family.
- *Annually:* audit season and budget season, when the filing entropy and definitional drift get paid for in overtime.
- *Per deal:* an acquisition adds a new hub with new quirks and a new CFO to socialise; an exit triggers the diligence reckoning, where years of hygiene debt come due in a data room.
- *Trust curve:* with portfolio CFOs, trust is *eroded by repetition* — every re-asked question and re-litigated definition signals the centre wasn't listening; with the family, trust is spent only in big visible moments, which is why everything bends toward the quarterly meeting.

**Cadence:** Monthly data pulse inward, weekly five-minute questions in both directions, quarterly performance theatre, constant low-grade chasing. Zoom-first, email-heavy, SharePoint as landfill.

**Boundary objects:** The reporting template (a unified, investor-level format that has been on someone's annual goals for three years and remains unbuilt — warehousing costs above the line at one company, below gross margin at another), the monthly management accounts, the LP/family presentation, the 150-page facility agreement someone needs one number out of.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Lily — the alignment of five incompatible realities into one spreadsheet is articulation work wearing an analyst's job title. On Matthew at the judgment layer: what to chase, what to let slide, what the family actually needs to see.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Lily holds every company's quirks by heart, because the quirks live nowhere else. Matthew holds the facility agreements' contents — approximately — and the political map of which CFO will tolerate what. Nicolas and Emma each hold their company's real story behind the submitted numbers. Edward holds the family's expectations, unwritten. SharePoint holds everything and retrieves nothing. The fund's institutional memory is two people and a folder structure nobody else can navigate.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged: eliminate / contain / preserve)*:
1. Garbage-in alignment: "net sales" means five different things; consolidation is re-litigated every cycle — **eliminate**
2. The five-minute tax: "can you confirm the interest rate on the facility" = 30 minutes inside a 150-page PDF, several times a week — **eliminate**
3. Filing entropy: every person has a private mental filing system; documents exist in five places, none of them the one you'd check — **eliminate**
4. AI distribution failure: a good ChatGPT answer on someone's phone has no route to the team — **eliminate**
5. Hygiene debt: the fixed-asset register, the triplicate supplier contacts in Xero — perpetually deferred, perpetually corrosive — **eliminate**
6. The standardisation war: the fund's template vs. each CFO's autonomy — **contain**: a genuine governance conflict; mandates don't work on companies you own but don't run, so the tension must be managed through cost-reduction, not authority
7. Nicolas's blunt challenges to the fund's questions — **preserve** (anchored): his pushback is often the only signal that a metric is badly defined; soften the delivery, never the content
8. Matthew's scepticism about half-right answers — **preserve**: on tax and covenants, half-right is worthless; his verification instinct is the fund's quality bar

**Conflicting interests:**
- The fund wants standardisation; each portfolio CFO wants autonomy and a minimal reporting burden
- Edward wants a clean story for investors; the CFOs want their messy reality represented fairly
- Everyone wants someone *else* to maintain the template
- And the deepest tension: the most valuable data is precisely the data the fund is least willing to feed into any third-party AI

*Contain-class throughout — this constellation runs on influence, not authority, and the AI must make compliance cheaper than resistance.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Lily leaving is the catastrophic case dressed as a junior departure: the quirk-knowledge that makes consolidation possible exists only in her head, so her notice period is the fund's deadline to finally write it down — and history says it won't. A portfolio CFO swap (routine — it happens somewhere in the portfolio most years) resets that company's reporting quality and re-opens every settled definition. Matthew's seat transfers slowly and politically; Edward's doesn't transfer at all — the family relationship *is* him.

**Afterlife:** There is no dissolution — the constellation is perpetual — but there are exits, and an exit is where the afterlife gets priced: every definitional inconsistency and filing-entropy sin resurfaces in the data room, with money attached. Edges persist across deals: a portfolio CFO who felt steamrollered by the centre tells the next founder the fund courts; one who felt helped becomes a reference. The unbuilt template is, in effect, deferred afterlife cost — paid at every diligence, every audit, every CFO transition.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Be the interrogating intermediary the template never had: collect each company's numbers in their own format, *ask the clarifying questions* ("you told me last month warehousing sat below gross margin — still true?"), and consolidate into the investor view. Answer the five-minute questions from the document estate with citations. Maintain the hygiene nobody gets to. Distribute insight: an answer found by one person becomes available to the team. All of it inside a security posture a sceptical CFO can defend to himself at 11pm.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (count of collaboration archetypes per seat; 3+ signals structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Matthew | Conductor · Coordinator · Strategist · Gatekeeper (what reaches the family) | **4 — the centre's overload: judgment, chasing and presentation in one chair** |
| Lily | Production Realiser · Coordinator (de facto) · Specialist Maker (the consolidation) | **3 — overload hidden under a junior title** |
| Edward | Sponsor · Client Owner (the family's proxy) | 2 |
| Nicolas | Peripheral Expert · Gatekeeper (his company's data) | 2 |
| Emma | Peripheral Expert · Gatekeeper | 2 — with no capacity behind either |

Two overloaded seats at the hub; the product's first job is to absorb the Coordinator load at both levels — Matthew's chasing and Lily's aligning.

### Matthew Crane, 49 — Fund CFO *(The Defensible Sceptic)*
**Highgate.** Rates his own AI enthusiasm a firm 3/5: dazzled once by a flawless French-CV translation, burned once by half-right cross-border tax advice — and on tax, half-right is worthless, so the specialist gets the call and AI gets a list of better questions to ask them. Uses free Claude for life admin and ChatGPT for work, and tells nobody the real reason he won't connect either to SharePoint: the nagging voice that says *don't put the portfolio numbers in the chatbot*. **Attitude to AI:** sceptical in exactly the way that converts hardest and evangelises loudest afterwards. **Winning him looks like:** provenance, citations, and a deletion policy he can recite — then the five-minute questions answering themselves with page references.

### Edward Vane, 58 — Founder & Principal *(The Storyteller)*
**Mayfair/Wiltshire.** Built the fund on the family's trust and thirty years of consumer-brands instinct. Doesn't open SharePoint; opens the quarterly pack the night before and phones Matthew with questions. **Attitude to AI:** none personally, decisive institutionally — one bad number in front of the family and the whole experiment ends. **Winning him looks like:** the pack is right, early, and the story holds up under the family's questions.

### Lily Okeke, 27 — Analyst *(The Aligner)*
**Clapham.** Does the actual consolidation; knows every company's quirks by heart because the quirks live nowhere else. The template's failure is her overtime. Privately fluent with AI; nothing in her workflow lets her use it on the data that eats her week. **Attitude to AI:** ready yesterday. **Winning her looks like:** the quirks get encoded once — by her, credited — and month-end stops being archaeology.

### Nicolas Faure, 52 — CFO, Flagship Brand *(The Blunt Instrument)*
**Paris/London.** Famously low-EQ emails; privately runs an AI critique of his own drafts "from the recipient's perspective" before sending. Resists every reporting change as a tax on his team. **Attitude to AI:** quietly sophisticated, publicly dismissive. **Winning him looks like:** the fund's requests arrive pre-aligned to his chart of accounts, cost his team nothing, and stop repeating themselves.

### Emma Lindqvist, 38 — CFO, 27-Person Brand *(The Stretched)*
**Hove.** No digital team, no slack in the week; will comply with any reporting demand that costs under an hour and silently sandbag anything above it. **Attitude to AI:** would love to care; has no afternoon free to start. **Winning her looks like:** the monthly submission becomes a 20-minute confirm-and-send — the AI asks her the clarifying questions instead of the fund asking her twice.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fund ↔ portfolio CFOs | Shareholder rights, board seats | Professional → **eroding by repetition**: every re-asked question and re-litigated definition spends it. Sustained by: requests that respect their time. Eroded by: mandates, repetition, surprise | Email + Zoom + monthly packs | Influence without authority — compliance must be cheaper than resistance |
| Matthew ↔ Lily | Employment | High → **dependent**: he relies on quirk-knowledge he can't see and hasn't priced | Office + spreadsheets | She holds the operational truth; he holds the judgment and the credit |
| Matthew ↔ Edward | Partnership of long standing | Deep → stable | Phone, the night before | Edward consumes conclusions only; all uncertainty must be resolved before it reaches him |
| Edward ↔ the family | Blood and history | Total → **brittle in one direction**: decades to build, one bad meeting to dent | The quarterly meeting | The edge every other edge ultimately serves |
| Matthew ↔ Nicolas | Governance | Wary respect → **stable, spiky** | Blunt emails | Nicolas's pushback carries real signal under bad manners |
| Matthew ↔ Emma | Governance | Warm, guilty → **sandbagging in silence** | Email | Her non-compliance is capacity, not attitude; only cost-reduction fixes it |
| Fund ↔ auditors / banks | Engagement letters, facilities | Formal → calendar-driven | Letters, portals, PDFs | The 150-page documents with one needed number live on this edge |
| **Matthew ↔ AI** | — | Sceptical 3/5, privately tempted | In-product | The SharePoint connection is the trust event; provenance and deletion policy are the price |
| **Lily ↔ AI** | — | Eager, unprovisioned | In-product | The fastest win in the building, blocked only by tooling she isn't given |
| **Nicolas ↔ AI** | — | Secret user | His own tools | Already uses AI on the relationship layer; would use a governed version if nobody made a fuss |
| **Emma ↔ AI** | — | No bandwidth to adopt anything | Email bridge | Must arrive as less work on day one, or not at all |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The fund↔CFO edges are the product thesis in miniature: the AI succeeds by making the *spoke's* life easier as the means of making the hub's data better. Design for Emma's twenty minutes and Nicolas's chart of accounts, and the consolidation fixes itself.
- The Matthew↔AI edge gates everything: until the SharePoint question ("where does it go?") has an answer he can recite, the document estate stays closed. Security posture is the onboarding flow here.
- The Lily↔AI edge is the immediate beachhead — maximal pain, minimal politics — and encoding her quirk-knowledge converts the fund's biggest key-person risk into its first shared asset.
