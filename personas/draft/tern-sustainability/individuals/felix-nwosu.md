## His world
Felix's quarter lives or dies on strangers' goodwill: packaging managers in Wakefield deciding whether his questionnaire is worth a cold cup of tea. He's empiricised everything empiricisable — send times, question order, reminder cadence — and still spends most of his week hand-normalising the responses that do come back. The coverage reckoning (60% by spend; estimate the rest, defensibly) is his recurring judgement call.

## What he's trying to do
- Break 80% coverage by spend — the number nobody in the industry hits
- Automate the dialect-normalisation that eats his weeks
- Turn his folklore into a playbook before it's just his memory

## What gets in his way
- The outer ring's structural indifference (they owe nothing; he knows it; he designs around it)
- Steph's finite internal capital funding every chase
- Last year's answers, re-requested from scratch, because the system forgot

## How he feels about AI
Day-one maximalist: normalisation, chase automation, response prediction — his entire bottleneck in three features. He'll co-design the campaign engine if anyone lets him.

## In his own words
> “Pre-fill from last year, ask in their units, return them a benchmark. ‘Confirm or correct' beats ‘complete from scratch' by thirty points of response rate. I've tested it. By hand. Please stop making me do it by hand.”

## Win him by
The campaign engine: cross-cycle memory, automatic normalisation, folklore encoded — his field operation, finally given infrastructure.
