# ACRE Persona 6 — The Embedded Consultancy
### Northbeam Advisory — consultants living inside their enterprise clients' environments

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Two-Laptop Consultancy
**Instance:** Northbeam, ~45-person transformation consultancy, organised in client-aligned pillars so siloed that the team serving one enterprise never needs to speak to the team serving another. Fully distributed — client offices, WeWorks, full-remote, Paris; one team day a month at a WeWork to remember each other's faces.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Embedded: consultants spend 1–2 days a week physically at client sites, carrying two laptops — their own and the client-issued one, each opening onto a different world (the client's Teams/stack vs Northbeam's own M365). |
| Tooling | M365 internally; whatever each client mandates externally; a whiteboard platform whose audit revealed 100+ licences from workshop guests added and never removed — and at least one case of clients being able to see the firm's *entire* whiteboard estate. |
| Who carries coordination cost | Each pod, invisibly, inside someone else's perimeter; and now Katie, who owns the governance cleanup. |
| Buying behaviour | Mid-ISO-accreditation (including the new AI management standard), so every tool now passes a governance screen that didn't exist a year ago. Commercially sharp: "how does this fit what we already have, and what does it cost at scale?" Token anxiety is real. |
| What failure costs | Cross-client leakage is the extinction event: a firm whose clients are competitors-adjacent does not survive appearing in a leakage incident report. Below that: knowledge that doesn't compound, and premium rates eroded by workslop. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A consultant pod embedded inside an enterprise client's environment — working in the client's tools, on the client's laptop, under the client's governance — while the consultancy tries to retain its own institutional knowledge and methods.

**Cast:**
- **Katie Marsh, 42** — engagement & ops lead, owner of the governance cleanup (London)
- **Raj Patel, 35** — senior consultant, embedded 2 days/week at a banking client (Reading)
- **Fiona Drummond, 51** — Northbeam partner, pillar owner
- **Helen Voss, 44** — the client's programme owner (client-side)
- **The client's IT/security function** — controls the environment the consultants work in
- **Workshop participants** — who flow through shared tools in dozens, leaving licences behind like sediment

**External immovable nodes** (outside the working team, veto power over it):
- **Client IT/security** — not hostile, simply sovereign: issues the laptop, grants the access, sets the rules; the product enters this constellation on their terms or not at all
- **The ISO auditors** (including the AI management standard) — periodic, evidence-hungry, and the reason the licence whiteboard happened
- **The client's procurement and MSA terms** — defining what data may live where, signed years before anyone in the pod arrived

**Topology:** Pods embedded in foreign territory. Each pod lives mostly inside its client's perimeter; the consultancy's own knowledge layer is wherever fragments escape back to — which is the problem.

**Lifecycle:**
- *Mobilisation (weeks 0–4):* access requests, laptop issuance, induction — weeks of friction before any value flows; every account created here is a liability nobody will remember to close.
- *Delivery:* workshop pulses — each one adding guests to shared tools, each guest a permanent licence in waiting.
- *Midpoint:* the re-scope. Transformation programmes reliably revise their frame mid-way; the pod's continuity through the pivot is its main defence of margin.
- *Closedown:* deliverables handed over, access *supposed* to be revoked, knowledge *supposed* to return to the firm. In practice: the access lingers (audit finds it years later) and the knowledge stays in the client's tenant, where the consultant loses it the day the engagement ends.
- *Trust curve:* presumed from the firm's reputation at signing; rebuilt person-by-person inside the client's building ("consultant" is a suspicious word on day one); spent by any visible internal confusion — and catastrophically by any data incident.

**Cadence:** Client-rhythm dominated: their standups, their steering committees, their Teams. The firm's own cadence (pipeline, pillar reviews, the monthly WeWork day) runs underneath, perpetually second.

**Boundary objects:** The workshop board (the single biggest leak surface), the deliverable deck (where workslop erodes premium rates), the client's own systems of record, the access list (now a compliance artefact), and the consultancy's methods/IP — which exists to be reused across clients but keeps getting trapped inside individual client environments.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** Inside each pod, on the engagement lead — running the client's programme rhythm and the firm's reporting in parallel. Firm-wide, on Katie, who is retrofitting an access lifecycle onto years of accumulated entropy.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Raj's best thinking lives in a client tenant he'll lose access to. The firm's methods nominally live in SharePoint; actually they live in whoever ran the last similar engagement — and pillar siloing means nobody knows who that was. Katie holds the only map of who-has-access-to-what, because she built it by hand for the audit. Helen holds the client's political reality, which no deliverable ever states. The two-laptop boundary runs through the middle of every consultant's working memory.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged: eliminate / contain / preserve)*:
1. Access sprawl: every workshop adds guests; nobody removes them; governance discovers it years later at audit — **eliminate**
2. Cross-client leakage — the nightmare scenario — **eliminate** (and prove it eliminated: the proof is itself the ISO evidence)
3. Knowledge fragmentation: what a consultant learns inside Client A's Teams never reaches the firm's collective memory — **eliminate**
4. Workslop and brain-fry: "there's nothing worse than being presented the obviously-AI set of slides" — quality erosion at exactly the layer clients pay premium rates for — **eliminate** the slop; **preserve** the senior review bar that catches it
5. Tool divergence: the firm's preferred AI tooling isn't accessible inside every client environment, so workflows can't standardise — **contain**: client sovereignty is legitimate; the firm adapts per perimeter, visibly
6. Client IT sovereignty vs. the firm's need for knowledge to compound across clients — **contain**: the central structural conflict; it cannot be resolved, only managed through an architecture both sides can audit

**Conflicting interests:**
- Client IT wants the consultants fully inside its perimeter
- The consultancy needs knowledge to compound *across* clients
- Individual consultants optimise for whichever laptop is in front of them
- Governance wants a single source of truth that the embedded reality keeps shredding
- Fiona wants utilisation high, which means consultants embedded, which means the other three problems

*Contain-class at the core: the perimeter conflict is the business model. The AI's job is an auditable line, not a workaround.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Rotate Raj off the banking engagement and his eighteen months of context stays behind in a tenant Northbeam can't enter — the replacement starts from the deliverables alone, and the client pays premium rates to watch the firm re-learn itself. Lose Katie and the access map — the only one in existence — stops updating the day she leaves; the next audit starts from zero. Lose Helen client-side and the engagement's political cover vanishes mid-programme: re-scope risk spikes. The pattern: substitution costs here are *knowledge-custody* costs, and they all stem from memory living in the wrong perimeter.

**Afterlife:** Engagements end; the firm's afterlife assets should be enriched methods, a reference, and a re-engagement path (alumni of the programme move companies and buy again — the strongest pipeline source). What actually survives today: deliverables (client's), lingering access (liability), and whatever the pod members personally remember. The ISO audit trail is the new, mandatory afterlife: every engagement must now leave evidence of access opened *and closed*. The firm that masters its own dissolution discipline gets to sell it.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Be the consultancy's own continuous layer that travels with the consultant across environments — a single source of truth on the firm's side, *piped into* each client environment (their Teams, their Salesforce) rather than replacing it. Manage the access lifecycle: who was added to what, for which workshop, and when they should be removed. Hold the firm's methods as reusable workflows. Keep an auditable line between client-confidential context and firm knowledge — which is also exactly what the ISO auditors want to see.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (count of collaboration archetypes per seat; 3+ signals structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Katie | Coordinator · Gatekeeper (governance) · Strategist | **3 — overload: the ops conscience carrying a firm-shaped cleanup** |
| Raj | Specialist Maker · Newcomer (per engagement, perpetually) · Peripheral Expert (to the client) | **3 — the embedded-consultant condition itself** |
| Fiona | Sponsor · Strategist | 2 |
| Helen (client) | Client Owner · Coordinator (client-side) | 2 |
| Client IT | Gatekeeper | 1 — sovereign |

Two seats at structural overload, for opposite reasons: Katie holds too much of the firm, Raj holds too little of it.

### Katie Marsh, 42 — Engagement & Ops Lead *(The Governance Conscience)*
**London.** Ex-startup operator; the person who discovered the 100 licences and now owns the cleanup. Has used vibe-coding tools for landing pages; fluent enough to be dangerous, senior enough to think commercially. Asks of any product: how does this fit the tools we already have, and what does it cost at scale — token anxiety is real. **Attitude to AI:** capable enthusiast with a procurement officer's soul. **Winning her looks like:** the access lifecycle runs itself, the audit evidence generates itself, and the unit economics survive her spreadsheet.

### Raj Patel, 35 — Senior Consultant *(The Two-Laptop Life)*
**Reading.** Tuesdays and Wednesdays at a banking client's office on their hardware, rest of the week everywhere. His best thinking is trapped in a client tenant he'll lose access to when the engagement ends. Quietly resents rebuilding his own working memory every time he switches machines. **Attitude to AI:** heavy personal user on his own laptop; conservative on the client's, where the rules aren't his. **Winning him looks like:** his context follows *him* — one continuous memory across both machines, without ever smuggling a byte across the boundary.

### Fiona Drummond, 51 — Partner *(The Pillar Owner)*
Cares about utilisation, quality, and never appearing in a leakage incident report. Holds the client relationships that predate every engagement. **Attitude to AI:** outcome-only — will sponsor anything that demonstrably reduces risk or raises margin, and read none of the documentation. **Winning her looks like:** a quality bar that catches workslop before clients see it, and a risk posture she can describe to a client's general counsel in one sentence.

### Helen Voss, 44 — Client Programme Owner *(The Sponsor in the Building)*
**Client-side.** Owns the transformation internally; spends her credibility every time the consultants stumble. Wants the pod to feel like *her* team while her IT department treats them as visitors. **Attitude to AI:** governed by her employer's policy, which she didn't write and half knows. **Winning her looks like:** the pod arrives pre-briefed, stays continuous through rotation, and never creates an IT ticket she has to explain upward.

### Client IT/Security *(The Sovereign)*
Not a person; a perimeter with a service desk. **Attitude to AI:** policy-shaped; vendor-due-diligence questionnaires are its love language. **Winning it looks like:** the product fits inside the client's tenancy rules so cleanly that approving it is easier than arguing.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Consultant ↔ client environment | MSA + NDAs + issued hardware | Guest-status → **earned per person, per engagement**: presumed competence, suspected loyalty. Sustained by: visible discipline. Eroded by: any boundary fumble | The client's everything | The consultant has two identities; the AI must follow the person across the boundary without smuggling data across it |
| Pod ↔ firm knowledge base | Internal | Weak → **the broken edge the product exists to repair**: knowledge flows in, almost nothing flows back | SharePoint, theoretically | Pillar silos mean even the *firm* doesn't know what the firm knows |
| Firm ↔ workshop guests | None — invitations | Momentary → **outlives its purpose by years**: high-volume, short-lived access with no lifecycle | Shared tools | An edge type most software doesn't model at all; here it's the top audit finding |
| Katie ↔ auditors | Accreditation | Formal → annual, evidence-based | Documentation | Governance as a feature: the edge map *is* the compliance evidence |
| Fiona ↔ Helen | Commercial relationship | Long, personal → the real contract | Calls + dinners | Predates and outlasts every engagement; invisible to the pod it shelters |
| Raj ↔ Helen | Engagement | Good → **rebuilt at every rotation** | Client Teams | Continuity through rotation is what the client thinks it's paying for |
| **Katie ↔ AI** | — | Willing, costed | In-product | Adoption hinges on lifecycle automation plus unit economics |
| **Raj ↔ AI** | — | Eager on his laptop, cautious on theirs | In-product, boundary-aware | The defining human–AI edge: one memory, two perimeters, zero leakage |
| **Client IT ↔ AI** | — | Questionnaire-first | Due-diligence process | A permission edge, not an adoption edge; design for the questionnaire |
| **Fiona ↔ AI** | — | Outcome-contingent | Reports she skims | Sponsorship arrives via risk reduction, not features |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The consultant↔environment edge sets the architecture: a personal/firm memory layer that travels with the human and provably never crosses the client perimeter. "Provably" is the feature — it serves Raj, Katie, the auditors and client IT simultaneously.
- The workshop-guest edge demands access-lifecycle as a first-class object: granted-for-what, expires-when, removed-by-whom. No incumbent does this; the audit pain is fresh; it's the wedge.
- The pod↔firm edge is the compounding-value story Fiona will pay for: every engagement should make the next one smarter, across pillar silos, without moving a single client-confidential fact.
