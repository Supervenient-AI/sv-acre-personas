# ACRE Persona 20 — The Broken Relay
### Hartwell & Co Conveyancing — a 12-person firm inside the UK's most famously dysfunctional chain

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #12 (Family 3, relay chains); ◆ adds the pure chain topology — no party owns the whole sequence.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Chain-Bound Practice
**Instance:** Hartwell & Co, Nottingham. Two partners, six fee-earners (conveyancers and legal executives), four assistants. ~2,000 transactions a year, fixed fees compressed by comparison sites, every file a node in a chain of strangers.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Maximal and involuntary: every transaction requires synchronised action from a buyer, a seller, two solicitors, two agents, a lender, a surveyor, search providers — none of whom chose each other, employed by no one in common. |
| Tooling | A legacy case management system (the firm's third), the Land Registry and HMRC portals, lender portals (one per lender, all different), email at industrial volume, the telephone, and fax-shaped processes wearing email's clothes. |
| Who carries coordination cost | Everyone, redundantly: each party chases every other party, duplicating the same status questions up and down the chain. The assistants spend half their day answering "any update?" with "we're waiting on the other side." |
| Buying behaviour | Margin-starved and burned by case-management migrations; buys only what provably reduces calls or risk. Regulated (CLC/SRA), insured, and accordingly conservative. |
| What failure costs | A collapsed chain: months of work unpaid (fixed fee, no completion no fee in many cases), a family's plans wrecked, and nobody at fault in a way anyone can act on. Daily: the five-call afternoon that produces zero progress. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A relay chain in which value (a house, a mortgage, a family's move) passes through a fixed sequence of parties, each handoff a potential stall, with *other whole chains* stacked invisibly behind both ends — and no party, anywhere, who can see or steer the whole.

**Cast:**
- **Gillian Hartwell, 52** — senior partner & conveyancer (Nottingham)
- **Kyle Sutton, 26** — conveyancing assistant; answerer of "any update?" (Nottingham)
- **Amy Whelan, 31** — the buyer; first-time, anxious, refreshing her email hourly
- **Ricky Doyle, 34** — estate agent; commission-hungry accelerant (Nottingham)
- **Plus:** the seller and their solicitor (the "other side"), the lender, the surveyor, search providers, and the chains stacked above and below

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The lender** — mortgage offers with expiry dates, valuation quirks and portal-shaped communication; can re-underwrite at any wobble
- **HMRC (SDLT) and the Land Registry** — statutory steps with their own clocks; the Registry's backlog is measured in months
- **Search providers and the local authority** — searches arrive when they arrive; some councils take six weeks and apologise for none of them
- **The rest of the chain** — transactions stacked behind both ends, each with its own cast, any of which can stall or kill this one

**Topology:** ◆ The pure relay chain: A → B → C → D, where each link sees only its neighbours. Hartwell can see its client, the other side's solicitor, the agent and the lender — and *nothing* beyond the next link. The chain's true length and health are unknowable from any seat in it. This is the inverse of every hub persona: there is no hub, and that absence is the defining pathology.

**Lifecycle (per transaction, 8–20 weeks, no two alike):**
- *Instruction & onboarding:* ID checks, client care letters, money laundering compliance — friction the client experiences as bureaucratic coldness at peak excitement.
- *The middle (the long dark):* searches ordered, enquiries raised, replies awaited, mortgage offers chased. Weeks where the honest status is "waiting on a stranger four links away." Client anxiety compounds in the silence.
- *Midpoint equivalent:* enquiries-and-replies ping-pong — the other side's answers spawn new questions; somewhere here the chain's weakest transaction wobbles and everyone feels the tremor without knowing its source.
- *Exchange (the cliff):* the synchronisation event — every party in every stacked transaction must be simultaneously ready; one missing redemption statement delays four families.
- *Completion (the crunch):* money moves down the chain in sequence on one morning; a delayed CHAPS at 11am is a removal van idling at 2pm.
- *Dissolution:* keys released, file archived, SDLT and registration trailing after; the chain's members never interact again.
- *Trust curve:* the client's trust starts moderate (conveyancers rank with traffic wardens in public affection) and is spent by silence faster than by delay itself — the *unexplained* wait is the relationship killer. Between professionals, trust is procedural and thin by design.

**Cadence:** Inbound call/email storms after every weekend and every Rightmove rumour; outbound chasing in batches; actual legal work in the gaps. The communication-to-progress ratio is the worst in the library.

**Boundary objects:** The contract pack, the enquiries-and-replies document (the ping-pong record), the mortgage offer, the searches, the completion statement, and the *status itself* — the most requested, least standardised artefact in the chain: every party maintains a different, stale version.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** Nowhere and everywhere — the chain's defining flaw. Each firm articulates its own link; nobody sequences the whole. The closest thing to a chain-coordinator is, absurdly, Ricky the estate agent: the least qualified, most motivated party, ringing everyone weekly.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Gillian knows this file's legal reality. The other side knows theirs and shares it grudgingly. The lender knows its own timeline and tells no one. Ricky knows the human gossip (the seller's divorce, the buyer above pulling out) that actually predicts the chain's fate — the most predictive data in the constellation lives in its least formal node. Amy knows nothing and feels everything. *No one knows the chain's true state.*

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Status opacity: the same "any update?" answered manually, redundantly, dozens of times a day, chain-wide — **eliminate**; this is the single largest coordination-waste pool in the UK SMB universe
2. The silent stall: a link stops (lost letter, sick conveyancer, slow council) and nobody downstream learns for weeks — **eliminate**
3. Duplicate chasing: five parties chasing the same redemption statement through each other — **eliminate**
4. Exchange-readiness ambiguity: nobody can confirm the whole chain is ready until the morning it has to be — **eliminate**
5. Client anxiety spirals filling the information vacuum — **eliminate** (an information failure with emotional interest)
6. The other-side's adversarial caution — slow, formal, minimal answers — **contain**: the parties' interests genuinely diverge (buyer vs. seller); the formality protects clients and cannot be smoothed away, only sped up
7. Gillian's refusal to skip a search or soften an enquiry under chain pressure — **preserve**: the conveyancer's awkward thoroughness is the only protection the buyer has

**Conflicting interests:**
- Buyer and seller want opposite things from every enquiry; their solicitors are professionally adversarial on their behalf
- Ricky wants completion at any speed; Gillian wants completion at safe speed — the accelerant vs. the brake, structurally
- The lender protects its security with total indifference to the chain's choreography
- Every firm in the chain wants the others to move faster while protecting its own queue

*Contain-class: the adversarial structure is the system working. The eliminable disaster is the opacity wrapped around it.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap Kyle: a week of file re-orientation. Swap Gillian mid-transaction: client trust resets at the worst moment, and file-state transfers only as well as the case management system was maintained (poorly). Swap *any other link in the chain* — and this is the defining feature — Hartwell can do nothing about it and may not learn for weeks: a conveyancer leaving on the other side of a stacked transaction two links away is invisible, consequential and unmanageable. The chain's substitution risk is unbounded because its membership is unknowable.

**Afterlife:** Transactions end; the parties scatter and never re-form (the anti-Hollywood model — almost no repeat game between professionals, which is *why* behaviour is so bad: there's no reputational tomorrow between firms). The exceptions: Hartwell↔local agents (a real repeat edge — Ricky refers business), and the file itself, which has a 15-year liability tail. The product insight: the chain's missing repeat-game incentive could be partially synthesised by a shared performance record — making tomorrow exist.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Be the first thing that ever sees the whole sequence. One live, shared, permission-scoped status per transaction — readable by every party at their legitimate level (client-grade reassurance for Amy, professional-grade detail for the solicitors, momentum-grade signals for Ricky). Chase the next action automatically, on whichever link owes it. Detect silent stalls by absence of expected events. Assemble exchange-readiness as a checklist across the visible chain. Answer "any update?" before it's asked. Never breach the adversarial boundary: the parties' positions stay private; only the *process state* is shared.

### Gillian Hartwell, 52 — Senior Partner *(The Careful Link)*
**Nottingham.** Thirty years of conveyancing; has seen every way a chain dies. Thorough to the point of folklore among local agents. **Attitude to AI:** deeply sceptical of anything touching legal judgement; quietly desperate for anything that kills the status-call tax. **Winning her looks like:** her assistants stop being a call centre, stalls surface in days not weeks, and nothing — nothing — drafts an enquiry reply without her.

### Kyle Sutton, 26 — Conveyancing Assistant *(The Human API)*
**Nottingham.** Answers forty status calls a day with information that's stale by lunchtime. Studying for his CLC qualification at night. **Attitude to AI:** please, today — he is the persona automation liberates rather than threatens, and he knows it. **Winning him looks like:** the update answers itself and his day fills with actual legal work that counts toward his qualification.

### Amy Whelan, 31 — The Buyer *(The Anxious Civilian)*
**Beeston.** First-time buyer; her life savings and a baby due in four months are riding on a process she cannot see, influence or understand. Refreshes her email hourly; interprets silence as disaster. **Attitude to AI:** irrelevant as a concept — she wants *to know what's happening* in human words. **Winning her looks like:** a live, honest, plain-English picture ("waiting on the council's search — typical wait 3 more weeks — nothing is wrong") that converts dread into mere impatience.

### Ricky Doyle, 34 — Estate Agent *(The Improvised Coordinator)*
**Nottingham.** Rings every party in every chain every Friday; knows the gossip that predicts collapses. Commission-paid, completion-focused, charm calibrated per audience. **Attitude to AI:** instant adopter of anything that gets deals done faster. **Winning him looks like:** chain-momentum signals he can act on (and brag about), without the solicitors feeling leaned on.

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- ◆ The chain topology demands the library's most radical product posture: the AI's value is *being the only party that sees the sequence* — which requires multi-firm adoption mechanics (each link invites the next; value at one link must be visible from the adjacent one) rather than single-account sales.
- One truth per legitimate audience, at four levels: client-grade, professional-grade, agent-grade, and the adversarial wall — process state shared, positions never.
- The chain has no repeat game between firms; the product can synthesise one (a shared record of responsiveness) — gently, or it becomes a weapon.
