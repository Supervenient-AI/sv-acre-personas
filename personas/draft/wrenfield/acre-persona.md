# ACRE Persona 23 — The Grief-Paced Relay
### Wrenfield Estate Services — a 7-person probate specialist between slow institutions and grieving families

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #15 (Family 3, relay chains); not yet interview-grounded.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Patient Administrator
**Instance:** Wrenfield Estate Services, Exeter. Director (licensed probate practitioner), two case managers, three administrators, a part-time accountant. ~180 estates a year, fixed-fee and hourly mixes, referred by will-writers, funeral directors and word of mouth.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | A relay through institutions that answer in weeks: banks, HMRC, the Probate Registry, pension schemes, insurers, utility companies — multiplied by every asset the deceased ever held, coordinated for clients at the worst moment of their lives. |
| Tooling | Case management software, HMRC's IHT forms and the probate portal, institutional bereavement portals (each different, most dreadful), letters — actual letters — and a phone line where hold music goes to thrive. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The case managers, across 40+ estates each; every estate is 15–40 institutional threads moving at institutional speed. |
| Buying behaviour | Conservative, confidentiality-bound, fee-pressure aware (banks and online services undercut the simple estates). Buys what reduces the chase burden — the cost base *is* chasing. |
| What failure costs | An IHT deadline missed accrues interest against the estate; a distribution error is personally recoverable from the practitioner; and every delay is experienced by a grieving family as disrespect to the person who died. The emotional stakes amplify every operational failure. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A relay chain whose counterparties are institutional bereavement departments with no deadline pressure and whose principals are grieving relatives with no process knowledge — the practice in between converting loss into paperwork and paperwork into resolution.

**Cast:**
- **Susan Pellow, 54** — director & licensed probate practitioner (Exeter)
- **Liam Tasker, 30** — case manager; runner of forty estates (Exeter)
- **David Bramley, 61** — client: executor for his late mother's estate, retired teacher, conscientious and drowning
- **Karen Bramley-Hodge, 57** — David's sister: beneficiary, not executor, increasingly suspicious of how long everything takes
- **Plus:** administrators, banks' bereavement teams, HMRC (IHT), the Probate Registry, a property valuer, an estate agent for the house

**External immovable nodes:**
- **HMRC** — IHT calculation, payment deadlines (interest accrues from month six), and queries on its own calendar
- **The Probate Registry** — the grant itself; backlogs measured in months, status opacity near-total
- **Banks' and institutions' bereavement processes** — each its own portal/letter ritual; weeks per round-trip, errors that restart the ritual
- **The property market** — the estate's largest asset sells when it sells; the chain (often a Persona 20 chain) attaches here

**Topology:** A hub running 15–40 *parallel* relay threads per estate — unlike conveyancing's single sequential chain, probate is a broom-head of simultaneous slow chases that must all complete before the estate can close. The family edge runs alongside: not a process thread but an emotional channel that colours every other.

**Lifecycle (per estate, 9–24 months):**
- *Instruction (in grief):* the meeting with tissues on the table; assets unknown even to the family, paperwork in carrier bags.
- *Asset discovery:* writing to every institution the deceased ever touched; the estate's true shape emerges over months.
- *IHT & the grant (the gate):* tax calculated and paid (often before assets are accessible — the liquidity trap), then the Registry's queue.
- *Midpoint equivalent:* the family-patience cliff — around month six, grief's administrative adrenaline fades and "why is this taking so long?" arrives, usually via the beneficiary, often via Karen. The practice's communication discipline is tested exactly when there's least to report.
- *Realisation & distribution crunch:* house sold, accounts closed, liabilities settled, estate accounts prepared to a standard a suspicious beneficiary's solicitor couldn't fault.
- *Dissolution:* distribution, final accounts, files retained for years against claims.
- *Trust curve:* the family's trust starts high (referred, in need) and erodes through institutional silence the practice didn't cause but must answer for. Between practice and institutions there is no trust — only process.

**Cadence:** Letters out in batches, responses in dribbles, monthly client updates (the discipline that separates good practices from complained-about ones), the occasional estate-shaking discovery (an unknown account, a second family).

**Boundary objects:** The will (sacred, occasionally contested), the asset-and-liability schedule (the estate's emerging truth), the IHT400 (forty pages of consequence), the grant of probate (the key that unlocks everything), the estate accounts (the final, auditable story), the monthly update letter.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Liam — forty estates × twenty-five threads each, sequenced against deadlines and dependencies, every thread a stranger's bereavement process. The discipline of "what's the next action on every thread" is the entire operational craft.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Liam holds the live state of a thousand threads. Susan holds the judgement calls (IHT reliefs, contested-will smells, when to involve counsel) and the regulatory liability. David holds family knowledge no institution has (the premium bonds in the loft, the sister's grievance). Karen holds expectations formed by no information. The institutions hold the assets and answer when they answer.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Thread stalls invisible among forty estates — a bank's non-response noticed at month three instead of week three — **eliminate**
2. The update vacuum: families filling silence with suspicion — **eliminate** (proactive, plain-English, even-when-nothing-happened updates)
3. Asset discovery latency: the unknown account that restarts IHT maths at month eight — **eliminate** the late discovery where records allow
4. The liquidity trap (IHT due before assets unlock) surprising families late — **eliminate** the surprise; the trap itself is statute
5. Beneficiary suspicion curdling into formal complaint or litigation — **contain**: executor/beneficiary interests genuinely diverge; the product keeps the process transparent enough that suspicion has nothing to feed on
6. Susan's insistence on estate accounts beyond reproach — **preserve**: the over-documentation is the defence
7. Institutional slowness itself — **contain**: unchangeable; the product's job is making the waiting *visible and explained*, which is most of what families actually need

**Conflicting interests:**
- David wants it done properly; Karen wants it done *now*; the will made one of them executor
- The institutions optimise for their own compliance, indifferent to the family's year
- The practice's fixed fees reward speed; its liability rewards thoroughness
- HMRC wants its money before the estate can pay it

*Contain-class: the executor/beneficiary tension is the emotional core — it cannot be resolved by software, only starved of the information vacuums it feeds on.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap an administrator: minor. Swap *Liam*: a thousand threads' state exists in the case system at perhaps 70% fidelity; the missing 30% is exactly the chase-instinct layer, and stalls bloom unseen for a quarter. Swap *Susan*: regulated continuity crisis — judgement, sign-off authority and the families' personal trust don't transfer with the files. Swap *David* (executors do die, renounce, or fall out): legal restart, family politics inflamed. The institutions substitute their staff constantly and invisibly — every chase starts from zero by design.

**Afterlife:** Estates close; files live for years against claims (the liability tail). The referral edges (will-writers, funeral directors, satisfied families) are the entire growth engine — and a family's verdict is formed mostly by communication, not speed, since speed was never available. The practice's process knowledge (which institutions respond to what, which forms restart cleanly) is folklore in two heads, rebuilt by every competitor separately — a commons the industry never built.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Run the broom-head: every thread's last action, owed response and age, surfaced as exceptions — chase letters generated and sent on schedule without Liam initiating each one. Translate process into family-grade updates monthly, automatically, warmly ("the Registry has had the application 11 weeks; their current average is 14; nothing is wrong"). Track IHT clocks and the liquidity timeline from day one. Keep the estate accounts assembling themselves from every transaction as it happens. Give Karen-the-beneficiary a legitimate, scoped window — enough transparency to starve suspicion, without breaching the executor's confidence. Tone is a compliance requirement: this product writes to the recently bereaved.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (3+ = structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Susan | Conductor · Specialist Maker (judgement) · Gatekeeper (sign-off) · Sponsor | **4 — the regulated-principal pathology** |
| Liam | Coordinator (×1,000 threads) · Production Realiser | 2 — at the library's highest thread count |
| David | Client Owner · Coordinator (family-side, unpaid, untrained) | 2 — a civilian doing articulation work in grief |
| Karen | Hidden Stakeholder · Gatekeeper (of family peace) | 2 — no formal power, all the emotional weather |

### Susan Pellow, 54 — Director *(The Steady Hand)*
**Exeter.** Twenty-five years in private-client law before founding Wrenfield; has seen families broken by estates and estates broken by families. Writes the difficult letters herself. **Attitude to AI:** measured — drafting and chasing welcome; anything touching distributions, tax judgement or the tone of a bereavement letter gets her eyes first. **Winning her looks like:** stalls surface themselves, the accounts build themselves, and every client family gets the communication discipline she currently enforces by willpower.

### Liam Tasker, 30 — Case Manager *(The Thread Runner)*
**Exeter.** Methodical to the point of art; his tracker spreadsheets have conditional formatting that tells stories. **Attitude to AI:** enthusiastic — chasing institutions is his week, and none of them deserve his creativity. **Winning him looks like:** the next-action engine runs the routine 80% and hands him the judgement 20%, with the chase history attached.

### David Bramley, 61 — Executor Client *(The Conscientious Drowner)*
**Tiverton.** Retired deputy head; executor because he's the responsible one, which his sister reads as "the favoured one". Keeps a folder, annotates everything, lies awake about the IHT payment. **Attitude to AI:** cautious but capable; would check a status page nightly rather than ring (ringing feels like bothering). **Winning him looks like:** he always knows what's happening, what's normal, and what (rarely) needs him — and the record protects him from his sister's solicitor-shaped questions.

### Karen Bramley-Hodge, 57 — Beneficiary *(The Suspicion Engine)*
**Taunton.** Loved her mother, resents the will's choice of executor, fills information vacuums with theories. Not unreasonable — just unserved: nobody has ever told her anything directly. **Attitude to AI:** none; attitude to being kept in the dark: combustible. **Winning her looks like:** a beneficiary-scoped view that treats her as a legitimate audience — what stage, what's typical, what the money picture is — so the practice stops being the villain in a story silence wrote.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Susan ↔ David | Engagement letter | High, referred → **vacuum-eroded**: institutional silence lands here | Meetings + monthly letters | He answers to family politics with information the practice controls |
| Practice ↔ Karen | None (duty runs to the estate) | Low, suspicious → **deteriorating by default** | Via David, distorted | The unserved audience; most complaints are born on this edge |
| David ↔ Karen | Blood | Strained → **the estate inflames it** | Family channels | The edge the product can only starve of fuel, never repair |
| Liam ↔ institutions | Statutory/process | None — procedural → glacial | Letters, portals, hold music | Forty bereavement departments, each a fresh start every call |
| Practice ↔ HMRC / Registry | Statutory | Procedural → **deadline-asymmetric**: their delays are free, the estate's cost interest | Forms + portals | The gate and the tax, on their own clocks |
| **Susan ↔ AI** | — | Measured, tone-vigilant | In-product | Bereavement-grade tone is a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have |
| **Liam ↔ AI** | — | Day-one adopter | In case management | Exception-driven thread management is the whole pitch |
| **David ↔ AI** | — | Quietly grateful | Status page + gentle email | Self-serve reassurance for people who won't "bother" anyone |
| **Karen ↔ AI** | — | Unknowing beneficiary of it | Scoped beneficiary view | One truth per legitimate audience, in its most delicate form yet |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The beneficiary view is this persona's signature design problem: a legally distinct, emotionally loaded audience who today gets nothing and therefore assumes the worst. Scoped transparency here prevents the complaints, the rifts and occasionally the litigation.
- Institutional chasing at the broom-head scale (parallel threads, not sequential links) extends the relay-family pattern: the unit of automation is the *thread next-action*, fired in batches, evidenced for the file.
- Tone is product surface: every outbound word may land on someone's worst year. The persona sets the bar for emotionally literate automation across the whole library.
