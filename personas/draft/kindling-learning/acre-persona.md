# ACRE Persona 18 — The SME-Starved Studio
### Kindling Learning — a 7-person e-learning studio whose critical path runs through other people's busiest experts

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #10 (Family 2, agency-led); not yet interview-grounded.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Knowledge-Extraction Studio
**Instance:** Kindling Learning, Belfast. Founder/learning director, three instructional designers, a producer, a developer (LMS/SCORM), a part-time QA — plus freelance animators, illustrators and voiceover artists per project. £850k; compliance training, onboarding programmes and product education for mid-size firms.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Doubly dependent: downstream on freelance media makers, upstream — and critically — on the client's subject-matter experts, who hold the knowledge the product is made of and have proper jobs that aren't this. |
| Tooling | Articulate Storyline/Rise, Google Workspace, Vyond for animation, a review tool clients half-use, the client's LMS at the end of everything. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The producer for media and milestones; the instructional designers personally absorb the SME-chasing, which is the real schedule risk. |
| Buying behaviour | Margin-conscious and deadline-driven; already deep into AI for drafting and voiceover scratch tracks — quietly, because clients haven't all blessed it. Will pay for anything that compresses the SME loop. |
| What failure costs | A launch date tied to a regulatory deadline or product release missed because an expert went quiet for three weeks; rework when the SME finally reviews module 3 and contradicts what they signed off in module 1. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A studio hub building learning content whose raw material — expert knowledge — must be extracted from time-poor client-side specialists, transformed through freelance media production, and delivered into the client's technical estate by a fixed date.

**Cast:**
- **Bridget Mahon, 45** — founder & learning director (Belfast)
- **Dan Veasey, 34** — senior instructional designer; chief SME-whisperer (Belfast)
- **Dr Hugh Stanmore, 57** — the client's subject-matter expert (head of regulatory affairs; this project is his 11th priority)
- **Sandeep Gill, 40** — client-side L&D manager; owns the launch internally
- **Flo Carter, 29** — freelance animator (Brighton)
- **Plus:** the producer, the LMS developer, a voiceover artist, the client's IT (LMS access), the client's legal review

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The regulatory or launch deadline** — compliance training tied to a regulation's commencement date; product training tied to a release. The date moves for no one
- **The client's legal/compliance review** — a late gate that can rewrite finished modules
- **The client's LMS and IT estate** — SCORM quirks, access requests, publishing windows: technical gates owned elsewhere
- **Accessibility standards (WCAG)** — non-negotiable, expensive to retrofit, checked late by habit

**Topology:** Hub-and-spoke with an inverted power spoke: the SME is formally a resource and functionally a gatekeeper — the constellation's schedule belongs to the diary of a person who never agreed to be on the critical path.

**Lifecycle (per programme, 3–6 months):**
- *Discovery & design:* kickoff enthusiasm; the SME attends one workshop, brilliantly, creating the illusion of availability.
- *Extraction (the silent killer):* scripts and storyboards built from documents plus SME interviews that take three weeks to schedule; each unanswered question becomes an assumption with a half-life.
- *Midpoint reset:* SME review of early modules arrives late and rewrites foundations — "that's technically wrong" landing after animation has rendered it beautifully. The classic recalibration, paid at media-production prices.
- *Production:* animation, voiceover, build — freelancers working from scripts whose verification status nobody tracked.
- *Crunch:* legal review, accessibility fixes, LMS publishing battles against the immovable date.
- *Dissolution:* launch, a flurry of fixes, then maintenance amnesia — until the regulation changes and the content needs updating by people who've forgotten how it was made.
- *Trust curve:* Sandeep's trust spends on every slipped milestone he must explain upward; the studio's trust in *the client* spends on every SME silence — and unlike most personas, the studio's biggest risk is a person on the client's own side.

**Cadence:** Weekly client calls (Sandeep always; Hugh rarely), review tools clients use as email-attachment generators, freelancer bursts per module, SME contact in precious scheduled slivers.

**Boundary objects:** The storyboard/script (the contract between knowledge and media — every ambiguity in it gets animated), the SME review comments (gold dust, arriving late and contradictory), the voiceover script (locked hardest, changed latest), the SCORM package, the sign-off record.

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Dan — extracting, paraphrasing and re-verifying expert knowledge while managing the gap between what Hugh said in March and what he remembers having said in May. On the producer for the media chain.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Hugh holds the actual knowledge, plus the unwritten institutional sensitivities ("we never phrase it that way since the incident"). Dan holds the best current approximation of Hugh's knowledge — the studio's working truth. Sandeep holds the political map and the launch stakes. Flo holds the source files (a future hostage situation if she's unavailable). The sign-off record, where it exists, holds who approved what — the document that settles the midpoint dispute.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. SME latency: weeks-long silences on the critical path — **eliminate** the silent queueing (make the cost visible, batch the asks, shrink each one); the SME's scarcity itself is a fact to design around, not fix
2. Verification drift: content built on unconfirmed assumptions that harden into modules — **eliminate**
3. Late SME contradiction of earlier sign-off — **contain**: experts genuinely refine their views; the product anchors versions and sign-offs so refinement is a tracked change, not a dispute
4. Media rework from upstream script changes after animation/voiceover — **eliminate** (a sequencing failure)
5. The legal-review rewrite at the end — **contain**: the gate is legitimate; its lateness is the eliminable part
6. Dan's pushback on cramming ("that's three modules of cognitive load in one") — **preserve**: learning-design integrity is the quality system

**Conflicting interests:**
- Hugh's actual job competes with the project for Hugh; his employer wants both
- Sandeep needs the date; Bridget needs Hugh; these are the same problem worn by different people
- Freelancers want locked scripts; the extraction process inherently unlocks them
- The studio wants AI-accelerated drafting; some client policies haven't caught up; the deadline doesn't care

*Contain-class: the SME-scarcity economy is permanent. The AI's job is to make every minute of expert time count triple — and make the cost of expert silence visible to the people who can fix it.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap Flo mid-production: source-file dependency surfaces; days lost if files weren't escrowed. Swap Dan: the extracted-knowledge approximation — months of interviews and inference — partially evaporates; Hugh must repeat himself, which he won't gracefully. Swap *Hugh* (reorg, departure — common): catastrophic; the new SME contradicts the old one's sign-offs wholesale and the programme partially restarts. Swap Sandeep: the launch loses its internal champion at the moment momentum matters. The pattern: this constellation's fragility is concentrated entirely in client-side seats the studio doesn't control.

**Afterlife:** Learning content lives for years and decays invisibly: regulations change, products update, and the maintenance request lands on whoever remains. The extraction archive — interview recordings, decision rationale, sign-off history — is the difference between a two-day update and a re-commission. Client edges persist strongly (L&D managers move companies and re-buy); freelancer edges are repeat-game standard. Today the archive is project folders and Dan's memory.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Make SME time hyper-efficient: batched, pre-researched questions; interviews transcribed and mined so nothing is asked twice; draft answers from existing documents for Hugh to confirm rather than compose. Track every claim's verification status from assumption → confirmed → signed-off, visibly. Sequence media production behind verification, automatically. Keep the sign-off record dispute-proof. Maintain the living content map so the year-two regulation change is a surgical update, not archaeology.

## R — Roles

**Archetype load** (3+ = structural overload):

| Seat | Archetypes held | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Bridget | Conductor · Sponsor · Client Owner (relationship) | **3 — founder-lead pattern** |
| Dan | Specialist Maker · Coordinator (SME loop) · Strategist (knowledge model) | **3 — the extraction overload** |
| Hugh | Peripheral Expert · Gatekeeper (truth) | 2 — held in 1% of his diary |
| Sandeep | Client Owner · Coordinator (client-side) | 2 |
| Flo | Specialist Maker | 1 |

### Bridget Mahon, 45 — Founder & Learning Director *(The Standard-Bearer)*
**Belfast.** Twenty years in L&D, agency and in-house; founded Kindling to make compliance training people don't hate. **Attitude to AI:** strategic adopter — AI drafting doubled her margin on scripting and she knows the clients will eventually ask; wants governance before they do. **Winning her looks like:** SME bottlenecks stop eating launches, and she can show clients exactly where AI did and didn't touch their content.

### Dan Veasey, 34 — Senior Instructional Designer *(The Extraction Artist)*
**Belfast.** Ex-teacher; can turn an hour of expert rambling into a learning structure. Maintains a private "Hugh-isms" document of everything ever confirmed. **Attitude to AI:** heavy daily user (transcription, structuring, drafting); guards the pedagogy. **Winning him looks like:** his private verification tracker becomes the system, every interview is mined automatically, and he never asks an expert the same question twice.

### Dr Hugh Stanmore, 57 — Client SME *(The Eleventh Priority)*
**Client-side, regulatory affairs.** Knows everything the course must teach; available approximately never. Genuinely cares about accuracy, which is why his late reviews are devastating rather than dismissible. **Attitude to AI:** none for himself; would dictate corrections to anything that made reviewing take fifteen minutes instead of an afternoon. **Winning him looks like:** asks arrive small, batched and pre-researched; reviews are markup-light; nothing he confirmed resurfaces.

### Sandeep Gill, 40 — Client L&D Manager *(The Launch Owner)*
**Client-side.** Bought the programme, promised the board the date, owns neither the expert nor the legal review that gate it. **Attitude to AI:** pragmatic enthusiast; under pressure, will paste anything into anything. **Winning him looks like:** an honest status he can forward upward, and the SME-silence cost made visible to the people who can unlock Hugh's diary — with someone else's name on the delay.

### Flo Carter, 29 — Freelance Animator *(The Downstream Maker)*
**Brighton.** Vyond and After Effects; four studios on rotation. Burned by re-animating scenes after "locked" scripts unlocked. **Attitude to AI:** uses it for storyboards; watches it encroach on her craft with professional unease. **Winning her looks like:** scripts arrive genuinely verified, changes arrive as priced amendments, and her source files are escrowed without her feeling expropriated.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Studio ↔ Sandeep | Programme contract | Good → **date-strained**: every slip spends his internal capital | Weekly calls + email | He owns the deadline but not the expert; the studio inherits his powerlessness |
| Dan ↔ Hugh | None (borrowed time) | Respectful → **scarcity-defined**: the constellation's true critical path | Scheduled slivers + email | All the knowledge one side; all the schedule pressure the other |
| Sandeep ↔ Hugh | Colleagues | Polite → **politically delicate**: chasing a senior expert is career-expensive | Internal | The edge that actually gates the project is internal to the client and invisible to the studio |
| Studio ↔ Flo | Per-project freelance | Warm, repeat → **rework-strained** | Email + review links | Script volatility lands on her margin |
| Studio ↔ client legal | None until the end | Formal → gate-shaped | Document review | The late rewrite engine; earlier contact is the cheap fix nobody arranges |
| **Bridget ↔ AI** | — | Strategic, governance-minded | In-product | Wants AI-use transparency as a client-facing feature |
| **Dan ↔ AI** | — | Power user already | In-product | The verification tracker is the feature he'd build himself |
| **Hugh ↔ AI** | — | Indifferent; benefits without using | Email/dictation bridge | The product serves him invisibly: smaller asks, faster reviews |
| **Sandeep ↔ AI** | — | Enthusiastic, leak-prone | Client window | Needs a governed surface before the ungoverned paste happens |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The Dan↔Hugh edge is the persona's heart: a scarce-expert protocol — batched micro-asks, transcript mining, draft-for-confirmation — that treats expert minutes as the constellation's rarest currency.
- Verification status as a first-class property of content: every claim's lineage from assumption to sign-off, gating downstream media spend automatically.
- The SME-silence cost must be made visible *diplomatically* — a neutral record that lets Sandeep escalate inside his own company without anyone being blamed by a vendor's tool.
