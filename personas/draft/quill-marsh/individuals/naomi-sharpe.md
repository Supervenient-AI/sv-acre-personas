> “One side pays my fees. My duty runs to the court. Every report I write must survive the question ‘would you have written this for the other side?' — because one day, under cross-examination, I'll be asked exactly that.”

## Her world
Naomi inhabits the law's strangest seat: commissioned by one party, owned by the court. Her independence isn't a posture — it's the asset, maintained against every instructing solicitor's gentle gravitational pull. Her "the numbers don't support that head of claim" has torpedoed cases she was paid to assist, and the firms that respected her for it instruct her again; the others don't, and that's the system working.

## What she's trying to do
- Produce reports that survive hostile cross-examination — the only standard there is
- Get clean instructions and complete data, the inputs her independence runs on
- Protect the firewall: coordination on logistics, never influence on opinion

## What gets in her way
- Instructions that arrive shaped like conclusions
- Data delivered late, partial, and re-litigated
- Draft-review processes that drift, by inches, toward advocacy

## How she feels about AI
Uses it in analysis with her own verification layers. For the report: her opinion, her words, her name. Tools that make the firewall *visible* — what was shared, what wasn't, when — actively protect her, and she'd welcome them by name.

## In her own words
> “Make the boundary auditable. The clearer the record of what I was and wasn't told, the safer my independence — and the stronger their case, whether they realise it or not.”

## Win her by
Mechanically visible firewalls: clean instructions, complete data trails, coordination logged and influence impossible — her independence protected by architecture, not just character.
