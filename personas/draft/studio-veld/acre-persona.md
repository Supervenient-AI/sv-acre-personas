# ACRE Persona 14 — The Crit-Culture Studio
### Studio Veld — a 9-person brand & digital design studio with a freelance bench and opinionated clients

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #6 (Family 2, agency-led); not yet interview-grounded.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Taste Business
**Instance:** Studio Veld, Manchester (Northern Quarter). Founder/creative director, design director, four designers, a producer, a strategist, a studio manager — plus a freelance bench that doubles the studio in busy quarters. £1.2m fees; identity and digital projects, 6 weeks to 6 months.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Project-based constellations: studio core + per-project freelancers + a client-side approval chain of unpredictable depth. The deliverable is subjective, which makes every edge an opinion channel. |
| Tooling | Figma (the actual workplace), Slack, Notion (half-gardened), Google Slides for decks, Dropbox archaeology from the before-times. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The producer, officially and actually — but the creative director carries *opinion coordination*, which is the expensive kind here. |
| Buying behaviour | Design-led to a fault: will reject capable tools for ugly ones. Adopts fast inside the studio; never imposes tools on clients (the deck goes to the client in whatever the client reads). |
| What failure costs | Scope creep is invisible in a taste business — "one more round" has no unit cost until the project loses money. A misread client politics costs a relationship; a leaked unfinished concept costs a pitch. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A studio hub running parallel project constellations where the work converges through structured critique — internal crits, client presentations, and the dangerous informal feedback that flows around both.

**Cast:**
- **Anneke Veld, 42** — founder & creative director (Manchester)
- **Callum Frost, 36** — design director, runs the crits
- **Gracie Lim, 30** — producer; scope, schedule and the feelings in between
- **Theo Marchetti, 27** — freelance designer, on his fourth Veld project
- **Rachel Dunn, 38** — client-side brand manager at a scale-up (the project's voice of the client)
- **Plus:** the strategist, the studio designers, the client's CEO (taste arrives from here, unscheduled)

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The client's CEO/founder** — not in the project channel, but the true approval gate; their drive-by opinion at week nine outranks nine weeks of process
- **Launch dates** — set by the client's funding announcements, store openings or campaigns; design absorbs whatever time is left
- **Client procurement/legal** — appears at contract and at IP handover, both times as a surprise

**Topology:** Agency-led hub per project, with a structured internal mesh (the crit) and a deliberately narrow client interface (the presentation) — which the informal back-channel (Rachel's DMs to Gracie, the CEO's texts to Anneke) constantly bypasses.

**Lifecycle (per project, 6 weeks–6 months):**
- *Pitch & assembly:* the studio over-invests creatively in winning; freelancers booked on probability. The brief agreed at kickoff is the client's *stated* brief; the real one emerges later.
- *Concepting:* divergence, internal crits, the first presentation — where the client falls in love with the wrong route or the right one for wrong reasons.
- *Midpoint reset:* the predictable one — around the second presentation, the client's internal politics surface ("the CEO has seen it now"), and the brief reveals its true self. Studios that re-scope here survive; studios that absorb it subsidise it.
- *Production & crunch:* rounds compress, freelancers churn, "final" becomes a version-name prefix.
- *Dissolution:* handover, guidelines, a retro that happens only after the painful ones; freelancer knowledge dissolves to the bench.
- *Trust curve:* the client's trust peaks at the pitch (they bought the dream) and is spent through every round that doesn't land; the studio's trust in the *client* — equally real — is spent by feedback chaos and scope amnesia.

**Cadence:** Slack-continuous internally; client contact compressed into presentations plus an unofficial Rachel↔Gracie channel where the truth travels.

**Boundary objects:** The Figma file (workplace, gallery, archaeological record and leak surface in one), the deck (where design goes to be judged), the brief (contested), the scope letter (forgotten), the brand guidelines (the deliverable that outlives everyone).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Gracie — rounds, scope, freelancer wrangling, and the unpaid diplomacy of translating "the CEO hates purple now" into actionable feedback. Callum carries crit synthesis; Anneke carries client-politics reading.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Anneke holds the client's real power map and the studio's taste bar. Callum holds why each design decision was made (the crit record, unminuted). Gracie holds true scope state and the feedback ledger. Theo holds his own files' logic — gone when his contract ends. Rachel holds what her CEO actually said versus what she relayed. The Figma file holds everything and explains nothing.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Feedback chaos: contradictory notes from multiple client voices, arriving in DMs, decks and meetings — **eliminate** the fragmentation; the disagreement itself is the client's to have
2. Round-creep: "one more pass" with no scope ledger — **eliminate** (an accounting failure before it's a relationship one)
3. The CEO drive-by at week nine — **contain**: the power is real and won't be processed away; the product anchors it to the artefact and prices the consequence
4. Freelancer context loss: Theo re-onboarded every project, his rationale lost after each — **eliminate**
5. Crit softening: critique relayed second-hand arriving vague ("they want it more premium") — **eliminate** the relay distortion; **preserve** the crit itself — the studio's crit culture is its quality engine and must never be smoothed
6. The strategist's awkward "this is off-strategy" late in production — **preserve**

**Conflicting interests:**
- The client wants unlimited refinement at fixed price; the studio sells finite rounds
- Anneke wants the portfolio piece; the client wants the safe choice; both are "quality"
- Rachel needs to look in-control upward; the truth of the process is iterative mess
- Freelancers want credit and rationale; studios default to absorbing both

*Contain-class: anchor rounds, feedback and scope to the artefact and the ledger; let taste conflicts stay loud but attached to specific frames.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap Theo mid-project: his Figma logic is undocumented; days of file archaeology and a visible style wobble. Swap Gracie: the scope ledger and feedback diplomacy live in her head; every active project's politics reset. Swap Rachel (scale-ups churn): the studio loses its translator and meets the CEO's taste raw. Swap Anneke: the studio survives operationally and dies commercially — clients buy her eye.

**Afterlife:** Strong repeat game: rebrands beget campaigns beget retainers; freelancers who felt credited return on short notice (the bench is the studio's elasticity). The guidelines outlive the project and misrepresent it — the *rationale* behind the system is never archived, so every future user of the brand re-litigates decisions the crit already settled. The case study is the studio's sales asset and is written from memory, months later.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** One feedback ledger per project — every note from every channel, attributed, attached to the frame it concerns, with contradictions surfaced instead of absorbed. A scope/rounds meter visible to both sides before resentment prices it. Freelancer onboarding from the project's own record. Crit capture: decisions and rationale recorded at the moment of critique, becoming the guidelines' "why" for free. Never let the client feel processed — presentations stay theatre, the ledger stays backstage.

| Gracie | Coordinator · Gatekeeper (scope) | 2 — both invisible until they fail |
| Callum | Creative Lead (operational) · Strategist (crit synthesis) | 2 |
| Theo | Specialist Maker · Newcomer (perpetually) | 2 |
| Rachel | Client Owner · Coordinator (client-side, unpaid) | 2 |

### Anneke Veld, 42 — Founder & Creative Director *(The Eye and the Handshake)*
**Manchester.** Built the studio on two famous rebrands and a reputation for saying no to bad briefs. Reads client politics like typography. **Attitude to AI:** aesthetically suspicious, practically curious; uses it for proposals, would never let it near a concept. **Winning her looks like:** she walks into every presentation knowing exactly who said what about which frame — and the studio stops losing money in the gaps her taste doesn't cover.

### Callum Frost, 36 — Design Director *(The Crit Engine)*
**Stockport.** Runs Tuesday crits like a seminar; the studio's quality bar is his voice in everyone's head. **Attitude to AI:** open for drudgery (asset prep, file hygiene), protective of judgement. **Winning him looks like:** crit decisions get captured without the crit changing, and rationale stops dying in the room.

### Gracie Lim, 30 — Producer *(The Diplomatic Ledger)*
**Salford.** Ex-agency PM; the person who knows the true status of everything and the polite version of it. **Attitude to AI:** enthusiastic — she is the persona the product relieves first. **Winning her looks like:** the rounds ledger and feedback record keep themselves, and she spends her diplomacy on moments that deserve it.

### Theo Marchetti, 27 — Freelance Designer *(The Bench)*
**Leeds.** Four studios, one Figma account, a portfolio he guards like a passport. **Attitude to AI:** fluent, unbothered — but alert to anything that tracks his hours or harvests his style. **Winning him looks like:** day-one context without meetings, and his name staying attached to his work in the record.

### Rachel Dunn, 38 — Client Brand Manager *(The Translator Upward)*
**Client-side, scale-up.** Owns the rebrand internally; spends her credibility on every round. **Attitude to AI:** daily ChatGPT user; will paste the studio's deck into it the night before her board meeting. **Winning her looks like:** she always has the "what changed and why" story ready for her CEO — supplied, not assembled at midnight.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Anneke ↔ Rachel | Project contract | Good → **politics-strained**: each round that misses spends it; each CEO drive-by strains it differently | Presentations + email | Rachel filters the client's truth; Anneke filters the studio's process — two curated realities negotiating |
| Rachel ↔ her CEO | Employment | Variable → the project's hidden driver | Internal, invisible | The most consequential edge in the constellation is one the studio can't see |
| Gracie ↔ Rachel | None (working) | Warm, real → the truth channel | DMs | Where actual status flows; officially doesn't exist |
| Anneke ↔ Theo (bench) | Per project | Warm, repeat → **credit-sensitive** | Slack + Figma | His rationale enriches the studio only if captured; his loyalty depends on credit |
| Callum ↔ designers | Employment | High, crit-bonded → stable | The crit | Quality lives here; the record of it lives nowhere |
| **Anneke ↔ AI** | — | Aesthetically conditional | In-product | The product must pass a designer's taste bar to exist in this building |
| **Gracie ↔ AI** | — | First and fastest adopter | In-product | Ledger automation is her entire job description's missing half |
| **Rachel ↔ AI** | — | Already using her own | Her ChatGPT | Like Persona 3's Dominic: govern the edge by being more useful than her paste-into-ChatGPT habit |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- Feedback is this constellation's bloodstream and its poison: one attributed, artefact-anchored ledger across decks, DMs and meetings is the core product surface.
- The invisible Rachel↔CEO edge can't be instrumented — but its *artefacts* (sudden reversals, week-nine bombs) can be caught, priced and anchored the moment they land.
- Two curated realities (studio-facing, client-facing) are legitimate here — another non-matchmaker instance of *one truth per legitimate audience*. The ledger is whole; the views are scoped.
