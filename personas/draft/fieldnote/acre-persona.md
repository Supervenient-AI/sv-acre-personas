# ACRE Persona 17 — The Consent-Heavy Field Chain
### Fieldnote Research — an 8-person market research agency running fieldwork through recruiters, moderators and transcribers

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #9 (Family 2, agency-led); not yet interview-grounded.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The PII-Custodian Agency
**Instance:** Fieldnote Research, Edinburgh. Founder/research director, two senior researchers, an analyst, a project manager, plus a per-project network: field recruitment agencies, freelance moderators, transcribers, a stats freelancer. £900k; qual and quant studies for consumer brands and public-sector bodies.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | Chain-and-hub hybrid: every study routes through external recruiters (who hold the participants), freelance moderators (who hold the rooms), and transcribers (who hold the raw words) — each handoff carrying personal data with legal weight. |
| Tooling | Office 365, Teams for client calls, a quota spreadsheet per study, recruiter portals that don't talk to anything, encrypted file transfer used inconsistently enough to keep the founder awake. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The project manager per study; the founder carries the compliance anxiety, which is its own full-time cognitive load. |
| Buying behaviour | Cautious, MRS-code-literate; any tool touching participant data triggers a DPA review they do themselves, slowly. Will pay properly for anything that de-risks consent and PII handling, because the downside is regulatory. |
| What failure costs | A consent-trail gap or a PII leak is existential (MRS code, GDPR, public-sector clients). Below that: under-recruited groups burning fieldwork dates, incentives unpaid souring the participant pool, findings late for the client's decision meeting — which was the entire point. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** A research hub coordinating a chain of external parties around human participants — recruit, consent, moderate, record, transcribe, analyse, report — where personal data flows down the chain and any broken link is a legal event, not just a delay.

**Cast:**
- **Maeve Quinlan, 48** — founder & research director (Edinburgh)
- **Jess Calder, 33** — project manager; quotas, dates, incentives, chaos (Edinburgh)
- **Karen Whitby, 51** — director of the field recruitment agency (Glasgow)
- **Asha Bhatt, 39** — freelance qual moderator (Manchester)
- **Lauren Mackie, 37** — client-side insight manager (consumer brand, London)
- **Plus:** transcribers, a stats freelancer, viewing-facility staff, and the participants themselves — the constellation's most important non-members

**External immovable nodes:**
- **GDPR and the MRS Code of Conduct** — consent, anonymisation, retention: rules that bind every link including the freelancers who never read them
- **The participants** — recruited civilians with lives; no-shows and dropouts are weather, not failure
- **The client's decision meeting** — the date the findings exist to serve; research that misses it is archaeology
- **Client legal/procurement** — DPAs and supplier-listing requirements that gate everything, slowly

**Topology:** A hub running a sequential chain (recruit → consent → field → transcribe → analyse → report) with the client attached at both ends — briefing at the start, consuming at the end, and increasingly often appearing in the middle (viewing facility, streamed groups) with opinions.

**Lifecycle (per study, 4–10 weeks):**
- *Design & commission:* proposal, screener, discussion guide — the client's internal disagreements about objectives surface here or, worse, don't.
- *Recruitment window:* Karen's team works the lists; quota panic builds silently until day three ("we have two of eight ABC1 males").
- *Fieldwork (the scheduled crunch):* groups and depths on fixed dates — moderator performing, client viewing, recordings accumulating with consent attached, theoretically.
- *Midpoint reset:* halfway through fieldwork, early findings leak to the client informally ("what are you hearing?") and reshape expectations before analysis exists — the analysis is then judged against the corridor conversation.
- *Analysis & crunch:* transcripts late, the analyst drowning, the debrief deck assembled against the decision-meeting date.
- *Dissolution:* debrief delivered, recordings *supposed* to be deleted per retention policy; the deletion happens "later", which is a finding in itself.
- *Trust curve:* the client's trust peaks at the debrief's first ten slides; the participant pool's trust — the industry's shared commons — erodes with every late incentive payment.

**Cadence:** Email-and-portal with recruiters, WhatsApp with moderators in the field, Teams with the client, silence from transcribers until delivery or apology.

**Boundary objects:** The screener (where recruitment quality is decided), the consent form (the legal spine), the discussion guide (the moderator's score), the quota grid, the transcripts (raw PII to one party, raw material to another), the debrief deck (the only artefact the client remembers).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Jess — quotas, facility bookings, moderator schedules, incentive payments, consent-form chasing — a chain of small handoffs where any drop is visible to the client or the regulator. Maeve carries the synthesis load and the compliance vigilance.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Karen knows the real state of recruitment (and pads it). Asha knows what participants actually said versus what the transcript caught, plus the unspoken room-feel that never reaches the deck. The analyst knows the data's weak spots. Lauren knows the political question behind the research question. Maeve knows the retention policy; nobody else could state it. Consent status lives across three organisations' inboxes.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Consent-trail fragmentation: forms in recruiter inboxes, recordings on moderator laptops, no unified register — **eliminate**; this is the regulatory-grade failure class
2. Quota blindness: recruitment shortfalls surfacing days before fieldwork — **eliminate**
3. The corridor-findings leak reshaping expectations pre-analysis — **contain**: clients will always ask "what are you hearing?"; the answer needs a sanctioned, caveated channel rather than a ban that won't hold
4. Transcription latency stalling analysis against a fixed debrief date — **eliminate**
5. Incentive-payment lag souring the participant commons — **eliminate**
6. Asha's pushback on a leading discussion guide and the analyst's "the data doesn't support that slide" — **preserve**: methodological friction is the product's integrity
7. Late-arriving client stakeholders re-litigating objectives at debrief — **contain** (Studio Veld's ambush pattern, with a fixed decision date)

**Conflicting interests:**
- Lauren needs a clear answer for her decision meeting; the data is usually murkier than the deadline
- Karen's agency profits from recruitment speed; quality erodes invisibly until a group is full of professional respondents
- Asha protects her craft and her participant rapport; observation rooms want performances
- Participants want respect and prompt payment; every other party's deadline pressure lands on them last
- Maeve wants methodological rigour; the budget wants fewer groups

*Contain-class: the rigour-vs-deadline conflict is permanent; the AI's job is keeping it visible and anchored to the data, so the deck's claims stay tethered to what was actually heard.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap a transcriber: a day's delay. Swap Asha mid-fieldwork: groups reschedule (participants booked to her sessions), rapport resets, and moderation style shifts visibly between group 3 and group 4 — a methodological footnote the client will ask about. Swap Karen's agency: the participant database and its quality history vanish; recruitment risk doubles for two studies. Swap Jess: every in-flight handoff (consents, incentives, bookings) is suddenly unowned — the chain's articulation map existed only in her trackers. Swap Lauren mid-study: the political question changes while the screener stays frozen.

**Afterlife:** Studies end; three things persist with weight: the *consent and retention obligations* (recordings must be deleted on schedule — dissolution has a legal deadline here, unique in the library), the *participant commons* (a pool shared with every other agency; abuse it and recruitment costs rise for everyone), and the *findings archive* — which clients lose and re-commission within two years ("didn't we research this in 2024?"). The recruiter and moderator edges are classic repeat-game infrastructure.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** A consent-and-PII register as the spine: every participant, form, recording and retention date in one auditable place, with deletion that actually happens. Live quota visibility from the recruiter's pipeline. Transcription handled in hours, inside the perimeter. A sanctioned early-signals channel — caveated interim themes the client can hold without the corridor leak. Incentives paid same-day. The debrief deck's claims traceable to transcript evidence. And absolute clarity that participant voices are confidential material, not training data.

### Maeve Quinlan, 48 — Founder & Research Director *(The Rigour Conscience)*
**Edinburgh.** Twenty-five years in qual; can smell a leading question at fifty paces. Personally carries the MRS code the way Calder Wealth carries the FCA. **Attitude to AI:** professionally conflicted — uses it for desk research and deck drafting, refuses it anywhere near raw participant data until provenance and deletion are provable. **Winning her looks like:** the consent register is finally one thing, deletion is automatic and evidenced, and the AI's synthesis suggestions cite transcripts line-by-line.

### Jess Calder, 33 — Project Manager *(The Chain Runner)*
**Edinburgh.** Runs four studies in parallel; her trackers are works of desperate art. **Attitude to AI:** yes, today — chasing, scheduling and incentive admin are her week. **Winning her looks like:** quota status, consent status and payment status stop being things she assembles and start being things she checks.

### Karen Whitby, 51 — Field Recruitment Director *(The Gate to the Public)*
**Glasgow.** Thirty years recruiting; her database of willing humans is her business. Optimistic by profession ("we'll get there") until suddenly not. **Attitude to AI:** wary — her opacity is partly her margin; a shared pipeline view feels like an audit. **Winning her looks like:** sharing live status *reduces* the panicked calls she gets and the over-recruit padding she pays for; transparency costs her less than the chaos did.

### Asha Bhatt, 39 — Freelance Moderator *(The Room Reader)*
**Manchester.** Hundreds of groups across a dozen agencies; holds rapport like a craft secret. **Attitude to AI:** open for logistics, fierce about the room — no recording analysis that reduces participants to sentiment scores, no tool visible to respondents. **Winning her looks like:** her post-group voice-note debrief becomes the system's richest input, credited, and the unspoken room-feel finally survives into analysis.

### Lauren Mackie, 37 — Client Insight Manager *(The Decision-Meeting Owner)*
**London.** Commissioned the research to settle an internal argument she's already losing. **Attitude to AI:** daily user; will paste the debrief into her own tools the minute it arrives. **Winning her looks like:** sanctioned early signals she can socialise carefully, and a debrief that lands the day before her meeting with every claim defensible.

## E — Edges

| Edge | Contract | Trust (level → trajectory) | Channel | Key asymmetry / note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maeve ↔ Lauren | Study contract + DPA | Good → **deadline-strained**: rigour vs. the decision meeting, every study | Teams + email | Lauren's real (political) question is never fully in the brief |
| Fieldnote ↔ Karen's agency | Per-study recruitment | Long, warm → **opacity-strained**: her optimism pads the truth | Portal + phone | She holds the participants; Fieldnote holds the liability for how they're treated |
| Fieldnote ↔ Asha | Per-study freelance | Craft-respect → stable | WhatsApp + briefings | She holds unrecorded knowledge (room-feel) the deliverable needs |
| Jess ↔ transcribers | Per-hour freelance | Transactional → **latency-defined** | File transfer | Raw PII moves on this edge with the least ceremony — the compliance soft spot |
| Fieldnote ↔ participants | Consent forms + incentives | Fragile commons → **eroding industry-wide** | Via recruiters | They're not members but the constellation exists entirely around them; respect is operationalised as prompt payment and real deletion |
| **Maeve ↔ AI** | — | Conditionally willing, provenance-first | In-product | Raw-data access is earned by auditability, never assumed |
| **Jess ↔ AI** | — | Immediate adopter | In-product | The chain's handoffs are the automation surface |
| **Karen ↔ AI** | — | Audit-wary | Portal bridge | Frame shared status as fewer panicked calls, not surveillance |
| **Lauren ↔ AI** | — | Will self-serve regardless | Client window | Give her a governed early-signals surface before she builds her own in ChatGPT |

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- The consent/PII register is the spine and the moat: this persona's compliance burden is the heaviest in the agency families, and a product that makes it automatic converts anxiety into adoption.
- The participant edge introduces a party who must benefit without ever being a user: prompt incentives and honoured deletion are product behaviours owed to people who will never see the interface.
- The early-signals channel is a one-truth-per-audience design: interim themes exist, caveated, for Lauren; raw data exists for the researchers; the corridor version stops existing.
