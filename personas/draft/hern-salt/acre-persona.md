# ACRE Persona 40 — The Four-Hundredfold Pulse
### Hern & Salt Productions — a 4-person festival producer that becomes a 400-person organisation for six weeks a year

*Status: hypothesis persona — generated from ACRE target universe candidate #32 (Family 9, seasonal/pulse); ◆ adds the pulse constellation — enormous temporary teams inflating and deflating on a calendar.*

---

## A — Account

**Archetype:** The Annual Organisation-Builder
**Instance:** Hern & Salt Productions, Frome. Year-round core of four: festival director, production manager, partnerships/finance lead, marketing manager. They produce Wildhern — a 15,000-capacity, three-day music and arts festival — plus two smaller events. For six weeks each summer the org chart explodes: 40 contracted crew chiefs and suppliers, 80 stewards via an agency, 250 volunteers, and a site village of subcontractors.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| Collaboration posture | ◆ Pulse-extreme: 90% of the people who deliver the festival are not in the organisation 46 weeks a year. Every July, a 400-person operation must be assembled, briefed, run and dissolved — mostly from scratch, partly from last year's returners, entirely against an immovable weekend. |
| Tooling | Core: Google Workspace + a festival-ops platform (scheduling, accreditation) bought after the Year of the Spreadsheet Disaster. Seasonal: WhatsApp groups breeding like midges, radio channels on site, printed site maps with laminated authority. |
| Who carries coordination cost | The production manager, titanically, May–July; the core four absorb everything else year-round. The event itself runs on crew chiefs' imported professionalism (the Gorse Films role-grammar, at hundredfold scale). |
| Buying behaviour | Annually rhythmic: tools bought in spring, stress-tested in July, churned in September. Cash flow is a sine wave (ticket revenue vs. supplier deposits); anything reducing show-stopping risk survives the autumn cull. |
| What failure costs | The licence: one bad SAG (Safety Advisory Group) review threatens next year's event — existential, annual. On site: a crowd-safety failure is the unthinkable tail; a supplier no-show on build week is a visible scramble; a volunteer no-show rate of 30% is Saturday's staffing crisis. Financially: one rained-out year without insurance discipline ends a decade of work. |

## C — Constellation

**Definition:** ◆ A tiny permanent core that annually constructs, operates and dissolves a complete temporary organisation — supplier constellations, volunteer armies, statutory overseers and 15,000 guests — against a fixed weekend, retaining almost nothing structurally from pulse to pulse except what the core four remember.

**Cast:**
- **Bea Hernshaw, 43** — founder & festival director; the vision and the licence-holder (Frome)
- **Mack Stennett, 39** — production manager; builder of the temporary city (Bristol)
- **Gail Hodder, 35** — seasonal event-ops manager (May–August contract, fourth year returning); volunteers, stewards, accreditation
- **Vern Olusoga, 47** — staging & structures contractor; build week's load-bearing supplier (Cardiff)
- **Plus:** the partnerships/finance lead, the marketing manager, 40 crew chiefs and suppliers (sound, light, power, water, sanitation, security, medical, bars), 250 volunteers, the council's SAG, emergency services, headline acts' tour managers, sponsors

**External immovable nodes:**
- **The licence and the SAG** — council, police, fire, ambulance: the festival exists at their annual pleasure; the Event Safety Plan is the constitution they audit
- **The weekend itself** — the date is sold; 15,000 tickets are a contract with the public
- **The weather** — the largest financial actor in the constellation, uninsurable above certain premiums
- **Headline acts' contracts and riders** — immovable fees, immovable stage times, occasionally immovable demands
- **The land** — one farmer's goodwill, renewed annually over kitchen tea

**Topology:** ◆ A breathing organisation: a four-node core that grows concentric rings each spring — contractors (professional, role-grammared), seasonal staff (returning, semi-known), volunteers (mass, unknown, 30% evaporation rate) — then sheds them all by September. On site, the topology goes radial-hierarchical overnight: radios, sectors, control room — a command structure that exists for 120 hours a year.

**Lifecycle (annual, the purest in the library):**
- *The long off-season (Sep–Jan):* debrief (if energy survives), licence renewal groundwork, headliner negotiation, cash-flow trough.
- *Assembly (Feb–May):* suppliers contracted (the same 30 names, renegotiated), the ESP rewritten, ticket sales watched like weather.
- *Inflation (May–Jun):* seasonal staff return (or don't — Gail's availability is an annual coin-flip the whole machine pretends isn't), volunteers recruited at scale, briefing documents bred.
- *Build week (the first crunch):* the temporary city erected; every supplier's punctuality compounds; Mack sleeps in a cabin.
- *The show (120 hours):* the organisation at full 400-person extension; control room rhythm; every coordination failure visible to 15,000 people in real time, every success invisible.
- *The breakdown & dissolution (one week):* the city vanishes; invoices and lost-property remain; the 400 evaporate mid-sentence.
- *Trust curve:* supplier trust is multi-year and load-bearing (Vern gets paid even in the bad year — that's why he answers in build week); volunteer trust is built in a 40-minute briefing and a t-shirt; the SAG's trust is documentary and cumulative — this year's paperwork buys next year's benefit of the doubt.

**Cadence:** Annual sine wave; weekly core meetings year-round; daily all-hands in build week; radio-discipline by hour during the show; silence by September.

**Boundary objects:** The Event Safety Plan (the licence's body — risk assessments as the central boundary object, the universe doc's flag confirmed), the site plan (laminated law), the build schedule, the crew briefing pack (read by 40%), the accreditation matrix (who may be where — access control as physical fact), the running order, the radio log, the debrief doc (the pulse's only institutional memory, written exhausted).

**Where articulation work concentrates:** On Mack from February (supplier sequencing, site logistics) crescendoing to total in build week; on Gail at inflation (250 volunteers × shifts × competence-unknowns); on Bea across the licence, the acts and the money. The show itself runs on the imported articulation of crew chiefs — bought, not built.

**Where who-knows-what lives:** Bea holds the vision, the SAG relationship and the farmer's tea-based goodwill. Mack holds the site in his head — where the cables ran *last* year, which gate floods. Gail holds the volunteer-quality folklore (who actually shows, who hides). Vern holds structural truth. The debrief docs hold fragments, decaying. ◆ The pulse's defining pathology: *the organisation's memory dissolves with the organisation* — every year rebuilds knowledge the previous year already paid for.

**Characteristic failure modes** *(tagged)*:
1. Pulse amnesia: the same problems re-solved annually because dissolution destroyed the learning — **eliminate**; the family's signature waste
2. Onboarding at scale: 250 volunteers and 80 stewards briefed by document-nobody-reads — **eliminate** the format failure (zero-onboarding design, the Gorse rule at battalion scale)
3. The Gail coin-flip: seasonal-key-staff availability discovered late — **eliminate** the lateness; **contain** the seasonality (it's the model)
4. Build-week cascade: one supplier's slipped day re-sequencing forty others — **eliminate** the invisibility
5. Volunteer evaporation (30% no-show) staffing Saturday by panic — **contain** the human reality; **eliminate** the surprise (predictive over-recruitment is folklore that should be maths)
6. The SAG's paperwork burden — **contain**: the scrutiny is legitimate and protective; the assembly of evidence is the automatable part
7. The control room's "stop the stage" call and the safety officer's veto — **preserve**, absolutely: on-site safety authority is the constellation's deepest preserve-class friction; nothing may ever smooth it
8. Core-four burnout in the August trough — **contain**: structural to the pulse; the product reduces the rebuild tax that deepens it

**Conflicting interests:**
- The vision wants magic; the ESP wants control; the budget wants both cheaper
- Suppliers want early lock-in and deposits; cash flow wants late commitment
- The council wants documentation; the site wants improvisation under weather
- Volunteers want the fun shifts; the rota wants Saturday 2am gate cover

*Contain-class: the safety/spectacle/budget triangle is festival production itself. The AI serves the paperwork, the sequencing and the memory — never the judgement calls in the control room.*

**Substitution stress-test:** Swap a volunteer: invisible (the design absorbs it ×250). Swap a crew chief day-of: the role grammar holds (the Gorse finding, confirmed at scale). Swap *Vern* in spring: structural rebid, survivable; swap him in *build week*: show-threatening — supplier substitutability collapses as the date approaches, a time-decaying function unique to pulse constellations. Swap *Gail* in March: a season of volunteer-folklore gone; the no-show maths resets to guesswork. Swap *Mack* at any point after February: the site-in-his-head is the single greatest concentration risk in the library's whole production family. Swap *Bea*: the licence, the farmer and the headline agents all relate to *her* — the festival is legally an event but socially a person.

**Afterlife:** ◆ The pulse's afterlife is next year's pulse: supplier edges, SAG goodwill, volunteer returners and the debrief's half-remembered lessons are the only bridge across the trough. Done well, each year compounds (returner rates rise, the SAG relaxes, suppliers prioritise); done badly, each year starts from scratch with worse credit. The site itself remembers (ruts, drainage, the farmer's patience). One cancelled year tests whether the constellation was an organisation or just a habit.

**What this constellation needs from the AI:** Be the memory the pulse can't keep: every decision, supplier performance note, site quirk and debrief lesson banked across the trough and surfaced at next year's matching moment ("last year this gate flooded; the fix was…"). Assemble the ESP and SAG evidence from the living record. Run inflation as a pipeline: volunteer onboarding that works on a phone in four minutes, no-show maths from history, accreditation that builds itself. Sequence build week visibly across forty suppliers with slippage ripples computed live. Brief by role, by phone, just-in-time — never by 60-page PDF. And go quiet during the show: 120 hours of radio discipline is human terrain; the product's job was the eleven months before.

### Bea Hernshaw, 43 — Founder & Festival Director *(The Licence and the Dream)*
**Frome.** Started Wildhern in a field with 800 people and a generator; now answerable to a council, a bank and 15,000 ticket-holders. Carries the festival's soul and its insurance schedule in the same head. **Attitude to AI:** open wherever it buys back February evenings; immovable that the festival must never feel produced-by-machine. **Winning her looks like:** the SAG pack assembles itself from a record that's already true, the off-season stops being archaeology, and the magic stays handmade.

### Mack Stennett, 39 — Production Manager *(The City in His Head)*
**Bristol.** Fifteen festival seasons; can read a field's drainage like Dave Hartley reads stock. The build schedule is his cathedral. **Attitude to AI:** show-me pragmatism — he's seen event tech die in mud before. **Winning him looks like:** the site knowledge gets banked (credited, his), slippage ripples compute themselves, and build week's 5am surprises drop by half.

### Gail Hodder, 35 — Seasonal Ops Manager *(The Returning Pulse)*
**Glastonbury (the town).** Works five events a summer, Wildhern's her favourite; every spring she and Bea perform the availability dance. Holds the volunteer folklore: who shows, who shines, who hides behind the bins. **Attitude to AI:** practical — 250 humans on spreadsheets is her whole June. **Winning her looks like:** onboarding runs itself on phones, the no-show maths is maths, and her folklore survives the years she doesn't come back.

### Vern Olusoga, 47 — Staging Contractor *(The Load-Bearing Supplier)*
**Cardiff.** Stages for thirty events a summer; his crews are booked eighteen months out. Answers Wildhern's calls in build week because Hern & Salt pays on time and plans straight. **Attitude to AI:** indifferent; his rigging calcs are his own. **Winning him looks like:** the build schedule he's handed is real, changes reach him before his trucks roll, and the paperwork (RAMS, sign-offs) flows without a portal he'll never log into.

**Product needs from the edge map:**
- ◆ The pulse family's core demand: **memory across dissolution**. The product is the bridge over the trough — the only structure that persists from pulse to pulse besides four exhausted humans. Bank everything; resurface it at the matching moment next year.
- Onboarding-at-scale sets the library's hardest zero-training bar: 250 volunteers, four minutes, a phone, no second chance. Solve it here and every conscripted-spoke problem in the library is solved beneath it.
- The SAG edge confirms the Howarth/Pete/Niamh pattern at existential stakes: the gatekeeper can't be a user, so the product's output — a self-assembling, evidence-linked safety case — *is* the relationship strategy.
